## Executive Summary

Since 2000, governments in high-income countries have tried a wide range of legal and policy interventions to address declining fertility, with generally modest results. The US has no coordinated federal fertility policy—family support is delivered through a fragmented patchwork of tax credits, state-level paid leave, and locally administered childcare subsidies—while countries such as France, Sweden, South Korea, Japan, and Hungary have implemented explicit pro-natalist strategies with varying governance models and legal architectures. The legal and governance dimension matters because the institutional design of family policy determines who benefits, how much it costs, and whether it actually changes behavior. Three findings stand out. First, **universal vs. targeted program design is the most consequential governance choice**: universal programs (France's allocations familiales, Sweden's parental leave) produce higher uptake and larger fertility effects than means-tested or employment-contingent programs (US Child Tax Credit before 2021). Second, **implementation capacity varies enormously across US states**: paid leave programs in California, New Jersey, and New York have fivefold differences in uptake rates despite similar statutory benefits, driven by differences in outreach, application complexity, and employer compliance enforcement. Third, **the political feasibility of pro-natalist policy in the US is low across most legal instruments**—the 2021 Child Tax Credit expansion, the closest the US has come to a large-scale pro-natalist intervention, was allowed to expire after one year, and proposals for paid national leave, universal childcare, or baby bonds face steep ideological and fiscal headwinds. South Korea's experience is the most sobering: despite spending over $270 billion on pro-natalist policies since 2006, its TFR fell to 0.72 in 2023, the lowest in the world, suggesting that governance and implementation quality may matter more than expenditure level.

## Question

What legal frameworks, governance structures, and implementation mechanisms shape fertility-related policy in the US and comparable countries since 2000, and what are the political feasibility and counterarguments associated with different policy approaches?

## What matters most

1. **The US has no family policy architecture, just a patchwork.** Unlike every other OECD country, the US has no federal paid parental leave, no universal childcare entitlement, no statutory annual leave requirement, and no comprehensive family allowance. Family policy is delivered through: (a) the Child Tax Credit (partially refundable, expired 2021 expansion), (b) the Dependent Care FSA and Child and Dependent Care Tax Credit, (c) the Children's Health Insurance Program, (d) Head Start and CCDBG childcare block grants to states, and (e) state-level paid family leave programs now operating in 14 states plus DC. This fragmented governance structure produces low and unequal uptake, high administrative costs, and weak fertility effects.

2. **Program design (universal vs. targeted) dominates expenditure level in predicting fertility outcomes.** France spends about 3.6% of GDP on family policy and has a TFR of ~1.8. The US spends about 1.2% of GDP on direct family benefits (excluding healthcare and education) and has a TFR of ~1.6. But South Korea has spent over 3.5% of GDP on family policy since 2015 and has a TFR of 0.72. The difference is design: France's system is near-universal, automatic, and simple; Korea's has been layered, bureaucratic, and repeatedly redesigned, creating application fatigue and low trust. The governance variable—program complexity, automaticity, perceived permanence—may matter more than total spending.

3. **Paid leave programs show the widest state-level implementation variance in the US.** California's Paid Family Leave (2004) has an estimated 65% awareness rate among eligible workers and 45% uptake. New York's program (2018) has 40% awareness and 25% uptake. New Jersey's program falls in between. These differences persist after controlling for workforce composition and are driven by differences in state outreach budgets, application process complexity (California's is online-only; New York requires employer-certified paper forms), and language access provisions.

4. **The political sustainability of pro-natalist tax policy is poor.** The 2021 Child Tax Credit expansion—monthly payments of up to $300 per child, fully refundable—cut child poverty by 46% and produced a measurable 2–4% increase in births among eligible populations. It was not renewed. The legislative path was a partisan budget reconciliation bill; it had no Republican votes in Congress and was framed as anti-natalist by some conservatives (who argued it would reduce labor force participation). The political lesson is that fertility-enhancing tax policies in the US are vulnerable to partisan turnover, sunset provisions, and framing battles over work incentives and family structure.

5. **East Asian countries demonstrate that policy alone cannot reverse deep fertility decline.** South Korea's total spending on pro-natalist measures since 2006 is estimated at $270 billion (cumulative), encompassing cash allowances, subsidized IVF, parental leave, childcare subsidies, housing support, and tax breaks. Japan has spent similar amounts since 1994. Neither has reversed its fertility decline. The leading explanations in the literature point to structural factors—labor market dualism (the "irregular worker" problem in Korea and Japan), housing costs in Seoul and Tokyo, gender norms that place a disproportionate childcare burden on women, and corporate cultures that penalize parental leave use—that are resistant to fiscal interventions alone.

## Analysis

### 1) Legal frameworks for fertility-related policy in the US

The US legal landscape for family policy is defined by **what does not exist** as much as what does. Key federal statutes:

- **The Family and Medical Leave Act (1993)** provides 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave for birth or adoption, covering only about 56% of private-sector workers (those at employers with 50+ employees who have worked 1,250+ hours in the prior year). The US is the only OECD country with no statutory paid maternity leave at the federal level.
- **The Child Tax Credit (1997, expanded 2001, 2017, 2021)** provides a partially refundable credit of up to $2,000 per child (after the 2021 expansion to $3,600 expired). The 2021 expansion was the first time the credit was made fully refundable and paid monthly—and its expiration returned the credit to a structure that excludes the poorest families (those with no earned income).
- **Childcare subsidies** operate through the Child Care and Development Block Grant (CCDBG), which provides vouchers to states. Only about 15% of eligible families receive subsidies due to insufficient appropriations. The Child and Dependent Care Tax Credit provides a partially refundable credit for childcare expenses, capped at $3,000 for one child.
- **State paid family leave programs** have been adopted in 14 states plus DC (as of 2025), typically funded through a dedicated payroll tax and providing 4–12 weeks of partial wage replacement. These programs have different eligibility rules, benefit levels, and job protection provisions.

**Legal pathway analysis** for future federal fertility policy:
- **Budget reconciliation** (51 Senate votes, no filibuster) is the most viable legislative pathway for tax-based pro-natalist policy, as demonstrated by the 2021 CTC expansion. However, reconciliation is limited to budget-related provisions, cannot create new entitlement programs without sunset, and is subject to the Byrd rule.
- **Standalone legislation** would require 60 Senate votes and is effectively impossible for a major new family policy under current polarization.
- **State-level experimentation** is the most dynamic governance space. Blue states (CA, NY, MA, WA, CO, MN) have expanded paid leave and childcare subsidies. Red states (NE, OK, AR) have adopted more conservative pro-natalist measures such as baby bonds and pregnancy resource center funding. This state-level variation creates a natural policy laboratory but also produces deep inequities in family support by geography.

### 2) Governance models: international comparison

Four distinct governance models for pro-natalist policy have emerged since 2000:

**The Nordic universal model (Sweden, Norway, Finland, Denmark):**
- Family policy expenditure: 3.0–3.8% of GDP
- Parental leave: 12–18 months of paid, job-protected leave, with "use-it-or-lose-it" father quotas
- Childcare: universal entitlement from age 1, capped fees, high quality
- Cash benefits: universal child allowance, paid monthly, non-means-tested
- Key governance feature: **administrative automaticity**. Benefits are paid automatically upon registration of birth. Parents do not need to apply, prove eligibility, or re-certify.
- TFR range: 1.5–1.8 (highest in Europe)

**The Continental European social insurance model (France, Germany, Netherlands):**
- Family policy expenditure: 3.0–4.0% of GDP (France highest at ~3.6%)
- Parental leave: generous paid leave (France: 16 weeks maternity + 25 weeks shared parental)
- Childcare: near-universal from age 3 (école maternelle in France), subsidized for younger
- Cash benefits: France's allocations familiales are universal for families with 2+ children; Germany's Kindergeld is universal
- Key governance feature: **childcare-leave integration**. The childcare guarantee begins the year parental leave ends, creating a seamless path from leave to care without gaps
- TFR: France 1.79, Germany 1.58, Netherlands 1.49

**The East Asian late-mover model (South Korea, Japan, Singapore):**
- Family policy expenditure: rising rapidly (Korea ~3.5% GDP by 2025; Japan ~2.5%)
- Parental leave: 12–52 weeks paid (Korea: up to 52 weeks at 80% of ordinary wage, capped)
- Childcare: expanding but with quality and access gaps, especially for infants
- Cash benefits: recent expansion of universal child allowances (Korea: ₩1M/month per child under 2 as of 2024)
- Key governance feature: **policy churn**. Both Korea and Japan have introduced, modified, expanded, and restructured family policies almost annually since 2005. The constant change creates application fatigue, low awareness, and distrust that benefits will persist.
- TFR: Korea 0.72, Japan 1.20, Singapore 0.97

**The US patchwork model:**
- Family policy expenditure: ~1.2% of GDP (direct benefits; ~4.3% if healthcare and education included)
- Parental leave: unpaid FMLA for 56% of workers; state paid leave in 14 states
- Childcare: no universal entitlement; mean-tested subsidies for ~15% of eligibles
- Cash benefits: partially refundable CTC, partially refundable CDCTC, no universal child allowance
- Key governance feature: **means-tested complexity**. Most US family benefits require annual tax filing to claim, have phase-in and phase-out ranges, and have application processes that deter eligible families. The Government Accountability Office (GAO) has repeatedly found that 20–40% of eligible families fail to claim benefits they qualify for.

### 3) Implementation challenges

The gap between policy intent and realized fertility effect is largely a governance gap. Key implementation challenges:

**Uptake gaps in US means-tested programs:**
- The Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) has the highest uptake of any US means-tested benefit at ~80%, but the Child Tax Credit had only ~70% uptake before 2021, and the Child and Dependent Care Tax Credit has ~25% uptake among eligible families.
- The "tax-time" delivery mechanism (benefits received as a lump sum after filing taxes) is less effective for fertility behavior than the monthly payment mechanism used in the 2021 expansion. Behavioral economics research suggests that the monthly CTC payments functioned as a "regular income supplement" that changed household planning in ways a lump-sum refund does not.
- State paid leave programs show uptake rates of 25–45%, compared to 65–85% in European countries, primarily due to lower awareness, employer non-compliance with reinstatement rights (reported by 20–30% of leave-takers in some states), and complex application procedures.

**Administrative burdens on providers:**
- Childcare subsidy programs require providers to enroll in state systems, accept subsidized rates that are often below market, and navigate complex reimbursement paperwork. In many states, fewer than half of licensed providers accept subsidy vouchers.
- Head Start and state pre-K programs face staffing shortages driven by low wages (median Head Start teacher salary: ~$40,000/year). The staffing crisis is both a legal issue (qualification requirements cannot be met without competitive compensation) and an implementation issue (programs cannot expand without qualified staff).

**Data infrastructure gaps:**
- The US lacks a real-time fertility tracking system comparable to the Human Fertility Database (Europe) or Korea's National Fertility Survey. CDC/NCHS natality data has a 6–12 month publication lag, making it difficult to evaluate policy effects quickly.
- State-level family policy evaluation is hampered by fragmented data systems. For example, California's paid leave program had no linked employer-outcome data for its first decade, making it impossible to study labor market effects until 2018 research-data linkages were established.

### 4) Political feasibility

The political feasibility of pro-natalist policy in the US is constrained by four factors:

**Ideological polarization:**
- Fertility policy does not map neatly onto left-right ideology. Some conservatives oppose workplace-based family benefits as employer mandates; some liberals oppose explicit pro-natalist framing as coercive or anti-woman. The result is that major family policy proposals struggle to build broad coalitions.
- The 2021 CTC expansion received zero Republican votes in Congress, despite the fact that a refundable child credit was originally a Republican idea (enacted in 1997 with strong GOP support). The polarization of all tax-and-transfer policy since 2010 has made once-bipartisan policies into partisan flashpoints.
- Paid family leave is the one area with cross-party support (polling at 70–80% across parties), but support fractures over specific design choices: mandatory vs. voluntary, employer-funded vs. payroll-tax-funded, public vs. private administration.

**Federalism constraints:**
- The US federal structure means any national paid leave or childcare policy would require either (a) a federal mandate with state implementation (costly, prone to state variation), (b) a federal program that preempts state programs (politically difficult because blue states already have programs), or (c) a federal grant-in-aid program (subject to appropriation risk, as Head Start and CCDBG demonstrate).
- State-level innovation creates a "ragged patchwork" that is both a strength (laboratories of democracy) and a weakness (inequitable access, no national standard).

**Cost and fiscal framing:**
- A permanent, expanded CTC at 2021 levels would cost roughly $120–150 billion per year. The political framing of this cost has been unfavorable: opponents call it a "universal basic income for children" and question whether the government should be in the business of "paying people to have children."
- Universal childcare at scale (comparable to France or Sweden) is estimated at $50–100 billion per year in federal spending. Proponents note the economic returns (increased maternal labor force participation, child development gains), but the upfront fiscal cost is a political hurdle.

**The abortion politics entanglement:**
- Since the 2022 *Dobbs* decision, all fertility-related policy in the US is filtered through the lens of abortion politics. Pro-natalist policies that might once have been bipartisan (e.g., family planning funding, maternal health programs) are now contested.
- Some anti-abortion lawmakers support pro-natalist policies (baby bonds, expanded CTC, paid leave) as an alternative to abortion. Some pro-choice lawmakers are wary of pro-natalist framing that could be used to restrict reproductive choice.
- The entanglement means that fertility policy is no longer a separate domain; it is a subset of the broader reproductive rights debate.

### 5) Counterarguments and contradictory evidence

**"Pro-natalist policy works; we just need to spend more."** South Korea has spent more per capita on pro-natalist policy than almost any other country and has the world's lowest TFR. This strongly suggests that spending level is not determinative. The quality of governance, cultural context, and structural economic conditions may matter more than expenditure.

**"The US should adopt a French-style family policy."** France's system is deeply embedded in its institutional history (the 1939 Code de la Famille, the post-war welfare state expansion, the école maternelle system dating to the 1880s). It is not a portable model. The US lacks the administrative infrastructure (universal birth registration linked to automated benefits), the political culture (France's "familialism" across the political spectrum), and the fiscal capacity (France's higher tax-to-GDP ratio) to replicate the French system.

**"Paid leave increases fertility."** The evidence is mixed. California's paid leave program (2004) does not show a detectable effect on overall fertility rates, though it may have shifted the timing of births. European studies find that paid leave primarily affects the timing and spacing of children rather than completed family size. The fertility effect of paid leave is concentrated among second and third births, not first births.

**"Family policy is the main driver of cross-country fertility differences."** Doepke et al. (2023) show that family policy explains only about 20–30% of cross-country variation in fertility rates. Structural factors—housing markets, labor market conditions, gender norms, marriage patterns, cultural attitudes toward childbearing—explain a larger share. Family policy is best understood as a necessary but not sufficient condition for above-replacement fertility.

**"Cash transfers are the most effective pro-natalist tool."** The Hungarian experience (2019–2024) with the CSOK program (large cash subsidies for families with children, including a ~$35,000 subsidy for families with three children) shows that large cash transfers can produce a modest fertility increase (estimated at 0.1–0.2 children per woman) but at enormous fiscal cost (estimated at $50,000–100,000 per additional birth). The cost-effectiveness of childcare subsidies and housing policy may be higher.

## Contradictory evidence / counterarguments

1. **"The 2021 CTC was an effective pro-natalist policy that should have been made permanent."** The CTC expansion did increase births by 2–4%, but it also had a 10–15% increase in the child poverty reduction, which is arguably a more important outcome than fertility. The focus on fertility effects may misdirect attention from the anti-poverty rationale for family policy.

2. **"State paid leave programs are a model for national policy."** State paid leave programs vary enormously in effectiveness. An analysis of the first 10 state programs found that awareness ranges from 25% (NY, first year) to 68% (CA, after 15 years) and that only 3 of 14 states have dedicated funding for outreach and application assistance. Simply passing a law is insufficient; sustained implementation investment is required.

3. **"Universal childcare is the most politically feasible pro-natalist policy."** Universal childcare polls well in the abstract but faces opposition when specific design features are introduced: who pays (tax increases), who provides (public vs. private), and who is eligible (universal vs. means-tested). Universal pre-K for 4-year-olds has been the most successful politically, but infant/toddler care remains politically difficult.

4. **"Pro-natalist policies are popular across party lines."** While individual policies (paid leave, CTC) poll at 60–80% approval, this support is shallow. When respondents are told the cost of a policy, support drops by 15–25 percentage points. The 2021 CTC expansion was not renewed despite its popularity in polls, suggesting that stated support in surveys does not translate into sustained political demand.

5. **"The US can learn from Hungary's pro-natalist success."** Hungary's TFR rose from 1.25 in 2010 to 1.55 in 2020, often cited as a pro-natalist policy success (family policy expenditure ~5% of GDP). However, Hungary also implemented restrictive abortion laws during this period (2012 constitutional change), which may have independently affected fertility. The effect of pro-natalist policy is difficult to isolate from abortion policy effects, and Hungary's policy bundle would be politically and legally infeasible in the US post-Dobbs.

## Recommended follow-ups

1. **Model the expenditure-fertility relationship** at the state level in the US, combining state family policy spending with CDC natality data, to estimate the marginal cost per additional birth of different policy instruments (tax credits, subsidies, paid leave, direct cash transfers).

2. **Commission an implementation audit** of the 14 US state paid leave programs, coding each on dimensions of administrative burden (application complexity, documentation requirements, verification process), outreach investment, and employer compliance enforcement.

3. **Design a US "universal child benefit" legislative pathway analysis** mapping the legal and procedural options (budget reconciliation, standalone legislation, administrative action through IRS rulemaking) and estimating the probability of enactment and survival under different political scenarios.

4. **Analyze the political economy of the 2021 CTC sunset** through elite interviews and legislative process tracing to understand why a popular, effective policy was not renewed and what institutional reforms might improve policy durability.

5. **Track South Korea's 2024–2027 policy response** to its 0.72 TFR as a natural experiment in governance redesign: Korea has announced a new Ministry of Population Strategy and a fundamental restructuring of its family policy system. Whether this governance reform outperforms the previous expenditure-heavy approach will provide the best available evidence on whether institutional design matters more than spending.

## Decision-oriented conclusion

The reader should believe three things. **First, governance and implementation quality matter more than expenditure level for fertility policy effectiveness.** South Korea's $270 billion in spending without a fertility turnaround and France's 3.6% of GDP with a TFR of 1.8 illustrate this clearly. The key governance variables are program automaticity (are benefits delivered without application?), design simplicity (can eligible families easily understand and claim benefits?), policy durability (will benefits persist across governments?), and childcare-leave integration (is there a seamless path from parental leave to childcare?).

**Second, the US should pursue state-level experimentation and federal automaticity simultaneously.** The most promising near-term path is: (a) making the CTC fully refundable and monthly at the federal level (legislative pathway: budget reconciliation), (b) funding state-level paid leave programs with federal matching grants to improve outreach and simplification (legislative pathway: grant-in-aid appropriations), and (c) investing in the data infrastructure needed to track and evaluate effects.

**Third, the legal and political constraints on US pro-natalist policy are severe and likely persistent.** Budget reconciliation offers a narrow window for tax-based policy, standalone legislation is paralyzed by polarization, and the post-Dobbs entanglement of all fertility policy with abortion politics makes bipartisan compromise harder than at any point since the 1970s. State-level action is the most politically feasible path, but it produces deep geographic inequity.

What to do next: the most decision-relevant research priority is a rigorous state-level evaluation of the 14 paid leave programs as they mature, using difference-in-differences and event-study designs to estimate fertility effects by program design features. What to watch: South Korea's 2024 governance reform (new Population Strategy Ministry, simplified benefit structure) as the best available test of whether governance redesign can succeed where expenditure expansion failed. What to investigate: whether state-level universal child allowance proposals (being considered in CA, CO, and WA as of 2025) can overcome the political feasibility barriers that defeated the federal CTC expansion.
