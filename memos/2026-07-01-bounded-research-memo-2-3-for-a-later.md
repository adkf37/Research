## Executive Summary

Governments seeking to influence fertility rates through policy face a landscape shaped by legal constraints, federalism, political polarization, and limited evidence of policy effectiveness. The US has no explicit national fertility policy; instead, fertility is affected indirectly through the tax code (Child Tax Credit), social insurance (paid leave, childcare subsidies at state level), and reproductive rights law (abortion access, contraception mandates). The legal framework is fragmented: family policy is primarily a state responsibility under US federalism, while tax-based interventions are federal. Internationally, countries with explicit pro-natalist policies (Hungary, South Korea, France) show that generous transfers can raise fertility by 0.1-0.3 children per woman but have not reversed below-replacement trends. Political feasibility in the US is sharply divided: Republicans favor tax-based approaches (expanded CTC, marriage penalties in tax code), while Democrats favor social-spending approaches (paid leave, subsidized childcare, universal pre-K). Implementation faces three binding constraints: (1) the US lacks administrative infrastructure for universal family allowances, (2) federalism creates wide variation in state-level support, and (3) budget costs are large—a permanent expanded CTC costs roughly $100-200 billion annually. The strongest counterargument is that government policy cannot address the root causes of fertility decline: cultural shifts toward individualism, delayed partnership formation, and the fundamental incompatibility of career and childrearing in high-cost, high-pressure economies.

## Question

What legal, governance, implementation, and political feasibility constraints shape fertility-related policy in the US and comparable countries since 2000, and what are the strongest counterarguments to government intervention in fertility?

## What matters most

1. **No US fertility policy exists—only indirect levers.** The US has no explicit pro-natalist policy. Fertility is affected through the tax code (CTC, dependent exemptions, marriage penalties), labor law (FMLA unpaid leave, state paid leave programs), health policy (Medicaid coverage of maternity care, contraception mandates, abortion access), and housing/education policy (student loans, zoning). This fragmented approach is a feature of US federalism, not a bug.

2. **International evidence on pro-natalist policy is sobering.** France's family allowance system (the oldest in Europe, established 1932) sustains a TFR of ~1.8, the highest in the EU outside Scandinavia. Hungary's post-2015 pro-natalist package (family housing subsidies, loan forgiveness for three-child families, income tax exemptions for mothers of four) initially raised fertility from 1.3 to 1.6 but has since plateaued. South Korea's $270 billion in pro-natalist spending over 15 years failed to prevent the TFR from falling from 1.3 to 0.72. Policy effects are real but marginal relative to the structural decline.

3. **Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health (2022) reshaped the US fertility landscape legally.** The removal of federal constitutional protection for abortion transferred fertility regulation to the states. As of 2026, 14 states have near-total abortion bans, 7 have gestational limits, and the remainder protect access. Early evidence suggests abortion bans have increased birth rates in restricted states by 2-5%, concentrated among women under 25, but have also increased maternal mortality and infant mortality in those states. This is the largest legal change affecting US fertility since 2000.

4. **Political feasibility of pro-natalist policy is low and polarized.** US voters do not rank "raising the birth rate" as a top priority. Polling shows Democrats prioritize making childbearing affordable through social investment, while Republicans prioritize reducing abortion and supporting "traditional family" tax policies. A unified pro-natalist coalition does not exist.

5. **Implementation capacity varies enormously.** France administers family allowances through the CAF (Caisse d'Allocations Familiales), a dedicated agency with 50 years of institutional experience. The US would need to build comparable administrative infrastructure for means-tested universal child benefits, currently overseen by the IRS for tax credits and HHS for social programs—neither optimized for family allowance delivery.

## Analysis

### 1) Legal framework: US fertility policy is a byproduct of other policy domains

The US Constitution does not mention family policy. Fertility-relevant law falls into four areas:

**Tax law:** The Child Tax Credit (created 1997, expanded 2001, 2017, temporarily expanded 2021) is the largest federal fertility-related transfer. The 2021 American Rescue Plan made it fully refundable and paid monthly, reaching 61 million children. It expired in December 2021 after a single year. The legal form (tax credit rather than direct spending) reflects the constitutional limitation that revenue bills originate in the House and avoids the 60-vote Senate threshold for direct spending via reconciliation. H.R. 7024 (Tax Relief for American Families and Workers Act, 118th Congress) would have restored partial CTC expansion but failed in the Senate.

**Labor law:** The Family and Medical Leave Act (1993) provides 12 weeks of unpaid leave—the only federal family leave in US law. States have filled the gap: 14 states and DC now have paid family leave programs, with benefits ranging from 60-100% of wages for 4-16 weeks. These programs are constitutionally permissible under state police power but create geographic variation in fertility support.

**Health law:** Medicaid covers 42% of US births. The Hyde Amendment (1976-present) prohibits federal funding for abortion except in cases of rape, incest, or life endangerment, creating differential access by state. The Dobbs decision (2022) overruled Roe v. Wade (1973) and Planned Parenthood v. Casey (1992), returning abortion regulation to states. Post-Dobbs, 14 states have enacted near-total bans, while 20 states and DC protect access. This legal fragmentation means fertility rates are now partially a function of state abortion law, with measurable effects on births, maternal mortality, and interstate travel for care.

**Housing and education law:** Zoning regulations are primarily local, but their effect on fertility—through housing costs—is larger than any federal program. Federal student loan policy (HEA of 1965, income-driven repayment regulations) affects fertility timing and quantum as discussed in memo 1/3. Neither domain is administered with fertility effects in mind.

### 2) Governance: federalism creates 50 fertility policies

The US federal system means fertility-relevant policy is made at three levels:

**Federal:** Tax credits, Medicaid, student loans, immigration policy (which affects population growth more than fertility).

**State:** Paid leave, Medicaid expansion (39 states), abortion access, child welfare, public university tuition, family court (divorce, child support, custody).

**Local:** Zoning (controlling housing costs and availability), school quality, public transit, parks and recreation.

This multi-level governance creates coordination problems: no single level of government can implement a coherent fertility strategy. A state that expands paid leave may see its effect diluted if high housing costs (locally determined) push young families elsewhere. A federal tax credit may be absorbed by higher housing costs in supply-constrained markets.

International comparison: France's unitary system allows national family policy implemented through regional CAF offices. South Korea's centralized system similarly allows national coordination. The US would require intergovernmental compacts or federal preemption to achieve comparable coherence.

### 3) Implementation: administrative capacity is a binding constraint

The 2021 CTC experience illustrates implementation challenges. The IRS:
- Had no prior experience making monthly payments
- Relied on prior-year tax return data (2019 or 2020), excluding many non-filers
- Reached 61 million of 65 million eligible children (93% coverage), but non-filers and homeless families were largely missed
- Could not adjust payments mid-year when family circumstances changed

By contrast, France's CAF system processes 12 million family allowance files through dedicated local offices that can adjust payments based on births, income changes, and family composition changes reported by beneficiaries.

**Cost constraint:** The permanent expanded CTC (at 2021 levels) would cost approximately $200 billion/year—roughly 0.7% of GDP. This is comparable to the cost of French family policy (about 3.5% of GDP including all programs) but would require either new revenue or reprioritized spending. The US already spends over $1 trillion/year on tax expenditures for children (CTC, child and dependent care credit, dependent exemption, head of household filing status), but these are poorly targeted and embedded in the tax code rather than structured as a coherent family policy.

### 4) Political feasibility: the absence of a pro-natalist coalition

US politics do not map neatly onto pro- vs. anti-natalist positions:

**Republican position:** Support tax-based family support (expanded CTC, marriage penalties reduction), oppose social-spending approaches (universal pre-K, subsidized childcare as "government overreach"), support abortion restrictions that increase births. The GOP has not made raising fertility an explicit goal.

**Democratic position:** Support social-spending approaches (paid leave, universal pre-K, subsidized childcare, expanded CTC as permanent), oppose tying policy to fertility goals (viewed as coercive or population-control adjacent), support abortion access.

**Neither party** explicitly advocates "raising the birth rate" as a national priority. When framing is used (e.g., Hungary's "demographic crisis" language or South Korea's "population emergency"), it is typically from conservative governments.

**Public opinion:** Pew Research (2024) found only 24% of Americans say the declining birth rate is a major problem, while 42% say it is not a problem. Younger adults (18-29) are least concerned. The public is more concerned about the cost of raising children than about fertility levels per se. This suggests policy framed as "making childbearing affordable" has more support than policy framed as "raising the birth rate."

### 5) International case studies: what works, what doesn't

**France (TFR: 1.8):** The most durable pro-natalist system in the developed world. Family allowances date to 1932 (Code de la Famille). Key features: universal monthly allocations des familles (starting at second child, ~$180/month), means-tested complement familial, subsidized childcare (crèche from 3 months, école maternelle from age 3), generous paid leave (16 weeks maternity, 28 days paternity). Cost: ~3.5% of GDP. Effect: estimated 0.2-0.3 additional children per woman above the counterfactual. Political consensus: family policy enjoys cross-party support.

**Hungary (TFR: 1.3 -> 1.6 -> 1.5):** Orbán government's post-2015 "demographic action plan" included: CSOK housing subsidy (up to $35,000 for three-child families), student loan forgiveness for mothers of three, personal income tax exemption for mothers of four, expanded GYED (childcare allowance for grandparents). Initial rise in TFR from 1.3 to 1.6 by 2021, but has since declined to ~1.5. Cost: ~5% of GDP. Effect: initially positive but appears to be fading. Methodological concern: timing effects (women pulled forward births) may account for much of the initial increase.

**South Korea (TFR: 1.3 -> 0.72):** The most expensive fertility policy failure in history. South Korea spent $270 billion (2006-2021) on pro-natalist measures including: cash allowances (increased 7x between 2018-2024), parental leave (up to 18 months shared), subsidized childcare, public housing for newlyweds, free IVF treatment, and an entire ministry ministry (Ministry of Gender Equality and Family, renamed and restructured multiple times). Result: TFR fell from 1.3 to 0.72 (world's lowest). Why it failed: housing costs in Seoul (where half the population lives), education costs (the hagwon private tutoring system), long working hours, gender inequality (world's largest gender pay gap in OECD), and cultural attitudes that made marriage and children aspirational rather than expected.

**Lessons:** (1) Policy cannot overcome structural economic pressures (housing, education costs); (2) gender equality correlates with fertility within Europe but not globally; (3) timing effects inflate early results; (4) spending levels alone do not predict success.

### 6) Counterarguments and contradictory evidence

**Counterargument 1: Government cannot and should not influence fertility.**
- The ethical case against pro-natalist policy is that it limits reproductive autonomy, particularly for women. Feminist critics argue that "pro-natalist" always means "women bear the cost" through disrupted careers, unpaid care work, and health risks.
- Evidence: Pro-natalist policies in Hungary and Poland are explicitly linked to traditional gender roles. South Korea's failure is partly attributed to insufficient attention to gender equality.
- Rebuttal: Pro-natalist policy need not be anti-feminist—Sweden and Norway show that generous parental leave, shared between parents, can support both gender equality and fertility.

**Counterargument 2: Fertility decline is a feature, not a bug.**
- Lower fertility means smaller families, more resources per child, higher female labor force participation, and less environmental pressure. A TFR of 1.5-1.8 may be optimal in high-income countries.
- Evidence: Women with fewer children report higher life satisfaction in cross-country surveys. Children in smaller families receive more parental investment.
- Rebuttal: Below-replacement fertility creates long-run fiscal challenges (aging populations, declining workforce, strained pension systems) that are hard to address through immigration alone, especially in countries where immigration is politically contested.

**Counterargument 3: Housing and education reform would have larger fertility effects than family policy.**
- Memo 1/3 showed housing costs are the strongest leading indicator of fertility. Zoning reform, reduced student debt, and lower education costs would likely have larger fertility effects than direct child subsidies.
- Evidence: The CTC's fertility effect (2-4% increase at the intensive margin) is modest compared to the housing cost effect (10% rent increase reduces births by 2.5%).
- Rebuttal: Housing and education reform face equally difficult political obstacles and are not substitutes for family policy—both may be necessary.

**Counterargument 4: Existing evidence overstates policy effects.**
- Many studies of pro-natalist policy use short time horizons (3-5 years), capturing timing effects (pulled-forward births) rather than genuine increases in completed cohort fertility.
- Evidence: When Hungary's initial TFR rise is examined by cohort rather than period, the completed fertility increase is much smaller. South Korea's cash allowance increases were followed by further TFR declines, suggesting policy was overwhelmed by stronger countervailing forces.
- Rebuttal: Even modest policy effects (0.1-0.3 children per woman) are meaningful at population scale and accumulate over time. A permanent 0.2 increase from replacement level of 2.1 to 2.3 would add millions of births over a generation.

## Decision-oriented conclusion

The reader should believe four things. First, **the US has no fertility policy—only a fragmented set of programs (CTC, Medicaid, state paid leave, state abortion laws) that collectively affect fertility without coherence or coordination.** Second, **international evidence shows that pro-natalist policy can raise fertility by 0.1-0.3 children per woman, but cannot overcome structural economic and cultural forces** (housing costs, education costs, gender inequality, individualism). No country with below-replacement fertility has reversed to replacement through policy alone. Third, **the most consequential US legal change affecting fertility since 2000 is Dobbs v. Jackson,** which has created a natural experiment in state-level fertility variation that will produce evidence about the fertility effects of abortion access for years to come. Fourth, **political feasibility is the binding constraint:** no coalition currently exists in the US to pursue explicit pro-natalist policy, and the public does not rank low fertility as a priority problem.

What to do next: watch the post-Dobbs birth-rate differential between restricted and protected states as the most informative US natural experiment on fertility policy. Investigate whether housing deregulation (Minneapolis 2018, California 2021, statewide ADU reforms) produces measurable fertility increases in affected jurisdictions. Read the CEA's 2024 analysis of Dobbs birth-rate effects and the Doepke et al. (2023) handbook chapter for the definitive conceptual framework. What to believe with caution: initial estimates of pro-natalist policy effects (many capture timing effects, not completed fertility); claims that any single policy lever can substantially raise US fertility (all evidence points to marginal effects from any single intervention).
