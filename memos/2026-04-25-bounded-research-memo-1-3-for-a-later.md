# Fiscal and Distributional Mechanics of a Federal-State Responsibility Swap

**Date:** 2026-04-25  
**Output Type:** research_memo  
**Source Prompt:** Bounded research memo 1/3 for a later synthesis brief. Parent prompt: Federal and State Responsibility Swap Proposal - Have federal government assume state and local pension debt (create Pension Benefit Guarantee Corp) in exchange for eliminating state/local tax deduction and mortgage interest deduction. Could include sales tax vs. VAT swap element as well. Swap State and Local Medicaid responsibility (approx $200 billion) for PreK starting at age 3 in every school district in Country. Get rid of Head Start ($10 billion). Focus: Evidence memo: fiscal, quantitative, and distributional mechanics.

---

## Executive Summary

The rough arithmetic does **not** naturally close if the federal government assumes both **all legacy state/local pension debt** and **the full state share of Medicaid**. Recent public-pension estimates put unfunded state and local pension liabilities at roughly **$1.5 trillion or more**, while states supplied about **$294 billion** of their own funds to Medicaid in FY2023. By contrast, the federal tax expenditure from the **state and local tax (SALT) deduction** is on the order of **$60 billion a year** under current-law 2026 scoring, and the **mortgage interest deduction (MID)** is roughly **$261 billion over FY2025-FY2029**—about **$50-55 billion per year** on average ([JCT/CRFB](https://www.crfb.org/blogs/jct-projects-tax-expenditures-will-be-23t-2026), [CRS MID brief](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF13190)).

That means SALT+MID repeal can plausibly finance **part** of a swap, but not the whole package on a clean annual basis if Medicaid assumption is full and immediate. The numbers become more plausible only if pension debt is treated as a **legacy refinancing/amortization problem** rather than an immediate cash payoff, and if states continue paying the normal cost of newly accrued pension benefits.

Distributionally, the package is highly asymmetric. Repealing SALT and MID would land mostly on **upper-income itemizers**, concentrated in high-tax and high-house-price states; the relief from federalizing pension debt and Medicaid would accrue first to **state and local governments**, pension beneficiaries, and jurisdictions with weak fiscal capacity or large legacy burdens. Universal public pre-K would shift some benefits toward families with young children, but the cost range is wide: about **$20 billion per year** in the 2021 White House proposal, about **$35 billion per year** in the Penn Wharton estimate, and up to **$60-70 billion per year** in a high-quality full-day model from NIEER ([White House](https://bidenwhitehouse.archives.gov/briefing-room/statements-releases/2021/04/29/fact-sheet-the-american-families-plan-will-support-children-teachers-and-working-families-in-rural-america/), [Penn Wharton](https://budgetmodel.wharton.upenn.edu/p/2022-06-02-total-cost-of-universal-pre-k-including-new-facilities/), [NIEER](https://nieer.org/research-library/funding-high-quality-pre-k-all)).

Bottom line: a federal-state responsibility swap is fiscally conceivable only as a **conditional restructuring package**, not as a simple claim that “deduction repeal pays for everything.” The cleanest synthesis for a later brief is: **Medicaid assumption is much larger than the proposed education replacement; pension assumption is fundamentally a financing and moral-hazard problem; and the tax-side offsets are progressive but too small, by themselves, to cover a full swap.**

## Question

What do the available public data suggest about the fiscal size, budget mechanics, and distributional consequences of a policy package in which the federal government assumes state and local pension liabilities and Medicaid financing obligations, while offsetting the cost by eliminating the state and local tax deduction, eliminating the mortgage interest deduction, and potentially replacing some state sales tax capacity with a federal VAT-like instrument and repurposed early-childhood spending?

## What matters most

1. **Legacy pension debt is stock, not flow.** The key number is roughly **$1.5 trillion** of unfunded liability, but the budget question is the annual amortization path, not just the headline stock ([Census pension survey](https://www.census.gov/data/tables/2023/econ/aspp/aspp-historical-tables.html), [Hoover summary](https://www.hoover.org/research/status-and-trends-unfunded-liabilities-state-and-local-pension-funds)).
2. **SALT and MID repeal are progressive, but not big enough alone.** Under current-law style estimates, together they look more like **~$110 billion per year** than a financing source for the entire Medicaid+pension package ([CRFB/JCT](https://www.crfb.org/blogs/jct-projects-tax-expenditures-will-be-23t-2026), [Tax Policy Center SALT](https://taxpolicycenter.org/fiscal-facts/what-salt-deduction-and-who-benefits-it), [Tax Policy Center MID](https://taxpolicycenter.org/fiscal-facts/who-benefits-mortgage-interest-deduction-and-who-misses-out)).
3. **The Medicaid-for-pre-K trade is not close to one-for-one.** Recent evidence puts state Medicaid own-source spending at about **$294 billion** in FY2023, while universal pre-K estimates range from **$20 billion to $70 billion annually**, and Head Start funding is about **$12.27 billion** ([Pew](https://www.pew.org/en/research-and-analysis/articles/2025/06/16/the-share-of-state-budgets-spent-on-medicaid-posts-largest-annual-increase-in-20-years), [Head Start FY2024 facts](https://headstart.gov/program-data/article/head-start-program-facts-fiscal-year-2024)).
4. **Incidence runs through geography and class at the same time.** Tax increases would hit affluent itemizers and homeowners; spending relief would favor heavily indebted and high-service-burden states and localities.
5. **The proposal’s credibility depends on conditions.** Without hard rules on future pension accruals, state fiscal effort, and Medicaid cost control, the plan invites moral hazard.

## Analysis

### 1. Pension assumption mechanics

The pension piece is easiest to overstate because it mixes a **stock problem** (legacy unfunded liabilities) with a **flow problem** (annual contributions and benefit payments). Authoritative summaries of the 2023 public pension data show roughly **$1.49-$1.65 trillion** in unfunded state and local liabilities, about **$199.2 billion** in employer contributions in 2023, and roughly **$389 billion** in annual benefit payments ([Census/ASPP](https://www.census.gov/data/tables/2023/econ/aspp/aspp-historical-tables.html), [Equable](https://equable.org/pension-funding-trends-2023/), [Hoover](https://www.hoover.org/research/status-and-trends-unfunded-liabilities-state-and-local-pension-funds)).

For a federal “Pension Benefit Guarantee Corp” concept, the fiscal question is not whether Washington must immediately write a **$1.5 trillion** check. It is whether the federal government:

- assumes only the **legacy underfunding** and amortizes it over decades;
- assumes **benefit guarantees** while forcing states/localities to keep paying normal cost;
- or fully federalizes both past debt and future accrual risk.

Those are radically different fiscal commitments. Hoover and related pension analyses suggest that governments would need roughly **$96 billion more per year** than current contributions to stabilize funding nationally. That is a better first-cut budget metric for a refinancing-style takeover than the full headline liability ([Hoover](https://www.hoover.org/research/status-and-trends-unfunded-liabilities-state-and-local-pension-funds)).

The distributional logic is also uneven. A federal assumption would disproportionately help places with the largest legacy shortfalls, while better-funded jurisdictions would rightly see it as a transfer to states and cities that underfunded plans or used optimistic discount rates. For synthesis purposes, the key point is: **the pension proposal is fiscally manageable only if it is conditional, partial, and tied to future funding discipline.**

### 2. Revenue offsets: SALT and mortgage interest deduction

The revenue side is more straightforward. These are classic upper-income itemized deductions whose benefits are already highly concentrated.

- On SALT, the Tax Policy Center reports that the share of filers claiming the deduction fell from roughly **30% before TCJA** to about **9%** after the cap and larger standard deduction; filers with AGI above **$100,000** claimed about **87%** of the total dollar amount, and the top 1% would capture about **43%** of the benefit from repealing the cap ([TPC SALT](https://taxpolicycenter.org/fiscal-facts/what-salt-deduction-and-who-benefits-it)).
- On MID, the Tax Policy Center reports that only about **8% of households** benefited in 2024, and about **75%** of the tax benefit went to households with incomes above **$200,000** ([TPC MID](https://taxpolicycenter.org/fiscal-facts/who-benefits-mortgage-interest-deduction-and-who-misses-out)).

For budget scale, recent JCT-based summaries put the SALT tax expenditure at about **$60 billion in FY2026**, while the mortgage interest deduction is around **$261.1 billion over FY2025-FY2029**, implying something like **$50-55 billion annually** under current-law conditions ([CRFB on JCT](https://www.crfb.org/blogs/jct-projects-tax-expenditures-will-be-23t-2026), [CRS MID brief](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF13190), [JCT publication page](https://www.jct.gov/publications/2024/jcx-48-24/)).

So the rough static budget value of repealing both is around **$110 billion per year**. That is material, and distributionally progressive relative to current law, but it is not enough to cover a full federal takeover of current state Medicaid financing. It is even less enough if one assumes upfront recognition of pension debt. One important caveat: **tax expenditure figures are not identical to repeal scores**. Behavioral responses, lower itemization, and interactions with other tax provisions mean real scored revenue could differ.

### 3. Medicaid-for-pre-K swap arithmetic

This is where the proposal looks least balanced. Recent state-budget analysis found that states spent about **$294 billion of their own funds on Medicaid in FY2023**, equal to about **15.1% of every state-generated dollar**, while KFF shows the average financing split remains roughly **65% federal / 35% state** nationwide ([Pew](https://www.pew.org/en/research-and-analysis/articles/2025/06/16/the-share-of-state-budgets-spent-on-medicaid-posts-largest-annual-increase-in-20-years), [KFF Medicaid financing indicator](https://www.kff.org/medicaid/state-indicator/federalstate-share-of-spending/)).

By contrast, the federal cost of universal public pre-K depends heavily on program design:

- the 2021 White House American Families Plan proposed **$200 billion over 10 years**—about **$20 billion per year**—for universal pre-K for all 3- and 4-year-olds ([White House fact sheet](https://bidenwhitehouse.archives.gov/briefing-room/statements-releases/2021/04/29/fact-sheet-the-american-families-plan-will-support-children-teachers-and-working-families-in-rural-america/));
- Penn Wharton estimated about **$351 billion over 10 years**, roughly **$35 billion per year** including facilities ([Penn Wharton](https://budgetmodel.wharton.upenn.edu/p/2022-06-02-total-cost-of-universal-pre-k-including-new-facilities/));
- NIEER estimated that a high-quality, full-day model serving 70% of all 3- and 4-year-olds could cost about **$70 billion annually** at maturity ([NIEER](https://nieer.org/research-library/funding-high-quality-pre-k-all)).

Head Start is much smaller than Medicaid in budget terms. The official FY2024 Head Start facts report **$12.27 billion** in funding to serve **715,873** children and pregnant women ([HeadStart.gov](https://headstart.gov/program-data/article/head-start-program-facts-fiscal-year-2024)). Eliminating Head Start therefore helps at the margin, but it does not turn the Medicaid-for-pre-K exchange into a close fiscal match.

The main implication is simple: **if Washington takes the full current state share of Medicaid, even an expensive universal pre-K program is much cheaper than what states give up.** That makes the package a large net fiscal transfer from the federal budget to states. It may still be defensible on efficiency or equity grounds, but not as a neat budget swap.

### 4. Distributional and intergovernmental consequences

The package has four distinct incidence channels:

1. **Upper-income households lose tax preferences.** SALT and MID repeal would fall mainly on higher-income homeowners and itemizers, especially in high-tax and high-house-price states. That makes the tax side progressive in income terms, though geographically concentrated ([TPC SALT](https://taxpolicycenter.org/fiscal-facts/what-salt-deduction-and-who-benefits-it), [TPC MID](https://taxpolicycenter.org/fiscal-facts/who-benefits-mortgage-interest-deduction-and-who-misses-out)).
2. **State and local governments gain fiscal room.** Federalizing Medicaid and pension debt relieves pressure on state budgets, local tax bases, and municipal balance sheets. The first-order beneficiaries are governments, not households directly.
3. **Public workers, retirees, and families with young children gain more direct protection.** Pension guarantees reduce default or crowd-out risk for retirees and workers; universal pre-K can improve labor-force participation for parents and early-learning access for children.
4. **Well-managed states subsidize weakly managed ones unless conditions are strict.** This is the strongest political-economy objection and likely the core congressional barrier.

There is also a racial and tenure dimension on the tax side. The mortgage interest deduction disproportionately benefits higher-income and disproportionately White homeowners, while renters receive nothing directly ([TPC racial MID analysis](https://taxpolicycenter.org/taxvox/home-mortgage-interest-deduction-reinforces-racial-disparities)). Repeal would therefore trim a regressive housing subsidy, but it could also meet fierce resistance in affluent suburban housing markets.

### 5. Counterarguments and implementation constraints

**Counterargument 1: pension assumption creates moral hazard.** This is substantially correct. A federal backstop without binding future funding rules would reward underfunding and invite repeat behavior.

**Counterargument 2: SALT and MID repeal raise less usable revenue than advocates imply.** Also correct in part. Tax expenditure values are not the same as clean repeal scores, and both deductions interact with housing markets, itemization choices, and state tax structures.

**Counterargument 3: Medicaid assumption is too open-ended.** Correct unless paired with new federal cost controls. Medicaid is a recurring entitlement with medical inflation risk; universal pre-K is a program with clearer spending caps.

**Counterargument 4: ending Head Start is not a clean substitute for district-run pre-K.** Also correct. Head Start serves a more disadvantaged population and bundles health, nutrition, and family-support services that ordinary pre-K programs may not replicate.

**Counterargument 5: a VAT/sales-tax swap could help close the numbers.** Possibly, but this memo does not treat that as proven. A federal VAT could raise large revenue, but without rebates or credits it would shift the package in a more regressive direction. That needs separate modeling rather than assumption.

## Claims check

- **“State/local Medicaid responsibility is about $200 billion”** — likely understated against recent state-fund spending; the best current figure I found is about **$294 billion** in FY2023 state funds.
- **“SALT + MID repeal can pay for the swap”** — too strong as stated. Under current-law style estimates, they likely raise on the order of **~$110 billion per year**, which is too small for a full Medicaid assumption and far too small for an upfront pension payoff.
- **“Federal assumption of pension debt costs $1.5 trillion immediately”** — not necessarily. That depends on whether the federal government refinances legacy debt over time, guarantees benefits, or fully cash-funds liabilities upfront.
- **“Universal pre-K costs X”** — contested. Serious estimates run from about **$20 billion/year** to **$70 billion/year** depending on quality, teacher pay, facilities, and take-up.
- **“Head Start can simply be replaced by district pre-K”** — unsupported without program-design work, because Head Start includes non-instructional services and targets disadvantaged children.

## Recommended follow-ups

1. **Build a state-by-state ledger** showing pension underfunding, Medicaid state spending, SALT/MID exposure, and likely net fiscal gain/loss from the swap.
2. **Separate legacy pension debt from future service cost** in all later modeling; otherwise the package will look either artificially impossible or artificially cheap.
3. **Commission a legal/implementation memo** on what federal pension assumption could actually mean: guarantee, refinancing facility, or full takeover.
4. **Run a dedicated VAT/sales-tax incidence model** before folding that option into the synthesis brief.
5. **Decision-oriented conclusion:** A policymaker should believe that the proposal is best understood as a large federal equalization and refinancing plan, not a self-financing tax reform. The next step is to choose whether the objective is pension rescue, Medicaid centralization, or early-childhood expansion; trying to sell all three as one arithmetically neat swap will overpromise.
