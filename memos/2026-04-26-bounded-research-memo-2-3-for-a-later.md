# Legal, Governance, and Political Feasibility of a Federal-State Responsibility Swap

**Date:** 2026-04-26  
**Output Type:** research_memo  
**Source Prompt:** Bounded research memo 2/3 for a later synthesis brief. Parent prompt: Federal and State Responsibility Swap Proposal - Have federal government assume state and local pension debt (create Pension Benefit Guarantee Corp) in exchange for eliminating state/local tax deduction and mortgage interest deduction. Could include sales tax vs. VAT swap element as well. Swap State and Local Medicaid responsibility (approx $200 billion) for Prek starting at age 3 in every school district in Country. Get rid of Head Start ($10 billion). Focus: Evidence memo: legal, governance, implementation, political feasibility, and counterarguments.

---

## Executive Summary

The proposal is **not obviously unconstitutional**, but it is **much harder to implement and pass politically than the fiscal arithmetic alone suggests**. Congress likely could create a new federal program that assumes some state/local pension liabilities and more of Medicaid, or could preempt existing arrangements and fund replacements directly. But the Constitution’s anti-commandeering cases mean Congress cannot simply order states to keep administering or financing a federalized arrangement, and **NFIB v. Sebelius** shows it cannot coerce states by threatening all existing Medicaid funds to force acceptance of a major restructuring ([CRS federalism overview](https://crsreports.congress.gov/product/pdf/R/R45395), [NFIB decision](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/11pdf/11-393c3a2.pdf), [CRS Medicaid overview](https://crsreports.congress.gov/product/pdf/RS/RS20178)).

The pension piece would require **new federal legislation**, not just repurposing current PBGC machinery. The **PBGC insures private-sector defined-benefit plans, not governmental plans**, and state public-pension promises are often protected by state constitutions, statutes, and contract doctrines. That makes a simple federal “takeover” of state and local pension debt legally and administratively incomplete unless Congress also defines funding rules, eligibility, oversight, and how accrued benefits interact with strong state-law protections ([PBGC coverage page](https://www.pbgc.gov/workers-retirees/learn/pension-insurance-coverage), [CRS PBGC primer](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF10492), [Pew legal protections survey](https://www.pew.org/en/research-and-analysis/issue-briefs/2019/05/legal-protections-for-state-pension-and-retiree-health-benefits)).

The governance problem is equally large. Medicaid is a **cooperative federal-state program** built around state plans, waivers, provider networks, and shared financing; Head Start is a **federal grant program with its own governance bodies and wraparound-service mandates**, not just a preschool slot purchaser. Major universal pre-K proposals have generally used **mixed-delivery models that include Head Start**, rather than abolishing it outright ([CMS Medicaid history](https://www.medicaid.gov/about-us/program-history/index.html), [KFF Medicaid financing](https://www.kff.org/medicaid/follow-the-money-how-medicaid-financing-works-and-what-that-means-for-proposals-to-change-it/), [Head Start governance](https://www.headstart.gov/organizational-leadership/article/program-governance-head-start), [U.S. Education/HHS mixed-delivery letter](https://www.ed.gov/sites/ed/files/2024/02/FINAL-Mixed-Delivery-DCL-signed_022624.pdf)).

Politically, the package is a **stacked coalition problem**: it combines a likely “blue-state” tax fight over SALT, resistance from homeowners and real-estate interests on MID, governor and provider anxiety over Medicaid centralization, and strong “no bailout” objections to federal pension assumption. The most plausible version for a later synthesis brief is therefore a **conditional, phased restructuring package**—for example, federal assumption of legacy pension liabilities only, strong maintenance-of-effort rules, and integration of Head Start into mixed-delivery pre-K—rather than an immediate all-at-once swap.

## Question

What do the best available sources suggest about the legality, governance design, implementation path, political feasibility, and strongest counterarguments for a policy package in which the federal government assumes state and local pension debt and the state share of Medicaid, while offsetting the shift through repeal of the SALT deduction and mortgage interest deduction and replacing Head Start with universal public pre-K?

## What matters most

1. **Congress probably can offer or create a swap, but it cannot commandeer state administration.** Anti-commandeering doctrine and Spending Clause coercion limits push the design toward voluntary or fully federal administration, not “states must run this our way” ([CRS federalism overview](https://crsreports.congress.gov/product/pdf/R/R45395), [NFIB decision](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/11pdf/11-393c3a2.pdf)).
2. **Current PBGC law is not a ready-made vehicle for public pensions.** Governmental plans sit outside existing PBGC insurance, so any public-pension guarantor would require major new law and oversight design ([PBGC coverage](https://www.pbgc.gov/workers-retirees/learn/pension-insurance-coverage), [CRS PBGC primer](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF10492)).
3. **State pension liabilities are legally sticky.** Many accrued public-pension benefits enjoy strong state-law protections, which makes haircut-based federal restructuring much harder than simple refinancing or backstopping ([Pew legal protections](https://www.pew.org/en/research-and-analysis/issue-briefs/2019/05/legal-protections-for-state-pension-and-retiree-health-benefits)).
4. **Medicaid and Head Start are governance-heavy programs, not just funding streams.** A federal swap would have to replace state Medicaid administration and Head Start’s family-governance/comprehensive-service model, not merely move dollars around ([CRS Medicaid overview](https://crsreports.congress.gov/product/pdf/RS/RS20178), [GAO on Medicaid administration](https://www.gao.gov/assets/gao-20-407.pdf), [Head Start governance](https://www.headstart.gov/organizational-leadership/article/program-governance-head-start)).
5. **The politics run against a clean grand bargain.** Each component mobilizes a different opposition coalition, making partial and highly conditioned legislation more plausible than a total swap ([Bipartisan Policy Center SALT explainer](https://bipartisanpolicy.org/explainer/how-salt-may-shake-up-the-2025-tax-debate/), [CNBC on SALT politics](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/09/20/house-lawmakers-salt-deduction.html), [KFF Medicaid financing](https://www.kff.org/medicaid/follow-the-money-how-medicaid-financing-works-and-what-that-means-for-proposals-to-change-it/)).

## Analysis

### 1. Legal authorities and constitutional constraints

The best legal reading is that Congress has **wide power to spend, tax, and preempt**, but more limited power to force states to execute a federal restructuring. The anti-commandeering cases—summarized by CRS through **New York**, **Printz**, and **Murphy**—bar Congress from simply ordering states to legislate or administer federal policy. Separately, **NFIB v. Sebelius** held that Congress went too far when it conditioned existing Medicaid funding on acceptance of a large new Medicaid expansion; the Chief Justice described that threat as a “gun to the head” for states ([CRS federalism overview](https://crsreports.congress.gov/product/pdf/R/R45395), [NFIB decision](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/11pdf/11-393c3a2.pdf)).

That points to three legally safer designs:

- **full federal administration** of any assumed function;
- **voluntary state opt-in** with conditions attached to new federal money;
- or **prospective preemption** combined with federal replacement rules.

For pensions, the immediate legal barrier is simpler: **current federal pension insurance does not cover state and local plans**. PBGC states that it does not insure federal, state, or local government pensions, and CRS describes PBGC as a backstop for private-sector defined-benefit plans. A governmental “Pension Benefit Guarantee Corp” would therefore require a new statute or a major amendment to ERISA-era architecture, plus explicit rules for premiums, eligibility, reporting, and federal fiscal backing ([PBGC coverage](https://www.pbgc.gov/workers-retirees/learn/pension-insurance-coverage), [CRS PBGC primer](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF10492)).

The harder pension issue is not federal power in the abstract but **what is being assumed**. Pew’s 50-state survey shows that legal protections for accrued state pension benefits vary, but many states protect them through constitutions, contract clauses, statutes, or case law. That means a federal plan built around **honoring or refinancing accrued promises** is legally easier to defend than one that assumes authority to reduce or rewrite already-earned benefits ([Pew legal protections](https://www.pew.org/en/research-and-analysis/issue-briefs/2019/05/legal-protections-for-state-pension-and-retiree-health-benefits)).

### 2. Governance design and intergovernmental administration

Medicaid is not just a transfer from Washington to states; it is a **jointly financed, state-administered program**. CRS, CMS, and KFF all describe Medicaid as a cooperative federal-state arrangement in which states submit plans, seek waivers, set many payment and delivery rules, and operate large administrative systems under broad federal requirements ([CRS Medicaid overview](https://crsreports.congress.gov/product/pdf/RS/RS20178), [CMS Medicaid history](https://www.medicaid.gov/about-us/program-history/index.html), [KFF Medicaid financing](https://www.kff.org/medicaid/follow-the-money-how-medicaid-financing-works-and-what-that-means-for-proposals-to-change-it/)).

That matters because a “federal assumption” can mean at least two different things:

- **Washington pays, but states still run most of the machinery**; or
- **Washington both pays and replaces much of the machinery**.

The first option preserves state capacity but leaves moral-hazard and cost-control problems in place. The second option may be cleaner fiscally, but GAO’s survey of all 50 states shows how heterogeneous Medicaid administration already is, making uniform federal replacement a large operational lift with real risk of disruption ([GAO Medicaid administration](https://www.gao.gov/assets/gao-20-407.pdf)).

Head Start creates a parallel governance issue on the education side. The program is federally funded and regulated through HHS, and federal rules require both a governing body and a parent-heavy Policy Council, alongside mandates for health, nutrition, disability, and family-support services. That governance and service model is not the same as ordinary district pre-K ([Head Start governance](https://www.headstart.gov/organizational-leadership/article/program-governance-head-start)).

That is why major universal-pre-K designs have usually aimed for **mixed delivery**, not substitution. The 2024 Education/HHS letter urged states to expand preschool through systems that include schools, Head Start, community providers, and family child care. ACF and other policy analyses similarly treat Head Start as an infrastructure partner, not just a budget line to eliminate ([U.S. Education/HHS mixed-delivery letter](https://www.ed.gov/sites/ed/files/2024/02/FINAL-Mixed-Delivery-DCL-signed_022624.pdf), [ACF on Head Start systems interaction](https://acf.gov/opre/report/head-starts-interaction-federal-state-and-local-systems)).

### 3. Implementation path and transition risks

The implementation question is less “can Congress pass a bill?” than “what transition can survive contact with existing institutions?” The safest design for the pension side is a **legacy-liability intervention**: the federal government could assume or refinance already-accrued underfunding while requiring states and localities to keep paying the **normal cost** of future accruals. That does not eliminate moral hazard, but it narrows it compared with a full guarantee of all future state pension decisions. NASRA’s governance materials underscore how much plan authority is currently dispersed among legislatures, executives, boards, and administrators; replacing that entire structure federally would be more disruptive than ring-fencing legacy debt ([NASRA governance](https://www.nasra.org/content.asp?admin=Y&contentid=244)).

On Medicaid, the transition risk runs in both directions. If the federal government assumes financing but leaves state discretion mostly intact, federal exposure rises while state incentives to economize weaken. If it federalizes both financing and rules, it inherits a huge set of state-specific contracts, provider-payment systems, eligibility workflows, waiver arrangements, and data systems. KFF and GAO both suggest that this diversity is central to how Medicaid currently functions, which means a rapid nationalization would be legally possible only with an extensive transition period and probably years of rulemaking and procurement ([KFF Medicaid financing](https://www.kff.org/medicaid/follow-the-money-how-medicaid-financing-works-and-what-that-means-for-proposals-to-change-it/), [GAO Medicaid administration](https://www.gao.gov/assets/gao-20-407.pdf)).

For pre-K, the implementation lesson from current policy is similar: **layer first, replace later if at all**. Federal and state guidance around mixed-delivery systems assumes that existing Head Start grantees and community providers remain part of the capacity base. Eliminating Head Start before equivalent wraparound services, parent-governance structures, and provider contracts are rebuilt would create avoidable service loss for the highest-need children ([U.S. Education/HHS mixed-delivery letter](https://www.ed.gov/sites/ed/files/2024/02/FINAL-Mixed-Delivery-DCL-signed_022624.pdf), [ACF on Head Start systems interaction](https://acf.gov/opre/report/head-starts-interaction-federal-state-and-local-systems)).

### 4. Political feasibility

Political feasibility is the proposal’s weakest dimension. On the tax side, even today’s narrower SALT fights are difficult because lawmakers from high-tax states can make themselves pivotal in broader tax packages. Current reporting and policy analysis both show SALT remains a bargaining chip rather than settled terrain, while reform of the mortgage interest deduction still runs into homeowner and real-estate interests in expensive markets ([Bipartisan Policy Center SALT explainer](https://bipartisanpolicy.org/explainer/how-salt-may-shake-up-the-2025-tax-debate/), [CNBC on SALT politics](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/09/20/house-lawmakers-salt-deduction.html), [Yale Budget Lab MID options](https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/mortgage-interest-deduction-options-reform)).

The spending side also assembles strong opposition. A federal assumption of pension debt will be attacked as a **bailout of weakly governed jurisdictions**; RAND, Urban/Boston College legal work, and other analyses all highlight moral hazard and fairness concerns in federal rescues of subnational fiscal problems ([RAND pension crises brief](https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_briefs/RBA2307-2.html), [Beyond Bailouts](https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/publication/24796/1001582-Beyond-Bailouts-Federal-Tools-for-Preventing-State-Budget-Crises.PDF)).

Full Medicaid federalization faces a different but equally serious coalition: governors, state Medicaid agencies, providers, and fiscal conservatives all have reasons to resist. KFF and Pew emphasize that Medicaid financing is central to state budgeting and state policy leverage, so even states that would welcome more federal money may resist surrendering control or accepting the risk of future federal retrenchment ([KFF Medicaid financing](https://www.kff.org/medicaid/follow-the-money-how-medicaid-financing-works-and-what-that-means-for-proposals-to-change-it/), [Pew on Medicaid state pressures](https://www.pew.org/en/research-and-analysis/articles/2026/01/13/new-federal-medicaid-policies-compound-state-budget-pressures)).

Finally, replacing Head Start outright is politically harder than expanding pre-K around it. Head Start has federal grantees, local nonprofit operators, parent-governance structures, and anti-poverty advocates who would all see outright abolition as loss, not simplification. That is one reason recent federal mixed-delivery guidance and universal-pre-K proposals have usually included Head Start rather than displaced it ([Head Start governance](https://www.headstart.gov/organizational-leadership/article/program-governance-head-start), [U.S. Education/HHS mixed-delivery letter](https://www.ed.gov/sites/ed/files/2024/02/FINAL-Mixed-Delivery-DCL-signed_022624.pdf)).

### 5. Counterarguments and contradictory evidence

There are serious arguments **for** a larger federal role. One is that repeated emergency aid to states can produce the worst of both worlds: Washington supplies money without taking enough responsibility for long-run rules. Some analysts therefore argue that if a function is already quasi-national in financing or distributive purpose, a clearer federal role may improve transparency and reduce gamesmanship. That point has some force for Medicaid, where interstate inequities and recession-driven state budget stress are persistent features of the current system ([KFF Medicaid financing](https://www.kff.org/medicaid/follow-the-money-how-medicaid-financing-works-and-what-that-means-for-proposals-to-change-it/), [Manhattan Institute on nationalizing entitlements](https://manhattan.institute/article/beyond-bailout-federalism-the-case-for-nationalizing-entitlements)).

A second pro argument is that a mixed-delivery pre-K expansion could absorb Head Start’s strengths rather than erase them. The problem is that this supports an **integration** strategy more than an abolition strategy. The better evidence points toward building around Head Start and other providers, not assuming district pre-K is a clean one-for-one replacement ([ACF on Head Start systems interaction](https://acf.gov/opre/report/head-starts-interaction-federal-state-and-local-systems), [U.S. Education/HHS mixed-delivery letter](https://www.ed.gov/sites/ed/files/2024/02/FINAL-Mixed-Delivery-DCL-signed_022624.pdf)).

The strongest counterarguments remain:

1. **anti-bailout fairness** — well-managed states and localities will resist cross-subsidizing weakly funded pension systems;
2. **moral hazard** — federal assumption without hard maintenance-of-effort rules invites repeat underfunding;
3. **administrative overreach** — nationalizing Medicaid would require replacing deeply state-specific systems;
4. **service loss risk** — ending Head Start could strip out comprehensive services that ordinary pre-K does not automatically provide; and
5. **coalition overload** — combining tax increases, Medicaid centralization, pension rescue, and Head Start elimination in one bill makes enactment materially harder than any single component.

## Claims check

- **“Congress can just do this by ordinary statute.”** Too simple. Congress likely can legislate a broader federal role, but the anti-commandeering and coercion cases materially constrain how it can force states into the arrangement.
- **“A PBGC-style corporation already exists for this purpose.”** False under current law. PBGC does not insure state and local plans.
- **“Federal assumption of pension debt would let Washington rewrite benefits.”** Weak. Existing accrued-benefit protections are heavily state-law dependent and often strong.
- **“Medicaid can be federalized without rebuilding administration.”** Weak. Financing and administration are tightly linked in the current program.
- **“Ending Head Start is a clean budget offset for universal pre-K.”** Unsupported as stated. Head Start carries governance structures and wraparound services that district pre-K does not automatically replace.
- **“SALT and MID repeal make the politics easier because they hit mostly upper-income households.”** Incomplete. Distributionally that is broadly true, but politically the affected coalition is concentrated and well organized.
- **“A VAT or sales-tax swap closes the feasibility problem.”** Not shown here. That may improve revenue capacity, but it raises separate design and distribution questions outside this memo’s evidence base.

## Recommended follow-ups

1. Do a **state-by-state legal annex** on public-pension protections before any synthesis brief recommends federal assumption mechanics.
2. Build a **transition options table** for Medicaid: financing-only assumption, full federal administration, or opt-in federal contracts with states.
3. Model a **legacy-liabilities-only pension option** that leaves future accruals and normal cost with states/localities.
4. Treat **Head Start integration vs. replacement** as a separate design choice, not an automatic budget offset.
5. If the later synthesis brief keeps the VAT/sales-tax idea, commission a separate memo on its legal design, distribution, and political interaction with SALT repeal.
