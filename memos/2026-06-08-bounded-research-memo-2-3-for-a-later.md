## Executive summary (answer first)

Building small urban-river “beach” segments along the Anacostia/RFK corridor appears **legally feasible but procedurally heavy**. The highest-probability path is a **limited, pilot-scale, nature-based shoreline + managed access design**, not a large hard-engineered swim beach.

Why: (1) projects at/into the river trigger overlapping USACE + DC DOEE permits (Clean Water Act §404, Rivers and Harbors Act §10, and DC §401/wetland-stream rules), (2) the RFK campus transfer law adds binding covenants (park/open-space, river access, and riparian-area constraints), and (3) water quality and contaminated sediment risks require phased implementation and operational controls.

Bottom line for later synthesis: if memo 1/3 estimated baseline construction cost per 100 feet, decision-makers should assume **material add-ons for permitting, mitigation, monitoring, and operations** before a “swimmable beach” is politically durable.

## Question

What does current evidence suggest about the legal, governance, implementation, and political feasibility of creating beach frontage on riverbanks like the Anacostia near former RFK, and what are the strongest counterarguments?

## What matters most

1. **Permitting stack is unavoidable** for in-water fill/shoreline changes.
2. **RFK governance changed in January 2025** (administrative jurisdiction transfer), but federal constraints remain.
3. **Water-contact use remains conditional** in practice due bacteria and sediment legacies.
4. **Political feasibility depends on project type**: access + habitat + pilot operations is easier than “full-time swim beach.”

## Analysis

### 1) Legal feasibility: feasible, but only through multi-agency compliance

- DOEE states all District streams/wetlands are regulated, with federal-jurisdictional impacts requiring USACE §404 and DOEE §401 certification; non-federal waters still require District wetland/stream permits.
- DOEE’s §401 page confirms certification can approve, condition, deny, or waive requests and references a one-year decision window under CWA §401.
- USACE Baltimore describes permit pathways: nationwide/letter-of-permission for minor impacts versus individual permits for potentially substantial impacts requiring public-interest review and public notice.

**Implication:** even “small beach” concepts are not zoning-only decisions; they are water-law projects first.

### 2) Governance at RFK/Anacostia edge: more local control, but covenant-limited

- Public Law 118-274 (Jan. 6, 2025) transferred RFK campus administrative jurisdiction to DC (ownership remains federal), allows mixed uses, and imposes conditions.
- The law requires at least 30% park/open space (excluding riparian area), improved Anacostia access, no interruption of the Anacostia River Trail, and includes a riparian-area rule that limits new development/use there to existing development/infrastructure maintenance.
- The same law preserves obligations under CERCLA/Solid Waste law and includes reversion risk for covenant noncompliance.

**Implication:** governance is clearer than before transfer, but river-edge design space is not unconstrained; “beach” must be mapped against riparian-area limits and covenant compliance.

### 3) Implementation constraints: water quality gains are real, but public-health and sediment constraints remain

- DC Water reports major CSO progress through the Anacostia tunnel system (substantial reductions, with project segments extending from RFK area to Poplar Point).
- DOEE still frames swimming as historically banned with only permitted special-event exemptions; event approval requires sampling/testing, contingency plans, insurance, and interagency coordination (including NPS and MPD).
- DOEE announced daily E. coli testing in March 2026 for Potomac/Anacostia sites, signaling that operational variability still matters for recreational contact decisions.
- NOAA trustee information for the Anacostia identifies ongoing concerns (PCBs, PAHs, metals, etc.) and fish-consumption advisories.

**Implication:** implementation should separate (a) shoreline access/amenity from (b) routine swim designation; the latter needs stronger and sustained water-quality confidence.

### 4) Federal regulatory context changed again in 2026

- USACE announced 2026 nationwide permits (effective March 15, 2026 through March 15, 2031), replacing 2021 permits.
- EPA’s WOTUS implementation page confirms continuing post-*Sackett* complexity (2023 rule as conformed, plus state-by-state litigation posture and pre-2015 regime in some jurisdictions).

**Implication:** teams should not rely on stale 2021 permit assumptions; legal scoping must use 2026 NWPs and current jurisdictional determination practice.

### 5) Political feasibility and counterarguments

**Why it can pass politically**
- River access, visible waterfront activation, and park-forward framing align with RFK campus statutory requirements (public access and park/open space).
- A pilot-first model can demonstrate safety and ecological performance before full buildout.

**Serious counterarguments**
- **Environmental/habitat:** replacing vegetated edge with sand can reduce habitat and worsen erosion if poorly designed; living-shoreline guidance emphasizes site suitability and lower-energy contexts.
- **Public-health trust:** episodic bacterial spikes and legacy contaminants can undermine confidence in “urban beach” branding.
- **Governance conflict risk:** ongoing contamination accountability disputes (e.g., DC litigation against federal actors over historic Anacostia pollution) can politicize siting and sequencing.
- **Opportunity cost:** funds may produce larger health returns via upstream stormwater/CSO/source-control than beach amenities.
