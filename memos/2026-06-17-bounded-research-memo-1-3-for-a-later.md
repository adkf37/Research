## Executive summary

Prior memos (June 5–8, 2026) on this question converge on a **central planning range of roughly $60k–$310k per 100 ft frontage × 20 ft depth** for Anacostia-like urban river conditions, with the spread driven by site-specific permitting, contamination, and stabilization needs. Lower-bound estimates (~$60k–$180k) assume simplified permitting and clean sediment; upper bounds (~$200k–$650k+) incorporate full regulatory stack, contamination handling, and structural retention.

Three fresh findings tighten the decision framework:

1. **DC Water's Clean Rivers Project** has reached ~98% CSO reduction to the Anacostia, with the Potomac River Tunnel TBM ("Emily") now on site (May 2026). This materially advances the water-quality timeline for swim feasibility but does not yet eliminate E. coli exceedances after rain events.

2. **DOEE's water quality monitoring infrastructure** (USGS "super gauge," real-time E. coli forecasting, microbial source tracking, Enterococci PCR pilot) is operationally active and could support conditional swim-event permitting within 1–3 years for designated reaches—reducing the "2032 only" constraint cited in earlier memos.

3. **EPA's Urban Waters Anacostia profile (updated Sep 2025)** explicitly states that "inequality in DC region has grown, and some residents fear displacement caused by gentrification as the river improves"—the strongest institutional acknowledgment yet that distributional safeguards must accompany any riverfront amenity investment.

**Bottom line for decision-makers:** The cost-per-100-ft question is now less about tightening the unit-cost range and more about sequencing: (a) site-specific sediment screening to determine which band applies, (b) a 3-year water-quality monitoring and conditional-use pathway rather than waiting for full swim criteria, and (c) parallel anti-displacement funding from the outset.

## Question

What is the evidence-based cost per 100 feet of frontage (20 ft beach depth) for building a beach on a tree/grass urban riverside like the Anacostia near RFK, and how do fiscal and distributional mechanics shape the answer?

## What matters most

1. **Prior memo estimates converge but disagree on the midpoint.** Earlier memos give $30k–$150k (v1), $80k–$310k (v2), $40k–$200k (v3), and $60k–$180k (low complexity synthesis). The range reflects genuine method and assumption differences, not error. No memo claims a single-point estimate is reliable without site-specific data.

2. **Water quality improvements are accelerating faster than earlier memos assumed.** DC Water reports 98% CSO reduction to the Anacostia as operational, and the Potomac River Tunnel will add further dry-weather capture. DOEE has moved from "monitoring-only" to active predictive modeling and PCR rapid testing—tools that enable conditional-use swimming within 1–3 years, not the 2032 timeline cited in the June 5 memo.

3. **Distributional risk is now institutional consensus.** EPA, Urban Institute, and Trust for Public Land all document that waterfront investment without anti-displacement safeguards can worsen equity outcomes. The EPA Anacostia profile (2025 update) is particularly direct about displacement fears among existing residents.

4. **Permitting pathway remains the dominant cost uncertainty.** Maryland DNR's living shoreline costs ($100–$1,000+/lf) exclude federal Section 404 individual permit costs, which can add $50k–$200k+ for a single 100-ft segment in urban conditions.

## Analysis

### 1) Prior memo estimate convergence and divergence

| Memo (date) | Planning range per 100 ft × 20 ft | Method |
|---|---|---|
| June 5 (v1) | ~$60k–$135k (urban non-contaminated); ~$180k–$400k (contaminated) | Component build-up (clearing, grading, sand, stabilization, permitting) |
| June 8 (v2) | ~$80k–$310k; central ~$160k | NOAA peer-reviewed $/lf dataset (hybrid $1,626/lf, nourishment $3,094/lf) |
| June 8 (v3) | ~$40k–$200k; working midpoint ~$60k–$150k | Maryland DNR $/lf + USACE nourishment $/cy |
| June 8 (synthesis brief) | ~$60k–$180k (low); ~$200k–$650k (likely); ~$650k–$1.2M+ (complex) | Multi-method synthesis with full urban overhead |

Convergence: all peg the **low-to-mid complexity urban band at $60k–$200k** and **high-complexity contaminated/permitting-heavy band at $200k–$650k+**.

Divergence: the lower bands differ by whether sand-only costs are included vs. total project cost. V3's $40k–$200k includes design, permitting, and monitoring uplift but uses the lower end of Maryland DNR's $/lf range. V2's $80k–$310k relies on peer-reviewed dataset that averages actual project costs across Mid-Atlantic sites. The difference is real: $40k is possible if the site is clean, simple, and already accessed; $300k+ is more realistic for a constrained urban reach with full compliance.

### 2) Fresh water quality evidence (June 2026 update)

**DC Water Clean Rivers Project status:**
- 98% CSO reduction to Anacostia River already operational (from ~2.6 billion gallons/year pre-project)
- 96% system-wide CSO reduction
- Potomac River Tunnel: tunnel boring machine "Emily" arrived from Germany May 2026; construction ongoing
- Northeast Boundary Tunnel: reduces flood risk from ~50% to ~7% annual chance
- Green infrastructure: 92 impervious acres managed at Rock Creek

**DOEE monitoring infrastructure (active as of 2025–2026):**
- USGS "super gauge" on tidal Anacostia: real-time stage, velocity, temperature, specific conductance, pH, DO, turbidity, nitrate
- Spatial/Temporal Forecasting of Bacteria Populations: USGS-DOEE project developing E. coli predictive modeling for tidal Anacostia
- Enterococci PCR pilot: testing rapid (5-hour) method for same-day swim advisories—if validated, this enables real-time beach management
- Microbial source tracking: human vs. non-human source identification completed for Anacostia and Rock Creek
- Citizen science monitoring: Anacostia Riverkeeper, Potomac Riverkeeper Network volunteer programs

**Implication for beach feasibility:** These systems are cumulatively building toward **conditional-use swimming**—monitored, post-rain-closure, real-time-advisory operations—well before the 2032 full-criteria target. A beach designed with operational monitoring infrastructure (signage, real-time data display, adaptive closure protocols) could be usable within a 1–3 year planning horizon, not a 6–10 year one.

### 3) Distributional mechanics: fresh EPA framing

EPA's Urban Waters Anacostia profile (updated September 2025) frames the distributional challenge explicitly:

> "The Anacostia River flows next to communities that suffer some of the region's lowest employment rates, highest poverty rates, poorest health indicators... inequality in the DC region has grown, and some residents fear displacement caused by gentrification as the river improves."

This is the strongest primary-source acknowledgment available that distributional risk is not hypothetical—it is the central political and social constraint on any riverfront investment. Prior memos cited Urban Institute and TPL in general terms; the EPA profile is location-specific.

The core fiscal-distributional design problem: a per-100-ft capital cost of $60k–$650k is modest public expenditure, but the value it creates (amenity, view premium, recreation access) will capitalize into land values. Without paired instruments—community land trusts, anti-displacement funds, inclusionary zoning overlay, local hiring set-asides, and affordable recreation pricing—the incumbents most at risk are precisely the communities EPA identifies.

### 4) Fiscal structure update

Maryland DNR's living shoreline loan program provides a useful fiscal model: 0% interest loans to homeowners, nonprofits, and municipalities, with no upfront cash contribution for loans under $60k. This is relevant because it demonstrates a working state-level mechanism for financing shoreline stabilization at scales similar to a 100-ft beach segment. The loan terms (5–20 years, admin fee 3–10%) suggest that capital cost amortization is feasible.

For a DC-based project, available fiscal pathways include:
- USACE CAP Section 103 (beach erosion): 65% federal / 35% non-federal cost share with local sponsor bearing lands and O&M
- EPA Urban Waters Program grants (competitive, smaller scale)
- DC Water rate-funded green infrastructure (if beach integrates CSO reduction function)
- DOEE watershed restoration grants (RiverSmart, stream restoration programs)

The most cost-effective path for a single 100-ft pilot segment is likely a DOEE-led concept design with USACE pre-application consultation, funded through a combination of state/local capital budget and EPA Section 319 or Urban Waters small grant, with anti-displacement programming funded separately through DC's Housing Production Trust Fund.

## Contradictory evidence / counterarguments

1. **"98% CSO reduction means the river is almost swimmable."** Not yet—DOEE data show that even with 98% CSO capture, post-rain E. coli spikes still occur from non-CSO sources (stormwater runoff, wildlife, illicit connections). The conditional-use pathway is promising but not a guarantee.

2. **"The memo convergence means the cost estimate is settled."** The convergence is on ranges, not point estimates. The difference between $60k and $310k for the same linear foot is real money and depends entirely on site-specific factors (sediment chemistry, permit path, access constraints) that cannot be resolved through desk research.

3. **"Displacement risk is overstated for a small beach."** Counterpoint: even small high-profile amenities have outsized signaling effects in hot real estate markets. The RFK redevelopment is estimated at billions; a $1–3M beach frontage is marginal in that context but can be a symbolic anchor for neighborhood change.

The per-100-ft cost question is now well-bounded at **$60k–$310k for typical urban conditions**, with **$200k–$650k+** when contamination, complex permits, and structural retention are required. Decision-makers should stop refining the range and start investing in site-specific data.
