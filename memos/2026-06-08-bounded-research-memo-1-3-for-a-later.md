# Building Beaches on Tree- and Grass-Filled Riversides: Fiscal, Quantitative, and Distributional Mechanics
Date: 2026-06-08
Output type: research_memo
Source prompt: Bounded research memo 1/3 for a later synthesis brief. Parent prompt: Best ways to build beaches on tree and grass filled riversides (like the Anacostia River around the former RFK stadium). How much does it cost per 100 feet of frontage, assuming 20 feet of beach from shore? Focus: Evidence memo: fiscal, quantitative, and distributional mechanics.

## Executive summary

For a **100-foot frontage × 20-foot beach** on an Anacostia-like urban riverside, a practical planning range is **about $80,000 to $310,000 per 100 feet** for shoreline/beach construction work, with a central planning case around **$160,000 per 100 feet**.

Why this range is wide:
- A peer-reviewed Mid-Atlantic dataset shows average costs of about **$1,626/lf for hybrid shoreline** and **$3,094/lf for beach nourishment** (implying **$162,600 to $309,400 per 100 feet**).
- A nearby large USACE watershed restoration benchmark implies roughly **$813/lf (~$81,300 per 100 feet)**, but that project is not a one-to-one “public beach” analogue.
- Regulatory and water-quality constraints in DC are binding: any shoreline fill work is in Clean Water Act Section 404 territory, and routine swimming in DC rivers remains restricted despite major CSO progress.

Distributionally, waterfront amenities can improve access and health, but without anti-displacement policy, benefits can shift to higher-income newcomers. So the decision is not just engineering cost per foot; it is a package of shoreline design + permitting + water-quality realism + equity safeguards.

## Question

What is a defensible cost per 100 feet of frontage (assuming 20 feet of beach depth) for building beaches on tree/grass urban riverbanks like the Anacostia near RFK, and how should fiscal and distributional mechanics shape decisions?

## What matters most

1. **Unit-cost evidence is method-dependent, not one number.** Mid-Atlantic evidence differs substantially across nourishment, hybrid living shoreline, and hard-armoring approaches.
2. **Regulation is a core fiscal driver.** Shoreline fill/placement requires federal permitting under CWA Section 404 (USACE-administered), with project-specific conditions and mitigation.
3. **Anacostia context constrains use value.** DC still generally prohibits routine swimming in rivers; that affects near-term recreational benefit assumptions.
4. **Distributional outcomes are policy-sensitive.** Waterfront improvements can raise nearby land values and displacement pressure unless paired with equity and anti-displacement tools.

## Analysis

### 1) Geometry and baseline quantity math

- Frontage: 100 ft
- Beach depth: 20 ft
- Surface area: **2,000 sq ft**

This geometry is useful for conceptual design, but available institutional cost evidence is mostly reported **per linear foot of shoreline treatment**, not per square foot of beach area.

### 2) Cost evidence for shoreline/beach methods

A peer-reviewed NOAA-hosted Mid-Atlantic/New England comparative dataset reports mean installed costs approximately:

- **Hybrid shoreline (stone + vegetation, lower-exposure): $1,626/lf**
- **Beach nourishment: $3,094/lf**
- **Hard walls: $5,628/lf**

Converted to this memo’s 100-foot unit:
- Hybrid: **$162,600 per 100 ft**
- Nourishment: **$309,400 per 100 ft**
- Hard wall comparator: **$562,800 per 100 ft** (included as a capex comparator, not a beach recommendation)

Interpretation: for a public “beach-like” edge in an urban river corridor, **hybrid shoreline costs are often the most policy-relevant benchmark**; pure nourishment can be materially higher depending on exposure and maintenance regime.

### 3) Local benchmark context from Anacostia restoration

USACE/Prince George’s County reported approximately **$30M federal funding** (with total project cost around **$34M**) for restoration actions including roughly **7 miles** of stream habitat restoration.

Back-of-envelope conversion from total cost:
- $34,000,000 / (7 × 5,280 ft) ≈ **$920/lf**
- Equivalent ≈ **$92,000 per 100 ft**

Using the federal appropriation amount alone ($30M) gives ≈ **$813/lf** (≈ **$81,300 per 100 ft**).

Caveat: this project is watershed/stream restoration, not a direct beach installation. It is best used as a **lower-bound regional institutional comparator**, not a direct bid proxy.

### 4) Regulatory and schedule mechanics (fiscal implications)

- EPA and USACE guidance: discharge of dredged/fill material into waters/wetlands requires Section 404 authorization.
- Activities with minimal impacts may proceed under general permits; larger or more impactful projects can require individual review and mitigation.

Fiscal implication: permitting path uncertainty can dominate schedule risk and transaction costs even when direct construction quantities are modest.

### 5) Distributional mechanics

Evidence from Urban Institute and Trust for Public Land suggests a recurring pattern:
- Waterfront/park investments can improve environmental access and amenities.
- Without safeguards, they can also increase nearby rents/values and displacement pressures.
- Equity outcomes improve when projects are bundled with anti-displacement tools (tenant protections, affordability preservation, inclusive governance, targeted programming and access investments).

For Anacostia-adjacent deployment, the distributional question is not just “how much per 100 ft,” but **who can still live nearby and use the amenity after investment**.

### 6) Contradictory evidence and serious counterarguments

1. **Counterargument: costs should be much lower for a small 2,000 sq ft beach footprint.**  
   Plausible for simplified, lightly regulated sites; however, institutional Mid-Atlantic shoreline evidence shows much higher realized $/lf once stabilization/permitting context is included.

2. **Counterargument: large watershed restoration comparators imply lower costs (~$80k–$92k per 100 ft).**  
   True for some regional restoration programs, but those are not one-to-one with public beach builds and may understate beach-specific edge treatment and public-use requirements.

3. **Counterargument: if swimming is not yet routinely legal/safe, benefit may be overstated.**  
   Strong point in current Anacostia conditions; near-term value may be more “riverfront recreation/access” than designated swim-beach utility.

## Claims check

- **Claim: $80k–$310k per 100 ft is a defensible planning envelope.**  
  **Status:** Supported but approximate. Built from institutional comparators with different scopes; appropriate for screening-level budgeting, not procurement.

- **Claim: Central case near $160k per 100 ft is reasonable for hybrid shoreline treatment.**  
  **Status:** Well-supported by peer-reviewed per-linear-foot mean converted to 100 feet.

- **Claim: Anacostia context can reduce near-term swim benefits even if shoreline amenity is built.**  
  **Status:** Well-supported by DC swimming-status and water-quality sources.

- **Claim: Waterfront investments can worsen displacement without policy safeguards.**  
  **Status:** Supported by institutional and policy synthesis sources; local magnitude remains context-specific.

## Decision-oriented conclusion

For budgeting and policy screening, use **$80k–$310k per 100 feet** (20-foot depth), with **~$160k/100 ft** as a central hybrid-shoreline case and **~$300k/100 ft** as an upper nourishment-like case in constrained urban conditions.

Decision-makers should proceed only with a bundled approach: shoreline design + permit-path certainty + realistic non-swim/swim benefit framing + explicit anti-displacement safeguards. The biggest thing to watch is not just capex uncertainty; it is whether permitting and water-quality realities allow the intended public use on the project timeline.
