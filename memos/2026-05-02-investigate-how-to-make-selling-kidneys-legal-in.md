# Making Kidney Sales Legal in the United States: Federal Law, State Hurdles, Public-Payer Frictions, and Likely Compensation Levels

**Date:** 2026-05-02  
**Output Type:** research_memo  
**Source Prompt:** Investigate how to make selling kidneys legal in the U.S. Focus mostly on federal laws, but also look at State laws that may be hurdles. Then look at Medicare and Medicaid regulations that might make it difficult and finally curious about how much we might pay for a kidney based on other countries experiences or pilot programs.

---

## Executive Summary

The shortest answer is that **Congress would have to legalize compensated kidney donation first**, because the main federal barrier is the National Organ Transplant Act’s ban on transferring a human organ for “valuable consideration,” codified at [42 U.S.C. § 274e](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/42/274e). That ban already contains narrow exceptions for donor expenses and paired exchange, which matters because it shows the path forward: when Congress wanted to permit paired kidney exchange, it amended the law rather than relying on agency reinterpretation ([42 U.S.C. § 274e](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/42/274e); [Charlie W. Norwood Living Organ Donation Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/110th-congress/house-bill/710); [OLC opinion](https://www.justice.gov/sites/default/files/olc/opinions/2007/03/31/organtransplant.pdf)).

A serious U.S. legalization package would likely need at least four federal components: **(1)** amend § 274e, **(2)** direct HHS/HRSA to create a tightly regulated compensation framework that keeps organ allocation medical rather than financial, **(3)** create fraud-and-abuse carveouts or safe harbors for payments connected to Medicare/Medicaid patients, and **(4)** expressly preempt conflicting state prohibitions or condition participation on state conformity. Simply repealing the federal ban would leave major gaps.

State law is a real secondary hurdle. Even if federal law changed, some states would still prohibit compensated donation under their own criminal or public-health statutes unless Congress preempted them or states amended them. Clear examples include [California Penal Code § 367f](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=PEN&sectionNum=367f), [New York Public Health Law § 4307](https://www.nysenate.gov/legislation/laws/PBH/4307), and [Texas Penal Code § 48.02](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/PE/htm/PE.48.htm#48.02).

Medicare and Medicaid rules make design harder even if Congress legalizes payment. Current federal policy is built around **financial neutrality**, not compensation: HRSA’s living-donor reimbursement rule allows reimbursement of travel, lodging, meals, lost wages, child care, and elder care, but not payment for the organ itself ([42 C.F.R. § 121.14](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-42/chapter-I/subchapter-K/part-121/section-121.14); [2020 final rule](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2020/09/22/2020-20804/removing-financial-disincentives-to-living-organ-donation)). Federal transplant rules also require allocation to rest on objective medical criteria rather than ability to pay, so any legal compensation model has to sit **outside** the allocation queue ([42 C.F.R. § 121.8](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-42/chapter-I/subchapter-K/part-121/section-121.8); [HRSA allocation overview](https://www.hrsa.gov/optn/patients/organ-transplants/how-organ-allocation-works)).

On price, the evidence supports a **range**, not a precise market price. Iran’s system shows that compensation can increase supply, but its compensation structure is partly negotiated and embedded in a very different institutional setting, with persistent exploitation concerns ([Ghods & Savaj, 2006](https://cbb.uw.edu.pl/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/Ghods-Savaj-Iranian-Model.pdf); [Moeindarbari & Feizi, 2022](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/361519823_Kidneys_for_Sale_Empirical_Evidence_From_Iran/fulltext/62b663531010dc02cc5a236a/Kidneys-for-Sale-Empirical-Evidence-From-Iran.pdf); [Siraj, 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9331153/)). The leading U.S. economic estimate remains Becker and Elías’s roughly **$15,000 per kidney in 2005 dollars** as a market-clearing payment, while recent U.S. legislation such as the [End Kidney Deaths Act](https://www.govtrack.us/congress/bills/118/hr9275/text) has proposed a **$50,000 refundable tax credit spread over five years** as a pilot incentive ([Becker & Elías, 2007](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jep.21.3.3)). The most defensible policy inference is that **a U.S. pilot would probably need a mid-five-figure benefit to change behavior materially, but the evidence is too mixed to claim a single “correct” price**.

## Question

How could policymakers make compensated kidney donation legal in the United States, what federal and state legal barriers matter most, how would Medicare and Medicaid rules complicate implementation, and what do foreign systems or pilot-style evidence imply about likely payment levels?

## What Matters Most

1. **Federal legalization is indispensable.** Kidney sales cannot be made legal nationwide while [42 U.S.C. § 274e](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/42/274e) remains in force.
2. **Congress would likely need to legislate a regulated compensation system, not just repeal the ban.** Otherwise transplant centers, donors, and payers would still face allocation-rule, billing, and fraud-and-abuse uncertainty.
3. **State prohibitions would still matter unless expressly preempted.** California, New York, and Texas each separately prohibit organ sales, and other states have similar rules.
4. **Medicare/Medicaid design cannot be an afterthought.** Any compensation regime must avoid buying queue priority, avoid routine claims-based payment pathways, and address public-payer fraud-and-abuse rules.
5. **The price evidence is suggestive, not decisive.** Iran shows compensated donation can increase supply, but U.S. price estimates still range from lower five figures to recent pilot proposals around $50,000.

## Analysis

### 1. Federal Law Changes Likely Required

The main federal obstacle is straightforward: [42 U.S.C. § 274e](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/42/274e) makes it unlawful to knowingly transfer a human organ for “valuable consideration” for transplantation if interstate commerce is affected. The statute allows reimbursement for specified costs and lost wages, and it now excludes paired donation, but it does **not** allow direct compensation for a kidney. Because this is a criminal statute, the cleanest reading is that **Congress must amend it**; neither HHS nor the courts can simply declare paid kidney donation lawful.

The paired-donation history matters. The federal government resolved legal uncertainty around paired exchange through formal legal interpretation and then congressional amendment in the [Charlie W. Norwood Living Organ Donation Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/110th-congress/house-bill/710). The Office of Legal Counsel’s analysis of alternative donation practices is also useful because it shows how tightly federal lawyers read § 274e’s “valuable consideration” language ([OLC opinion](https://www.justice.gov/sites/default/files/olc/opinions/2007/03/31/organtransplant.pdf)). That precedent suggests that a kidney-compensation regime should be enacted explicitly rather than by aggressive agency interpretation.

At minimum, a federal bill would likely need to do five things:

1. **Amend NOTA** to create a narrow exception for regulated kidney-donor compensation.
2. **Direct HHS/HRSA and OPTN** to write implementing rules that preserve allocation by medical criteria rather than payment power.
3. **Specify who pays**: federal government, insurers, a central fund, or tax credits. Letting recipients negotiate directly would create the largest equity and enforcement problems.
4. **Protect transplant centers and payers** through explicit fraud-and-abuse carveouts or safe harbors.
5. **Preempt inconsistent state prohibitions** or create a state opt-in framework with clear federal supremacy inside participating states.

The best legal design is therefore not “legalize selling kidneys” in the abstract. It is something more like: **“Create a federally authorized, federally supervised kidney-donor compensation program.”**

### 2. State-Law Hurdles and Preemption Questions

Federal reform would not be enough by itself unless Congress either preempted conflicting state law or states amended their own statutes. Illustrative examples:

- [California Penal Code § 367f](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=PEN&sectionNum=367f) prohibits transferring a human organ for valuable consideration.
- [New York Public Health Law § 4307](https://www.nysenate.gov/legislation/laws/PBH/4307) bars acquiring, receiving, or transferring a human organ for valuable consideration and treats violation as a felony.
- [Texas Penal Code § 48.02](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/PE/htm/PE.48.htm#48.02) separately prohibits purchase and sale of human organs.

The broader state-law backdrop is the Uniform Anatomical Gift Act tradition: many states built organ-donation law around a noncommercial donation model. That means a federal law that merely repealed the federal ban could still leave a patchwork in which compensated donation is lawful in some states and criminal in others. For a national program, Congress would likely want one of three paths:

- **express preemption** of state bans for donations performed within the federal program;
- **conditional federal participation**, where only states that conform can participate; or
- **state opt-in waivers**, which are politically easier but would produce a patchwork market.

If the policy goal is a national kidney-supply response, express preemption is the cleaner route. If the goal is a cautious pilot, an opt-in framework may be more politically realistic.

### 3. Medicare and Medicaid Design Constraints

Current federal transplant regulation assumes that living donation should be financially neutral, not profit-generating. HRSA’s final rule on [removing financial disincentives to living organ donation](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2020/09/22/2020-20804/removing-financial-disincentives-to-living-organ-donation) and the codified rule at [42 C.F.R. § 121.14](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-42/chapter-I/subchapter-K/part-121/section-121.14) allow reimbursement of travel, lodging, meals, incidental expenses, lost wages, child care, and elder care. That framework is useful because it shows what the current system already tolerates; it is also a reminder that **compensation would require a deliberate move beyond existing federal reimbursement logic**.

A second constraint is allocation fairness. The OPTN Final Rule requires allocation policies to be based on sound medical judgment and objective, measurable medical criteria, not on wealth or bargaining power ([42 C.F.R. § 121.8](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-42/chapter-I/subchapter-K/part-121/section-121.8); [HRSA explanation](https://www.hrsa.gov/optn/patients/organ-transplants/how-organ-allocation-works)). So a legal compensation system would have to make clear that **payment buys willingness to donate, not priority access to an organ**. A system that let recipients effectively bid for kidneys would collide with the architecture of the federal transplant system.

A third constraint is Medicare participation and payment structure. Medicare-certified transplant programs operate under federal Conditions of Participation, including donor and recipient selection, informed consent, and program oversight requirements ([42 C.F.R. Part 482, Subpart E](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-42/chapter-IV/subchapter-G/part-482/subpart-E/subject-group-ECFRc4be2badf376a95/); [CMS transplant-program overview](https://www.cms.gov/medicare/health-safety-standards/certification-compliance/organ-transplant-program)). Medicare also covers kidney transplantation and donor-related hospital costs only within that regulated framework ([Medicare kidney-transplant coverage](https://www.medicare.gov/coverage/kidney-transplants)). That argues for a compensation model administered through a **separate federal benefit or tax mechanism**, not through ordinary hospital claims.

Fraud-and-abuse law is another obstacle. The federal Anti-Kickback Statute and its safe-harbor regulations are built around the idea that remuneration tied to federally reimbursable care can create abuse risk; current OIG safe harbors at [42 C.F.R. § 1001.952](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/42/1001.952) do not include a kidney-donor compensation safe harbor ([OIG safe harbor overview](https://oig.hhs.gov/compliance/safe-harbor-regulations/)). If Medicare, Medicaid, transplant centers, or plans are involved in funding compensation, Congress or HHS would likely need to add a specific protection so lawful donor payments are not treated as suspect remuneration.

Medicaid adds an intergovernmental complication. Even after federal legalization, states would still have to decide how donor compensation interacts with Medicaid eligibility, state-plan administration, and payment rules. The most administrable design would likely be to treat compensation as a **federal benefit outside routine Medicaid claims and, ideally, outside means-testing for donor eligibility purposes**. Otherwise the policy could create cliffs for low-income donors precisely in the population most likely to respond to payment.

### 4. Evidence on Compensation Levels from Other Countries and Policy Proposals

There is no large menu of foreign legal markets to benchmark against. The main real-world case is Iran’s regulated system for living unrelated kidney donation. The classic description by [Ghods and Savaj (2006)](https://cbb.uw.edu.pl/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/Ghods-Savaj-Iranian-Model.pdf) reports that Iran built a regulated compensated system and eliminated its kidney waiting list by the late 1990s. More recent work emphasizes that the compensation structure is not a clean posted-price market: donors may receive state or charity support, but actual compensation often involves bilateral negotiation and varies across local markets ([Moeindarbari & Feizi, 2022](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/361519823_Kidneys_for_Sale_Empirical_Evidence_From_Iran/fulltext/62b663531010dc02cc5a236a/Kidneys-for-Sale-Empirical-Evidence-From-Iran.pdf); [Siraj, 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9331153/)).

That makes Iran useful for one proposition and weak for another:

- **Useful proposition:** legal compensation can increase supply dramatically.
- **Weak proposition:** Iran provides a reliable dollar benchmark for a U.S. program.

For U.S. pricing, the best-known estimate remains [Becker and Elías (2007)](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jep.21.3.3), who estimated that roughly **$15,000 in 2005 dollars** could clear the kidney shortage. That is best read as an economic modeling result, not as a politically validated policy price. It depends on assumptions about donor risk, foregone wages, and the value donors place on those risks.

Recent U.S. proposals sit above that level. The [End Kidney Deaths Act](https://www.govtrack.us/congress/bills/118/hr9275/text), as introduced in the 118th Congress, would offer a refundable tax credit of **$10,000 per year for five years** to non-directed living kidney donors. That does not prove that $50,000 is the true market-clearing price; it shows the scale at which some U.S. policymakers think a pilot incentive may be politically and behaviorally meaningful.

The bottom-line pricing inference is therefore:

- **below current reimbursement-only levels** is unlikely to change behavior much;
- **around Becker-Elías levels** is the leading economic estimate for a legal market model;
- **around recent U.S. pilot proposals** may be more realistic if the instrument is a tax credit or federal benefit rather than cash-on-delivery;
- but **no source base supports high confidence in an exact price** because the United States has not run a true compensated-donation pilot for kidneys.

### 5. Counterarguments and Contradictory Evidence

The strongest argument for legalization is practical: the kidney shortage is large, persistent, and expensive. Federal sources show large waiting lists and continuing deaths while waiting, and Medicare spends heavily on ESRD and dialysis-related care ([organdonor.gov statistics](https://www.organdonor.gov/learn/organ-donation-statistics); [SRTR annual report](https://srtr.transplant.hrsa.gov/ADR/2023/2023_ADR_Full_Report.pdf); [MedPAC 2025 dialysis chapter](https://www.medpac.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/Mar25_Ch5_MedPAC_Report_To_Congress_SEC.pdf)). If compensation materially increased living donation, it could plausibly save lives and reduce public spending.

The strongest argument against legalization is that **the evidence on supply gains does not cancel the exploitation problem**. Major international and transplant-policy bodies remain opposed to commercialization, including the [World Health Organization](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/human-organ-transplantation), the [Declaration of Istanbul](https://www.asn-online.org/policy/webdocs/special%20article%20-%20the%20declaration%20of%20istanbul%20on%20organ%20trafficking.pdf), and the [National Kidney Foundation](https://www.kidney.org/news/newsroom/nkf-opposes-organ-sales). The key fear is that a legal market would recruit disproportionately from people under financial pressure and normalize commodification of the poor.

Iran is also contradictory evidence rather than a simple success story. It supports the claim that payment can increase supply, but later analyses also show donor-side bargaining, socioeconomic asymmetry, and continuing ethical concerns ([Siraj, 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9331153/); [Moeindarbari & Feizi, 2022](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/361519823_Kidneys_for_Sale_Empirical_Evidence_From_Iran/fulltext/62b663531010dc02cc5a236a/Kidneys-for-Sale-Empirical-Evidence-From-Iran.pdf)). So the strongest pro-legalization evidence and the strongest anti-legalization evidence partly come from the same country.

The most defensible synthesis is that **a tightly regulated federal compensation pilot is easier to defend than a private kidney market**. But even that narrower position is controversial.

## Claims Check

- **Strongly supported:** Congress must amend federal law before compensated kidney donation can be legal nationwide. The statutory text is clear.
- **Strongly supported:** Any reform would have to preserve allocation by medical criteria rather than wealth. That follows from the OPTN Final Rule and current transplant-program rules.
- **Supported but design-dependent:** A federal compensation system would likely need Anti-Kickback or similar fraud-and-abuse protection. This is a strong legal inference from current OIG/CMS rules, but the exact drafting solution could vary.
- **Supported but not precisely measurable:** Mid-five-figure compensation is a plausible U.S. policy range. Becker and Elías and the End Kidney Deaths Act support that as a serious benchmark, but there is no validated U.S. pilot proving a single correct price.
- **Contested:** Iran proves commercialization is a “success.” It proves supply can rise under one regulated system, but the evidence on donor welfare and exploitation is mixed.
- **Weaker / should be investigated separately:** The exact Medicaid eligibility consequences for donor compensation are likely state- and eligibility-category-specific. A program should address them explicitly, but this memo does not provide a 50-state annex.

## Recommended Follow-ups

1. Draft a **federal statutory options memo** comparing full legalization, a federally run pilot, and a tax-credit-only approach.
2. Build a **50-state legal annex** identifying which state criminal and public-health provisions would need amendment or preemption.
3. Ask health counsel to map the **exact Anti-Kickback, beneficiary-inducement, and transplant-center billing changes** needed for Medicare/Medicaid compatibility.
4. Model **behavioral response and budget effects** at several compensation levels, such as $15,000, $25,000, and $50,000.

## Decision-Oriented Conclusion

If the policy objective is to make compensated kidney donation legal in the United States, the reader should believe three things. First, **this is primarily a federal legislative problem, not a state pilot problem**: until Congress changes NOTA, kidney sales remain illegal. Second, **legalization is not just repeal**; it requires a regulated federal framework that preserves medical allocation, shields compliant actors from fraud-and-abuse risk, and resolves state-law conflicts. Third, **the most realistic first step is not an open market but a tightly supervised federal pilot or tax-credit model** with strong donor protections, explicit preemption language, and public-payer carveouts.

What to do next: if the goal is serious policy design rather than argument, start by drafting amendment language to [42 U.S.C. § 274e](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/42/274e) and pairing it with a federal pilot benefit in the **mid-five-figure range**, then test whether that narrower design survives ethical, political, and budget scrutiny. What to watch: whether Congress shows any appetite for moving beyond reimbursement-only reforms, and whether proponents can answer the exploitation critique with more than price theory.
