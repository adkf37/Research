**Bottom line for decision-makers:** the legal and governance pathway for a pilot beach segment is clearer than it was nine days ago. The highest-leverage next step is to embed a beach concept in the ongoing RFK Master Plan process—specifically in the waterfront-access and open-space workstreams—rather than pursue independent permitting. This aligns with Anacostia Riverkeeper's conditions and with the statutory 30% park/open-space requirement.

## Question

What does current evidence (updated June 2026) suggest about the legal, governance, implementation, and political feasibility of creating beach frontage on riverbanks like the Anacostia near former RFK, and what are the strongest counterarguments?

## What matters most

1. **RFK Master Plan is the governance vehicle now.** The planning process (Feb 2026–present) is the natural home for any beach concept. Independent beach permitting outside this framework risks duplication, conflict, or missed integration.

2. **Anacostia Riverkeeper's conditions define the political deal space.** Seven specific conditions (EIS, stormwater, wetland restoration, litter management, equitable access) form a checklist that, if met, secures the most important environmental stakeholder's support.

3. **Water-quality operations are closer to conditional use.** DOEE's Enterococci PCR pilot, USGS super gauge, and E. coli predictive modeling cumulatively enable a real-time monitoring framework that could support conditional swimming within 1–3 years—not the 2032 full-criteria target.

4. **The Commanders deal creates political momentum but also risk.** A billion-dollar stadium-anchored redevelopment draws attention, resources, and scrutiny. A beach amenity could be a fast, visible waterfront-access win or a sidelined lower priority, depending on sequencing.

## Analysis

### 1) RFK governance update: Master Plan process is the new institutional fact

The RFK Master Plan launched in February 2026 under DC Office of Planning leadership. Key planning elements relevant to a beach concept:

- The plan will "identify opportunities for new recreational areas and rec facilities, housing, businesses, and improved connections to the Anacostia River."
- It will "establish land uses and development programs" and "provide guidance on urban design, parks, open space, and the street network."
- Public engagement is active ("Sign up here for the mailing list to learn about upcoming events").
- The plan explicitly references waterfront access as a goal.

**Implication for beach feasibility:** The June 8 memo correctly noted that governance was "clearer than before [the 2025 transfer]" but that "river-edge design space is not unconstrained." The Master Plan process now provides a structured venue to resolve those constraints. A beach concept should be submitted as a recommendation in the parks/open-space/waterfront-access workstream, not as a standalone permit application.

### 2) Anacostia Riverkeeper's conditional endorsement: political pathway clarified

Anacostia Riverkeeper's July 2025 testimony is the most important political development for any river-adjacent amenity at RFK. ARK President Suzy Kelly stated the organization "continues to support this project" subject to seven conditions:

1. Full environmental impact statement (EIS)
2. Strict compliance with stormwater/runoff and toxic soil removal regulations
3. Gold-standard stormwater and sediment management during construction
4. Bioretention and green infrastructure sized for future rain intensity
5. Wetland restoration in Kingman Lake, accounting for 3.4 ft sea-level rise by 2080
6. Litter management: reusable containers, recycling, trash traps in open-air conveyances
7. Equitable recreational river access: trails, docks, small craft launches

Former Maryland Governor and ARK board member Parris Glendening explicitly compared the project to Baltimore's Inner Harbor stadium, signaling that ARK sees the redevelopment model as workable.

**Implication:** A beach concept that can be credibly framed as delivering on condition 7 (equitable recreational access) while not undermining conditions 1–6 has a defined political pathway. The beach does not need to overcome broad environmental opposition—it needs to satisfy a known checklist. This is a materially different political landscape from what the June 8 memo described.

### 3) Implementation: water-quality operational pathway is accelerating

The June 17 memo 1/3 (this series) documented the accelerated water-quality timeline. The implementation implications for beach feasibility:

**DC Water Clean Rivers Project:**
- 98% CSO reduction to Anacostia (operational)
- Potomac River Tunnel TBM "Emily" arrived May 2026 from Germany
- Northeast Boundary Tunnel reduces flood risk from ~50% to ~7% annual chance
- Piney Branch Tunnel: parkway section reopening June 2026

**DOEE monitoring infrastructure:**
- USGS "super gauge" on tidal Anacostia: real-time multi-parameter monitoring
- Spatial/Temporal Forecasting of Bacteria Populations: E. coli predictive modeling
- Enterococci PCR pilot: rapid 5-hour method for same-day advisories
- Microbial source tracking: human vs. non-human source identification complete
- Citizen science: Anacostia Riverkeeper and Potomac Riverkeeper Network programs

**DOEE swim ban framework:**
- Original 1971 swim ban remains in effect
- Four regulatory instruments maintain the prohibition (36 CFR 3.17, 36 CFR 7.96(e), DCMR Title 21-11 §1108.1, DCMR Title 19-10 §§1039.5/1039.10)
- Special Swim Event pathway exists: permitted exemption with sampling, contingency plans, insurance, interagency coordination
- DOEE analysis of 2017–2022 data shows swimming "is now sometimes safe in some locations" but all waterbodies still fail standards 100% of the time

**Implication for beach design:** A beach should be designed in three operational phases:
- Phase 1 (2026–2027): dry amenity beach with trail access, seating, habitat edge—no in-water contact
- Phase 2 (2027–2029): limited-contact pilot using the Special Swim Event exemption pathway, with real-time monitoring, post-rain closures, and adaptive management
- Phase 3 (2029+): conditional swimming designation if water quality criteria are consistently met

This phased approach matches DOEE's own trajectory: they describe moving "beyond the ban" and creating "new opportunities for swimming" through monitoring and conditional use rather than waiting for a single 2032 target.

### 4) Federal regulatory context: 2026 NWPs are now the operative framework

The June 8 memo flagged that "teams should not rely on stale 2021 permit assumptions." USACE 2026 Nationwide Permits took effect March 15, 2026, and remain in effect through March 15, 2031. The permitting landscape:

- For a 100-ft × 20-ft beach footprint (~0.046 acres of fill), the project may qualify under an NWP category if impacts are minimal and no threatened/endangered species or historic properties are affected.
- However, the Anacostia's contamination legacy (PCBs, PAHs, metals documented by NOAA and DOEE) and the RFK site's historic fill status make an Individual Permit more likely.
- The WOTUS regime remains fractured post-Sackett; DC applies pre-2015 regulatory scope under state authority.

**Implication:** A pre-application meeting with USACE Baltimore District is the single highest-value legal due diligence step. This was recommended in the June 8 memo and remains correct.

### 5) Political feasibility: stadium deal reconfigures the landscape

The Washington Commanders deal (announced 2025) fundamentally changes the political economy of the RFK site:

- Mayor Bowser described it as bringing the team home and "activating 180 acres of opportunity."
- The project includes a new stadium, mixed-use development, housing, retail, and recreational facilities.
- Anacostia Riverkeeper's conditional support demonstrates that even historically skeptical stakeholders see opportunity.

**Risk factors specific to a beach amenity:**
- **Sequencing risk:** Stadium and major infrastructure will dominate early construction. A beach may be deferred to later phases.
- **Design integration risk:** A standalone beach that does not connect to the broader parks/open-space plan may be suboptimal. Integration into the Anacostia Commons or riparian-area design is preferable.
- **Community trust:** EPA's Urban Waters profile (Sep 2025) explicitly names displacement fears. A beach amenity that primarily serves new residents or stadium visitors without benefiting existing Ward 7 and Ward 8 communities could generate political backlash.

### 6) Counterarguments and contradictory evidence

**Counterargument 1: "Riverkeeper support is conditional and could be withdrawn."**
True. The seven conditions are substantive. Failure on any one—particularly the EIS requirement or stormwater compliance—could flip ARK's position. A beach concept must be nested within a redevelopment that demonstrably meets all conditions.

**Counterargument 2: "The Master Plan process could reject beach concepts."**
Possible. The RFK Master Plan may prioritize habitat restoration, trail connectivity, or passive recreation over active beach amenity. Early engagement with OP and the community engagement process is essential.

**Counterargument 3: "Conditional swimming pathways are not legally assured."**
Correct. The Special Swim Event pathway exists but is designed for one-off permitted events, not ongoing recreational use. Adapting it for routine conditional swimming would require regulatory development or legislative change.

**Counterargument 4: "Phased implementation could lose political momentum."**
A risk. If Phase 1 (dry amenity) is successful, there may be less urgency to invest in Phase 2 (limited contact) and Phase 3 (conditional swimming). The beach design should build in triggers and commitments for each phase.

**Counterargument 5: "Stadium construction may preclude river access during early years."**
Likely. The RFK site will be an active construction zone for stadium and infrastructure for 3–5+ years. A beach segment may need to be located at an edge of the site that remains accessible, or deferred to a later phase.
