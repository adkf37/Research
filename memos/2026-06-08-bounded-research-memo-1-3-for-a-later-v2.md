Title: Bounded Research Memo 1/3 for a Later Synthesis — Building Beaches on Tree/Grass Riversides (Anacostia-like): Fiscal, Quantitative, and Distributional Mechanics
Date: 2026-06-08
Output type: research_memo
Source prompt: Best ways to build beaches on tree and grass filled riversides (like the Anacostia River around the former RFK stadium). How much does it cost per 100 feet of frontage, assuming 20 feet of beach from shore? Focus: evidence memo on fiscal, quantitative, and distributional mechanics.

## Executive summary (answer first)

For an **Anacostia-like urban riverside**, a practical planning range for a **100-foot frontage with a 20-foot beach bench** is:

- **Incremental sand-only placement** (if mobilization and shoreline stabilization are already covered): roughly **$2,000–$8,000 per 100 ft**.
- **Typical implementable project** (permits, design, grading, stabilization/living-shoreline features, planting, contingencies): roughly **$30,000–$150,000 per 100 ft**, with upside risk above that in constrained urban sites.

Why this spread is large: river settings near legacy industrial corridors have high variance in sediment handling, permitting, and water-quality constraints. In Anacostia conditions, the binding constraints are often **regulatory/environmental risk and O&M governance**, not raw sand volume.

Distributionally, public subsidy can be justified where beaches function as **public-access resilience infrastructure** (flood/erosion risk reduction + recreation), but equity design matters: otherwise benefits capitalize into nearby land values while costs are socialized.

## Question

What are evidence-based cost mechanics and distributional implications of constructing small beaches on vegetated riverbanks (Anacostia/RFK-type context), expressed per **100 ft frontage** and **20 ft beach width**?

## What matters most

1. **Unit economics split into two layers**
   - Sand volume and placement cost (engineering quantity)
   - Full project delivery cost (design, permits, mobilization, edge treatment, habitat, monitoring)
2. **Governance and cost share**
   - Federal/non-federal shares differ by authority and project type.
3. **Anacostia-specific constraints**
   - Water-contact restrictions and sediment contamination history can reduce immediate recreational payoff and increase compliance cost.
4. **Who benefits vs who pays**
   - Without explicit access/equity rules, value gains may concentrate in adjacent property markets.

## Analysis

### 1) Quantity baseline for the requested geometry

Given 100 ft frontage × 20 ft width = **2,000 sq ft** of plan area.
If design thickness is 1.5–3.0 ft (small beach bench range), fill is:

- Low: \( 2,000 \times 1.5 / 27 \approx 111 \) cubic yards
- High: \( 2,000 \times 3.0 / 27 \approx 222 \) cubic yards
- With practical overfill/compaction factor (about 1.1–1.3), planning volume ≈ **120–290 cubic yards**.

### 2) Sand placement cost evidence (project-derived)

USACE project examples imply broad sand placement cost bands:

- Ocean Beach (San Francisco): about **$10.85M / 440,000 cy ≈ $24.7/cy**.
- Surfside-Sunset (CA) periodic nourishment program scale: **$23.13M / 1.8M cy ≈ $12.9/cy**.

Applied to 120–290 cy gives a narrow **material-placement subtotal** around **$1.5k–$7.2k per 100 ft**.

Interpretation: this is a lower-bound component only; it excludes many costs that dominate small urban river projects.

### 3) Full delivery costs in river-edge practice

Maryland DNR reports living shoreline construction costs from roughly **$100 to >$1,000 per linear foot**, and state updates cite ranges around **$300 to >$1,500 per linear foot** depending on exposure/technique.

Converted to 100 ft frontage:

- $100/lf → **$10,000 per 100 ft**
- $300/lf → **$30,000 per 100 ft**
- $1,000/lf → **$100,000 per 100 ft**
- $1,500/lf → **$150,000 per 100 ft**

This aligns with a policy-useful central range of **$30k–$150k per 100 ft** for implementable projects in constrained conditions.

### 4) Fiscal mechanics: likely payer structure

USACE CAP Section 103 documentation shows implementation cost sharing commonly at **65% federal / 35% non-federal** for hurricane/storm damage reduction beach-erosion projects (context-dependent authority limits apply).

For a $30k–$150k per-100-ft project equivalent:

- Federal: **$19.5k–$97.5k**
- Non-federal: **$10.5k–$52.5k**

Non-federal sponsor still bears lands/easements/ROW and local delivery burdens; cash flow timing and match capacity can be more restrictive than nominal share.

### 5) Distributional mechanics (Anacostia-relevant)

- Hedonic evidence shows beach width/protection can capitalize into nearby property values in many contexts, implying risk of regressive incidence if public dollars fund improvements that are not broadly accessible.
- In the District context, water-contact use is still tightly managed; DOEE notes the long-standing 1971 ban history and event-approval framework, so near-term recreation benefits may be intermittent rather than universal.
- NOAA and NPS sources document ongoing contamination/fish-consumption concerns in the Anacostia system, increasing the case for pairing any “beach” investment with sediment and water-quality milestones.

## Contradictory evidence / counterarguments

1. **Counterargument: hard armoring may be cheaper up front.**
   In some high-energy sites, bulkheads can appear cheaper initially, but agencies (e.g., Maryland) prioritize living-shoreline approaches for ecological and process reasons; lifecycle comparisons vary by site.

2. **Counterargument: nourishment benefits are overstated.**
   Economic literature is mixed across markets and methods; some studies show strong capitalization, others weaker or context-specific effects.

3. **Counterargument: coastal benchmarks do not transfer to rivers.**
   True; this memo uses coastal unit evidence only as a bounding input, then shifts to living-shoreline linear-foot ranges more relevant to estuarine/river edges.

## Claims check (weak or contested items)

- **Most uncertain claim:** direct transferability of coastal $/cy to Anacostia micro-sites.
  Status: used only as lower-bound arithmetic, not as total-cost estimate.
- **Potentially contested:** degree of nearby property-value capture in this specific corridor.
  Status: treated as directional risk, not point estimate.
- **Not yet pinned down in this memo:** exact permitting sequence and sediment testing protocol for a specific RFK-adjacent parcel footprint.
