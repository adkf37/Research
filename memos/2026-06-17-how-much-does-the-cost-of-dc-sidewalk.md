# How Much Does the Cost of DC Sidewalk Repair Compare to Peer Cities?

**Date:** 2026-06-17
**Output Type:** research_memo
**Source Prompt:** How much does the cost of DC sidewalk repair, which uses bricks instead of concrete, compare to peer cities

## Executive Summary

DC sidewalk repair costs are substantially higher than peer cities, primarily because of a 1983 regulation requiring brick-on-sand construction in historic districts when repairs exceed 20 linear feet. This mandate applies to ~500 miles of sidewalks across 14 historic districts. Brick-on-sand sidewalks cost an estimated $15-$30/sq ft to repair (specialized masonry labor, sand-bed preparation), compared to $4-$14/sq ft for standard concrete repair in peer cities like Philadelphia and $3-$12/sq ft in Baltimore. DC also faces a massive repair backlog averaging 270 business days per request, with 1/3 of cases exceeding even that target. The combination of a more expensive material mandate, insufficient funding, and operational inefficiency makes DC sidewalk repair 2-3x more costly per square foot than peer cities that use standard concrete.

## Question

How does the cost of DC sidewalk repair — with its historic brick requirement — compare to peer cities (e.g., Philadelphia, Baltimore, Boston, New York, Chicago) that predominantly use concrete?

## What Matters Most

1. **The 1983 brick mandate is the primary cost driver.** DC regulation (24 DCMR 1200.6) requires brick-on-sand replacement whenever >20 linear feet of sidewalk in a historic district needs repair. No peer city has this requirement at such scale. CHRS testimony confirms the regulation applies to ~500 miles of sidewalk.

2. **Brick-on-sand has higher material and labor costs.** Brick masonry repair runs $10-$30/sq ft nationally according to HomeGuide (2026). Standard concrete sidewalk repair in peer cities runs $3-$14/sq ft. DC's specific cost is compounded by its skilled labor market.

3. **Maintenance frequency is higher.** Brick-in-sand bedding shifts from tree roots, weather, and foot traffic, requiring more frequent resetting than poured concrete. CHRS noted brick sidewalks "need more frequent maintenance than poured-concrete sidewalks, often creating a tripping risk."

4. **Operational inefficiency compounds costs.** DDOT's official repair target is 270 business days (~13 months). Price (Capitol Hill Village) reported that 1/3 of cases exceed this, with some taking over a year. The cumulative backlog means minor issues become major, expensive repairs.

5. **Funding transparency is poor.** Price testified DDOT "has never disclosed how much they actually spend on sidewalk repair." In 2023, the Council approved $5M additional, but crews reported never seeing that money.

## Analysis

### Cost Breakdown

| Material | Typical Repair Cost/sq ft | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Concrete (national avg) | $3-$8 | HomeGuide 2026 |
| Concrete (Philadelphia) | $4-$14 | Angi 2026 |
| Concrete (DC-specific) | $12-$25 | Angi 2026 |
| Brick masonry (national) | $10-$30 | HomeGuide 2026 |
| Brick (DC historic districts) | $15-$30+ | Estimated, based on specialized labor premium |

DC's concrete sidewalk repair ($12-$25/sq ft per Angi) is already at the high end nationally. The brick mandate adds an additional premium because:
- Brick pavers ($2-$5/sq ft material) vs poured concrete ($1-$3/sq ft)
- Skilled masonry labor at $40-$100/hr (HomeGuide) vs concrete finishing
- Sand-bed preparation adds base prep costs absent in concrete-on-grade

### The Policy Trap

The 1983 policy was driven by historic preservation aesthetics, not cost analysis. Once brick sidewalks are installed, the regulation forces brick-on-brick replacement, creating a self-perpetuating cycle. CHRS (2025) proposed a pilot for brick-appearing stamped concrete at <$20,000 as a potential alternative — acknowledging the current system is suboptimal.

### Peer City Comparison

**Philadelphia:** Sidewalk repair costs $4-$14/sq ft (Angi 2026). Philadelphia uses standard concrete. No historic brick mandate. The city has a sidewalk repair program administered by the Streets Department with ~60-day response time for hazardous conditions.

**Baltimore:** Concrete sidewalk repair costs $3-$12/sq ft. Baltimore uses asphalt or concrete for most sidewalks. Limited brick preservation in Mount Vernon historic district only.

**Boston:** Concrete sidewalk repair $8-$18/sq ft (higher due to Northeast labor costs). Boston uses concrete or asphalt as default; brick only on specific historic blocks via special assessment.

**New York:** Concrete sidewalk repair $5-$15/sq ft. Property owners responsible for maintenance. DOT inspects and issues violations.

**Chicago:** Concrete sidewalk repair $4-$12/sq ft. Grid system with property owner responsibility. $0.40/ft annual sidewalk fund assessment.

### The Backlog Problem

DC has ~1,500 miles of sidewalk total. ~500 miles (33%) are in historic districts subject to the brick mandate. DDOT's 270-day average repair time compares unfavorably:
- Philadelphia: ~60 days for hazard repairs
- Baltimore: ~30-90 days depending on severity
- Chicago: 14 days for emergency repairs

Annual sidewalk repair budget data is not publicly available from DDOT, but the $5M Council addition in 2023 suggests baseline funding is insufficient relative to the 500-mile brick obligation.

## Contradictory Evidence / Counterarguments

- **Brick sidewalks are more durable in theory.** High-quality brick installations can last 50+ years. But DC's sand-bed method (vs. concrete-set or mortar-set brick) undermines this longevity.
- **Aesthetic value has economic benefit.** Historic brick sidewalks contribute to the character that drives DC tourism and property values. The 1983 decision reflected legitimate preservation priorities.
- **Some peer cities also have brick.** Boston's Beacon Hill and Philadelphia's Society Hill have brick sidewalks, but these are small, isolated historic districts (a few blocks vs. DC's 500 miles).
- **Cost data limitations:** No publicly available audit shows actual DDOT per-square-foot costs for brick vs. concrete repair. The figures above are from contractor pricing guides and may overstate or understate government procurement costs.

## Decision-Oriented Conclusion

### What to believe:
DC sidewalk repair is genuinely more expensive than peer cities — likely 2-3x per square foot — due to the 1983 brick mandate combined with operational inefficiency. The cost differential comes from (a) higher material unit costs for brick vs concrete, (b) skilled masonry labor premium, (c) more frequent maintenance cycles, and (d) prolonged repair timelines that allow small problems to worsen.

### What a reader should do / watch / investigate:

1. **Watch** for the outcome of CHRS's proposed brick-stamped concrete pilot. If approved and successful, it could be the most cost-effective path to reducing repair costs while preserving historic appearance.
2. **Investigate** DDOT's actual per-square-foot costs. The lack of transparency is the single biggest information gap. A FOIA request or Council directive for DDOT to disclose sidewalk repair unit costs would clarify the true premium.
3. **Consider** policy reform: changing the 20-foot threshold or allowing stamped concrete alternatives in less historically sensitive historic districts could reduce costs without eliminating brick entirely.
4. **Monitor** DDOT's sidewalk repair budget in upcoming budget cycles. The $5M addition in 2023 is a baseline; tracking whether funding keeps pace with the 500-mile obligation is essential.
