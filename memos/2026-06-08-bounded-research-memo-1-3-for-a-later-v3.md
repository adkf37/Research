Title: Bounded Research Memo 1/3 (v3): Building Beaches on Tree/Grass-Filled Riversides (Anacostia-style) — Fiscal, Quantitative, and Distributional Mechanics
Date: 2026-06-08
Output type: research_memo
Source prompt: Best ways to build beaches on tree and grass filled riversides (like the Anacostia River around the former RFK stadium). How much does it cost per 100 feet of frontage, assuming 20 feet of beach from shore? Focus on fiscal, quantitative, and distributional mechanics.

## Executive summary (answer first)

For a **100-foot frontage** and **20-foot beach depth** (2,000 sq ft), a practical planning range is:

- **$40,000–$200,000 total project cost per 100 ft** for a sheltered urban river “living shoreline + beach” concept in predesign conditions.
- A narrower “likely” band for concept budgeting is **~$60,000–$150,000 per 100 ft**, with large upside risk if contaminated sediment handling, utility conflicts, or complex access are triggered.

Why: primary public-sector references show (a) living-shoreline construction often priced by linear foot in the **hundreds to low-thousands** and (b) sand-placement unit prices can look low per cubic yard in large coastal contracts but are not representative of fully-loaded urban riverfront builds. Distributionally, Anacostia-adjacent communities are explicitly identified by EPA as high-need and displacement-sensitive, so who pays and who benefits is as important as engineering unit cost.

## Question

What does it cost to create beach-like shoreline frontage on a vegetated urban river edge (Anacostia-like context), expressed per **100 feet of frontage** with **20 feet cross-shore beach width**, and how should fiscal burden and benefits be distributed?

## What matters most

1. **Geometry is small; mobilization/permitting are not.**
2. **Linear-foot benchmarks (living shorelines) are more decision-useful** than pure sand-volume benchmarks for urban sites.
3. **Cost-sharing structure matters** (federal/local split, O&M burden).
4. **Equity risk is real**: improvements can coincide with displacement pressures unless anti-displacement tools are funded in parallel.

## Analysis

### 1) Converting the prompt geometry into costing units

- Frontage = 100 ft
- Beach width = 20 ft
- Surface area = 2,000 sq ft
- If average placed sand thickness ≈ 2 ft, fill volume is about 4,000 cu ft ≈ **148 cubic yards**.

This sand-only quantity is small enough that project overhead, access constraints, shoreline stabilization features, and compliance dominate cost.

### 2) Evidence anchors for unit costs

- Maryland DNR public guidance: living shoreline construction can run **$100 to >$1,000 per linear foot** (site dependent).
- Maryland DNR (May 22, 2026): “significant challenge” cost range **$300 to >$1,500 per linear foot**, and prior upfront match requirements (50–80%) were a barrier; law change reduced this burden.
- USACE coastal nourishment examples show wide sand unit variation:
  - LA District (Dec 2023): **$23.13M** for ~**1.1M cu yd** placed in Stage 13 (plus backpassing) → roughly **$21/cy** order of magnitude for that stage.
  - Philadelphia District (Dec 2024): **$73.5M** for **2.1M cu yd** → about **$35/cy**.

Interpretation: cubic-yard prices from large ocean contracts are useful lower-bound signals for raw sand placement, but not full urban-river project totals.

### 3) Derived planning range per 100 feet

Using linear-foot methods (better fit for this use case):

- **Low case:** $300/lf × 100 ft = **$30,000** construction
- **High case:** $1,500/lf × 100 ft = **$150,000** construction

Apply a fully-loaded uplift for design, permitting, construction management, contingency, and early monitoring.
USACE Anacostia watershed restoration report shows total first costs materially above bare construction (project first cost $34.106M vs construction $26.198M, plus separate OMRR&R).

Practical fully loaded band:

- **~$40,000–$200,000 per 100 ft**
- Working midpoint envelope: **~$60,000–$150,000 per 100 ft**

### 4) Fiscal mechanics and distributional mechanics

- USACE-style ecosystem projects commonly use **federal/non-federal split** (example in Anacostia watershed restoration: **65/35**), with local partner responsibilities on land/real-estate and ongoing O&M.
- EPA’s Urban Waters Anacostia profile states the river runs beside communities with some of the region’s **lowest employment, highest poverty, poorest health indicators**, and notes fear of displacement as restoration improves conditions.
- Therefore, a fiscally “correct” package is not only capex for shoreline works; it should include:
  - affordability protections (tax relief, anti-displacement funds, targeted homeowner support),
  - local hiring/workforce set-asides,
  - transparent O&M funding so amenities do not degrade in lower-income riverfront segments.

## Contradictory evidence / counterarguments

- Counterargument: nourishment can be cheap on a $/cy basis (large USACE projects), implying very low per-100-ft costs.
- Response: true for bulk sand movement in large contracts, but weak for small urban riverfront projects where permitting, access, utility conflicts, ecological constraints, and public-space finishes dominate.
