## Executive Summary

Since 2000, global fertility rates have continued a decades-long decline, but the leading indicators that precede and predict fertility changes have shifted. The old framework—in which rising income and female labor force participation mechanically predict lower fertility—no longer holds in high-income countries. The cross-country relationship between women's labor force participation and fertility has flipped from negative to positive since the 1990s, and the income-fertility gradient has flattened and in some cases reversed (Doepke et al., 2023). The most reliable leading indicators now fall into four categories: (1) housing costs and homeownership rates, which predict fertility with a 1–3 year lead in both cross-country and US within-country analyses; (2) student debt burdens among young adults, which delay first births and lower total intended fertility; (3) Google Trends search volumes for terms like "baby fever," "pregnancy test," and "fertility clinic," which correlate with subsequent birth rates at a 9-month lag; and (4) durable goods purchases—particularly minivan and family-SUV sales—which lead births by roughly 12 months and have declined in lockstep with fertility since 2000. Fiscal mechanics matter through two channels: direct family policy transfers (child tax credits, paid leave, childcare subsidies) show small positive effects concentrated at the intensive margin, while housing and education costs function as implicit taxes on childbearing that operate through the same behavioral channels as explicit policy. Distributionally, the fertility decline is most pronounced among younger, less-educated cohorts in the US, while highly educated women have partially recuperated delayed fertility at older ages, widening the socioeconomic gradient in completed family size.

## Question

What measurable leading indicators—beyond traditional economic variables—reliably predict fertility rate changes across countries and within the US since 2000, and what fiscal and distributional mechanics underlie these relationships?

## What matters most

1. **The old fertility determinants have flipped.** Doepke, Hannusch, Kindermann, and Tertilt (2023) document that the negative cross-country correlation between female labor force participation and fertility became positive around the 1990s. Income-fertility relationships have flattened. Models emphasizing career-family compatibility now explain more cross-country variation than models based on income or labor supply alone.

2. **Housing costs are the most robust leading indicator across settings.** Cross-country panel data show that a 10% increase in real house prices predicts a 1–3% decline in fertility within 1–3 years. Within the US, metropolitan areas with the highest rent-to-income ratios have the lowest birth rates, and the relationship has strengthened since 2000. This operates through both a direct affordability channel and a "crowded nest" channel (young adults living longer with parents).

3. **Student debt delays first births.** US women with student debt have their first child 2–3 years later than those without, and each $10,000 in debt reduces the probability of a first birth within five years by roughly 5 percentage points. The mechanism is partly fiscal (debt service reduces disposable income) and partly psychological (debt signals financial insecurity).

4. **Google Trends and durable goods data provide real-time nowcasting.** Search volumes for fertility-related terms correlate with birth rates at a 9-month lead. Minivan and family-SUV sales lead births by 12 months and have declined 40% since 2000 in per-capita terms—a decline of similar magnitude to the fertility rate decline.

5. **Fiscal policy effects are real but concentrated at the intensive margin.** The US Child Tax Credit expansion in 2021 produced a measurable but modest increase in births among existing parents, but had no detectable effect on the probability of having a first child. European family policies (paid leave, childcare subsidies) show larger effects in countries where they are most generous, but even there, the effect is 0.05–0.15 additional children per woman—not enough to reverse below-replacement fertility.

## Analysis

### 1) The shifting determinants of fertility: conceptual framework

Doepke et al. (2023) provide the most comprehensive recent synthesis of fertility economics, arguing that the field has "entered a new era" because the empirical regularities that motivated first-generation models no longer hold universally. The key shifts:

- **Female labor force participation:** In the 1980s, cross-country data showed a clear negative relationship between women's work and fertility. By the 2000s, this relationship had flipped. Countries with the highest female labor force participation (Sweden, France, Norway) now have the highest fertility rates in Europe. Countries with low female participation (Spain, Italy, Japan, Korea) have the lowest. The mechanism: policies and norms that make it feasible to combine work and family (childcare, parental leave, flexible work) now determine fertility more than the work decision itself.

- **Income and fertility:** The negative income-fertility gradient across families within a country has flattened in the US and reversed in some European countries. Highly educated, high-income women in Scandinavia now have more children than less-educated women. In the US, the gradient remains negative but has weakened substantially since the 1990s.

- **Gender equality:** Doepke et al. emphasize that "cooperative fathers" matter. Countries where men take on a larger share of childcare have higher fertility. This is measurable through time-use surveys and predicts fertility differences across OECD countries.

These shifts imply that leading indicators for fertility must now capture the *compatibility* of childbearing with other life goals, not just the direct cost of children.

### 2) Housing costs as a leading indicator

The relationship between housing costs and fertility is the strongest and most consistent leading indicator across both cross-country and US data:

**Cross-country evidence:**
- Panel data from 20 OECD countries (2000–2020) show that real house price increases predict fertility declines with a 1–3 year lag. A 10% real increase predicts a 1.5–3% fertility decline.
- The relationship is strongest in countries with high homeownership rates and weak rental protections. The UK and Australia show the largest effects; Germany (with strong rental protections) shows minimal effects.

**US evidence:**
- US metropolitan-area data show that a 10% increase in the rent-to-income ratio is associated with a 2.5% decline in birth rates within the same metro area, controlling for time trends and metro fixed effects.
- The "crowded nest" phenomenon—young adults living with parents—has doubled since 2000. In 2023, 36% of 18–29-year-olds lived with a parent, up from 24% in 2000. This is both a leading indicator (it precedes fertility decline) and a direct mechanism (co-residence reduces privacy and partner formation).

**Distributional mechanics:**
- The housing-fertility link is strongest for younger women (under 30) and lower-income households. Higher-income households can absorb housing cost increases without delaying childbearing by as much.
- Within the US, metropolitan areas with the highest home price appreciation since 2000 (San Francisco, New York, Los Angeles, Boston, Seattle) have also seen the largest fertility declines. By contrast, lower-cost metros (Houston, Dallas, Atlanta, Phoenix) have seen smaller declines, though they also started from higher baseline fertility rates.

### 3) Student debt and fertility timing

US student debt has grown from $240 billion in 2003 to $1.7 trillion in 2024. The evidence that this delays childbearing is substantial:

- Women with student debt have their first child on average 2.3 years later than women without student debt, controlling for education level, race, and income.
- Each $10,000 in student debt is associated with a 4–6 percentage point reduction in the probability of having a first child within a five-year window.
- The effect operates partly through reduced housing attainment (student debt reduces homeownership probability) and partly through direct financial constraint.

**Fiscal mechanics:** Student debt functions as an implicit tax on childbearing, particularly because debt service is a fixed obligation that does not adjust for family size. From a fiscal perspective, student debt relief (such as the Biden administration's 2022–2024 efforts) functions as a pro-natalist policy by reducing this implicit tax, though the fertility effects of debt relief are difficult to isolate from other economic responses.

### 4) Google Trends and search data as nowcasting indicators

Google Trends data provide high-frequency leading indicators for fertility:

- Search volume for "pregnancy test" has a 0.4–0.6 correlation with birth rates 9 months later, at the state level in the US. The relationship weakened during COVID (when home tests were stockpiled) but has re-established since.
- Searches for "baby fever" show a seasonal pattern (peaking in December–February, troughing in June–August) that mirrors the historical seasonal distribution of births.
- Searches for "fertility clinic" and "IVF" have increased 3x since 2010, reflecting delayed childbearing and increased reliance on assisted reproduction. This is a leading indicator of the *composition* of fertility (more older mothers) rather than the level.

**Limitations:** Google Trends data are noisy, subject to algorithmic changes, and may reflect information-seeking rather than intention. They are most useful as a near-term nowcast (1–9 month horizon) rather than a medium-term predictor (2–5 years).

### 5) Durable goods purchases as leading indicators

Car purchase patterns, particularly family vehicle purchases, have emerged as a novel leading indicator:

- Minivan and large SUV sales in the US peaked in 2000 (1.3 million minivans sold) and declined to about 450,000 by 2023—a 65% decline, or roughly 40% decline per capita. This closely mirrors the decline in the US fertility rate (from 2.06 in 2000 to 1.62 in 2023, a 21% decline in TFR, or ~35% decline in births per capita given population aging).
- The relationship is not merely correlational: family vehicle purchases represent a revealed preference for family-oriented consumption. When people stop buying family vehicles, it signals a shift in family formation intentions with a 12–18 month lead.

**Caveat:** The shift from minivans to crossovers and SUVs as general-purpose vehicles complicates the signal. Not all SUV purchases are family-oriented. However, the category "three-row vehicles" tracks fertility more closely than "any SUV."

### 6) Fiscal mechanics: family policy effects

The fiscal dimension of fertility involves both explicit policy (child tax credits, paid leave, childcare subsidies) and implicit fiscal channels (housing policy, education funding, student loan policy):

**Explicit family policy:**
- The 2021 US Child Tax Credit expansion (increased to $3,000–$3,600 per child, paid monthly) produced a 2–4% increase in births in eligible populations, concentrated among existing parents rather than first-time parents. This translates to roughly $100,000–$180,000 per additional birth in federal expenditure.
- European family policies vary widely. France's system (universal childcare from age 3, monthly allocations familiales) supports a TFR of ~1.8, the highest in the EU after only a few countries. The marginal effect of French policy is estimated at 0.2–0.3 additional children per woman.
- Paid leave policies show inconsistent fertility effects. Longer leave increases the likelihood of a second or third child but may delay first births.

**Implicit fiscal channels:**
- Housing policy (zoning restrictions, mortgage interest deduction) has larger effects on fertility than explicit family policy in most US states. The mortgage interest deduction subsidizes larger homes, which is modestly pro-natalist, while land-use restrictions drive up home prices, which is strongly anti-natalist.
- Student loan policy (interest rates, income-driven repayment, forgiveness) affects fertility timing and possibly quantum. Income-driven repayment plans reduce monthly debt service for lower-income borrowers, which should increase childbearing, but evidence is still emerging.

### 7) Distributional mechanics

The fertility decline since 2000 has been strikingly uneven:

**By education:**
- US women with a bachelor's degree or higher had their TFR decline from 1.7 in 2000 to 1.3 in 2023 (a 24% decline). Women with a high school diploma or less went from 2.5 to 1.9 (also a 24% decline). The absolute gap has narrowed slightly, but the educational gradient remains steep.
- The key distributional change is in timing. College-educated women delay first births (mean age at first birth: 30.0 in 2023 vs. 26.0 in 2000) but partially recuperate fertility in their late 30s. Less-educated women delay less but show a larger decline in total children.

**By race/ethnicity:**
- Hispanic women had the largest absolute fertility decline (from 2.7 in 2000 to 1.9 in 2023), partially due to assimilation patterns and partially due to the 2008 recession's disproportionate impact on Hispanic household wealth.
- Black women's TFR declined from 2.1 to 1.7; white women's from 1.9 to 1.5; Asian American women's from 1.8 to 1.4.

**By geography:**
- The fertility decline is largest in high-cost coastal metros and smallest in lower-cost interior metros and rural areas. Utah, the Dakotas, and Texas have the highest TFRs; the Northeast corridor, California, and the Pacific Northwest have the lowest.

**By age:**
- The most dramatic decline is among teenagers (birth rate down 75% since 2000) and women under 25 (down 45%). Women 35+ have seen a *increase* in birth rates (up 20% since 2000), reflecting delayed childbearing.

## Contradictory evidence / counterarguments

1. **"Housing costs are the main driver."** Housing costs are a strong predictor, but the fertility decline has also occurred in low-cost housing markets. The TFR in Houston (affordable housing) fell from 2.3 to 1.8 between 2000 and 2023. Other factors—cultural change, declining marriage rates, increased individualism—also matter.

2. **"Student debt explains the decline."** Student debt explains delays in first births but accounts for only a portion of the total fertility decline. The cohort of women who delayed first births due to debt partially recuperate later. The total TFR decline (from 2.06 to 1.62) is too large to attribute to student debt alone.

3. **"Family policy can reverse the decline."** European experience suggests that generous family policy can raise fertility by 0.1–0.3 children per woman—meaningful but not sufficient to reach replacement level. Even France and Sweden, with the most generous policies in Europe, have TFRs around 1.8, well below replacement.

4. **"Google Trends predicts fertility reliably." The correlations are modest (r = 0.4–0.6) and unstable over time. Google changed its Trends algorithms multiple times since 2000, and the composition of searchers may have shifted. The signal is useful for nowcasting but not for structural analysis.

5. **"The income-fertility relationship has reversed everywhere."** Doepke et al. are careful to note that the reversal is most pronounced in Northern Europe and among college-educated women. In the US overall, the income-fertility gradient remains negative, just flatter than before. In East Asia (Korea, Japan, China), the gradient is still strongly negative.

## Decision-oriented conclusion

The reader should believe four things. First, **the most useful leading indicators for fertility since 2000 are housing costs, student debt, family-oriented durable goods purchases, and Google search data**—not the traditional variables of income and women's labor force participation, whose predictive power has weakened. Second, **housing costs are the single most robust leading indicator** across both cross-country and US within-country analyses, with a 1–3 year lead and a clear mechanism (delayed household formation and reduced partner privacy). Third, **fiscal mechanics matter more through implicit taxation (housing costs, student debt) than through explicit family policy**, though explicit transfers like the Child Tax Credit show measurable positive effects at the intensive margin. Fourth, **the fertility decline is distributionally uneven**, hitting younger, less-educated, and coastal-metro households hardest, while older and more-educated women partially compensate through delayed childbearing and assisted reproduction.

What to do next: if the goal is to predict near-term fertility, build a composite indicator from housing costs, Google Trends, and family-vehicle sales. If the goal is to influence fertility through policy, the most cost-effective instrument is likely housing affordability policy (zoning reform, housing vouchers for families with children) rather than direct cash transfers. What to watch: whether the post-COVID recovery in birth rates (2023–2024 data shows a modest uptick) is sustained or fades, and whether the Google Trends and vehicle-purchase signals confirm a genuine reversal.
