## 1. Question and Framing

Latin America and large parts of sub-Saharan Africa together host the majority of the world's most violent societies. According to the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC) *Global Study on Homicide 2023*, the Americas recorded **15.0 intentional homicides per 100,000 population** in 2021, the highest regional rate on earth. The Igarapé Institute's Homicide Monitor places the Latin America and Caribbean subregional rate even higher at **19.6 per 100,000 in 2023** — approximately 3.5 times the global average of 5.6. Africa recorded roughly **12.7 per 100,000** in the same period, with particularly severe pockets in parts of southern, central, and West Africa.

This brief examines: *What structural, institutional, and social factors drive these elevated rates?* And: *Can police deployment strategies meaningfully reduce them — or does the illegitimacy of police institutions need to be addressed first?*

The question has sharply practical implications. Governments and international donors are under pressure to show results quickly, which favours visible deployment — more officers on the street, more checkpoints, sometimes military-style operations. The evidence base, however, suggests that in contexts of deep institutional distrust, additional police presence can paradoxically reinforce cycles of violence if the underlying legitimacy deficit is not addressed. This is not a purely theoretical concern: survey data from Latinobarómetro (2023) and Afrobarometer (2021–2023) reveal that large majorities of citizens in both regions regard police as corrupt, abusive, or ineffective — a finding that fundamentally shapes how policing interventions land.

---

## 2. Key Findings

1. **The homicide crisis is concentrated but not random.** Latin America and Africa together account for the vast majority of the world's intentional killings, but the pattern is highly heterogeneous: a subset of countries, cities, and even specific neighbourhoods account for a disproportionate share of violence. Understanding this concentration is critical for targeted intervention.

2. **Organized crime and drug markets are the proximate drivers in Latin America.** Up to 50% of homicides in the Americas are linked to organized crime, gang competition for drug routes, and territorial disputes. Drug trafficking does not mechanically generate violence everywhere, but contested market control — particularly in the absence of a dominant criminal actor — sharply raises lethality.

3. **In Africa, the causal mix is more heterogeneous**, spanning conflict-related killing, inter-communal violence, criminal networks, and urban gang activity. State fragility is the common thread: where formal institutions cannot impose order, multiple violent actors fill the vacuum.

4. **Impunity is both a cause and a consequence of high homicide.** Roughly only one in four homicides in Latin America results in a conviction. This failure of justice is not peripheral — it removes deterrence, signals impunity to perpetrators, and signals abandonment to communities.

5. **Evidence-based police deployment strategies — hot spots policing, focused deterrence, and optimised patrol allocation — demonstrably reduce crime.** These strategies have strong empirical backing from randomised and quasi-experimental studies across multiple countries.

6. **However, deployment strategies work substantially better when police are perceived as legitimate.** In contexts of deep institutional distrust, militarised or indiscriminate police operations can increase violence, undermine cooperation, and accelerate informal justice systems. Procedural justice — perceived fairness in police-citizen encounters — is both intrinsically valuable and instrumentally effective.

7. **The sequencing debate is not binary.** Legitimacy and effective deployment are not either/or choices. The most successful interventions in the evidence base tend to combine targeted operational strategies with reforms in police conduct, accountability, and community engagement simultaneously, rather than treating them as sequential stages.

8. **El Salvador's Bukele model offers a cautionary case.** An extreme mano dura (iron-fist) crackdown achieved dramatic short-term homicide reductions (from over 100 to under 2.5 per 100,000 between 2015 and 2024), but at significant costs to civil liberties, due process, and the rule of law — and the model has not been successfully replicated elsewhere.

## 3. Detailed Analysis

### 3.1 The Scale of the Problem: Global Context and Regional Rates

The UNODC *Global Study on Homicide 2023* found approximately **458,000 intentional homicides globally in 2021** — dwarfing the combined death toll from inter-state wars in the same period. Despite this scale, the problem is radically geographically concentrated:

- The **Americas** (predominantly Latin America and the Caribbean) have the world's highest homicide *rate* — around 15–20 per 100,000 depending on subregion and data source.
- **Africa** has the highest absolute number of homicides globally, with a rate of approximately 12.7 per 100,000, but this aggregate masks enormous variation: southern Africa (especially South Africa, at ~35 per 100,000) and parts of Central Africa are far more violent than North or East Africa.
- **Asia and Europe** average below 3 and 3 per 100,000 respectively, illustrating the magnitude of the regional gap.

Within Latin America, individual country rates reveal further concentration:
- **Jamaica**: ~44 per 100,000 (2023 estimates)
- **Honduras**: ~35 per 100,000
- **Venezuela**: ~35–40 per 100,000
- **Mexico, Colombia, Brazil**: 15–30 per 100,000 with massive intra-country variation
- **El Salvador**: fell from 103 per 100,000 (2015) to approximately 2.4 (2024) — an extreme outlier now examined in detail below

Within Africa:
- **South Africa**: ~35 per 100,000
- **Nigeria**: significant variation; Lagos and other urban centres disproportionately affected
- **DRC**: conflict-related violence drives rates that may exceed 30 per 100,000 in some zones
- **Kenya, Ghana**: substantially lower, in the 5–8 range

This concentration suggests that generalised national-level responses are insufficient; the most effective interventions need to be geographically and socially targeted.

---

### 3.2 Root Causes in Latin America

Research identifies several interlocking causal factors, none of which is individually sufficient to explain the regional pattern:

#### Organized Crime and Drug Markets

The strongest proximate correlate of Latin American homicide is organized crime activity, especially where criminal groups compete violently for territorial control of drug routes, markets, and extortion rackets. A 2024 IMF working paper (*Violent Crime and Insecurity in Latin America and the Caribbean: A Macroeconomic Perspective*) identified criminal market competition as the single most consistent predictor of homicide variation across countries and time periods. The key mechanism is *market instability*: where a dominant cartel or gang holds uncontested control, violence often falls (an equilibrium enforced by the criminal actor itself); when that control is challenged — by rival groups, state crackdowns, or leadership arrests — homicide spikes.

This dynamic explains paradoxes like Mexico's experience with "kingpin strategies" (targeting cartel leaders), which repeatedly fragmented criminal organisations and produced homicide surges rather than reductions.

#### Inequality, Urban Marginalisation, and Structural Exclusion

High Gini coefficients — Latin America remains the world's most unequal region — create conditions for criminal recruitment among young men with limited formal economic opportunities. Rapid and under-serviced urbanisation has produced extensive informal settlements (favelas, colonias populares, villas miseria) where state services are thin, employment precarious, and criminal economies offer alternative pathways to income and status. The World Bank and Inter-American Development Bank (IDB) have consistently identified economic inclusion as a medium-term moderator of violence, even while acknowledging it cannot explain short-term variation.

#### Firearm Proliferation

The Americas account for a disproportionate share of global firearm-related deaths. Illegal weapons, often originating from the United States or diverted from military stocks, are widely available across Central America, the Caribbean, and parts of South America. The Igarapé Institute estimates that over 70% of Latin American homicides involve firearms — one of the highest rates globally.

#### Institutional Weakness and Corruption

A major 2023 study in the *British Journal of Criminology* (institutional perspective on Latin American homicide) found that in Latin America — unlike other regions — the quality and legitimacy of state institutions explained more of the cross-country variation in homicide rates than structural factors like poverty or inequality alone. Countries with higher government effectiveness scores and lower corruption indices consistently had lower homicide rates, even controlling for income and urbanisation. This finding positions police and judicial corruption not as a marginal factor but as a central mechanism.

### 3.3 Root Causes in Africa

Africa's homicide crisis shares some drivers with Latin America but has distinct features:

#### Conflict and Political Violence

A significant proportion of African homicides — particularly in the Sahel, Great Lakes region, and Horn of Africa — are conflict-related. The Africa Center for Strategic Studies documented in its 2024 security trends report that militant Islamist activity produced record-high casualties in parts of the Sahel. Military juntas and allied militias were responsible for more civilian deaths in some countries than insurgent groups, illustrating the collapse of the state protection function.

#### Organised Crime and Illicit Economies

The ENACT Africa Organised Crime Index (2023) found that criminality scores across Africa have risen steadily, while state resilience has not kept pace. Human trafficking, cocaine transit (particularly through West Africa as a transshipment hub for South American product), and financial crime are the dominant growth markets. These economies intersect violently wherever competing networks contest control.

#### State Fragility as the Overarching Framework

The most consistent correlate across Africa is state fragility — as measured by the Fragile States Index (Fund for Peace) and related indices. Fragile states lack the institutional capacity to prevent, investigate, and prosecute violence; this absence creates permissive conditions for criminal and political actors. Critically, fragility also means that police forces — where they exist — are often underpaid, poorly trained, and incentivised by corruption rather than public service.

#### Urbanisation and Youth Demographics

Africa has the world's fastest-growing and youngest urban population. Cities like Lagos, Kinshasa, Nairobi, and Johannesburg have expanded faster than formal employment and services can accommodate. The result — large populations of young men with limited formal opportunities in poorly-serviced peri-urban areas — closely mirrors the Latin American pattern, and criminal recruitment follows similar pathways.

---

### 3.4 The Impunity Trap: When Justice Systems Fail

One of the most underappreciated drivers of sustained high homicide rates is the near-total failure of criminal justice systems to solve, prosecute, and convict perpetrators. UNODC data indicate that **approximately 76% of homicides in Latin America do not result in a conviction** — a figure some researchers describe as a "culture of impunity" that structurally removes deterrence.

The mechanisms are well-documented:
- **Weak forensic capacity**: Many police forces lack the trained personnel and equipment to process crime scenes systematically, leaving most homicides effectively uninvestigated.
- **Witness intimidation**: In contexts of gang or organised crime dominance, witnesses face lethal risks from testifying, depressing cooperation.
- **Judicial backlogs and corruption**: Courts that are overwhelmed, underfunded, or compromised cannot process cases effectively, even when investigations succeed.
- **Police corruption and complicity**: In some contexts, police officers are directly complicit in criminal activity or receive bribes to suppress investigations.

Human Rights Watch's 2025 report *Double Injustice* on Mexico documented systematic failures: cases delayed for years, witnesses threatened, suspects released due to procedural failures, with almost no accountability for perpetrators. Similar patterns are documented across Central America and parts of Africa.

The impunity trap is self-reinforcing: low conviction rates signal low risk to would-be perpetrators, incentivising continued violence; communities that observe justice failure stop cooperating with police, reducing the information available for investigations; police, knowing that cases will rarely result in convictions, reduce investigative effort.

### 3.5 Police Deployment Strategies: What the Evidence Shows

Despite the structural complexity of the homicide problem, police deployment strategies have a demonstrated evidence base for short- and medium-term crime reduction:

#### Hot Spots Policing

The most robust evidence base in policing research. Weisburd et al.'s 2016 Campbell Systematic Review of 62 studies — predominantly randomised controlled trials — found that **concentrating police presence and problem-solving activities at high-crime micro-locations consistently reduces crime**. Crucially, displacement (crime simply relocating to adjacent areas) was found to be rare; more often, benefits diffused to surrounding areas.

The mechanism is not purely deterrence through presence; interventions combining increased patrol with problem-oriented problem-solving (addressing the physical and social conditions that generate crime) generally outperform patrol-only approaches.

#### Focused Deterrence (the "Kennedy Model")

Developed by David Kennedy through Boston's Operation Ceasefire and refined through subsequent implementations in dozens of US cities, focused deterrence targets high-risk individuals and criminal groups directly. The approach combines:
1. Credible communication to high-risk individuals about the specific legal consequences of continued violence
2. Genuine offers of social service support for those willing to exit criminal activity
3. Swift, certain enforcement against those who persist

Boston's initial implementation reduced youth homicides by approximately 63%. A 2019 Campbell Systematic Review of focused deterrence strategies found that approximately 90% of evaluated programs produced statistically significant crime reductions, with moderate effect sizes. A Mexico City implementation of focused deterrence produced a roughly 17% reduction in homicides in targeted areas.

#### Optimised Patrol Allocation

A 2024 randomised study in Bogotá (published in *PLOS ONE*) tested an algorithm-driven police deployment model against standard patrol approaches. The optimised model reduced violent crime by approximately **7%** and property crime by over **8%**, without increasing costs or total police time. This demonstrates that smarter allocation — concentrating resources where and when crime occurs — produces meaningful gains independent of additional resources.

#### Community Policing

Community-oriented policing (COP) strategies — which emphasise officer-community relationship building, responsiveness to community concerns, and co-production of public safety — have a more mixed evidence base but consistent evidence that they improve public perceptions of police legitimacy. The mechanism is relevant: improved trust generates more information sharing, which aids investigations, which improves clearance rates, which reinforces deterrence.

### 3.6 The Legitimacy Problem: Why Deployment Alone Is Insufficient

The effectiveness of the above strategies is substantially moderated by the degree to which police are perceived as legitimate by the communities they serve. This is not merely a theoretical construct; it is one of the most replicated findings in criminology over the past three decades.

#### Procedural Justice Theory

Tom Tyler's foundational research (*Why People Obey the Law*, 1990; *Procedural Justice, Legitimacy, and the Effective Rule of Law*, 2003) established that people comply with legal authority primarily because they perceive it as legitimate — and that legitimacy is primarily driven by perceived procedural fairness in police-citizen encounters, not by outcome satisfaction. When officers are seen as respectful, unbiased, transparent, and genuinely accountable, cooperation increases — including the voluntary reporting of crime information.

Mazerolle et al.'s 2013 Campbell Systematic Review confirmed that procedural justice-focused interventions reliably improve perceived legitimacy and increase cooperation. Subsequent meta-analyses have also found associations between legitimacy and reduced reoffending.

#### The Latin American and African Legitimacy Deficit

The gap between this theoretical ideal and the reality in high-homicide regions is stark:

- **Latinobarómetro 2023** (18 countries): Only a minority of respondents expressed trust in police; approximately **only 20% of crime victims report crimes to authorities**, primarily due to perceived futility, fear of retaliation, or distrust of police.
- **Statista/LAPOP data**: In Mexico, over 85% of respondents believe state police are corrupt. Across the region, 50% or more of survey participants in several countries believe most or all police officers are involved in corruption.
- **Afrobarometer 2021–2023** (39 African countries): Only approximately **one in three Africans believes police generally act professionally and respect citizens' rights**. Widespread reports of bribe solicitation, abuse, and extortion document an institutional failure of the police protection function — illustrated vividly by Nigeria's #EndSARS protest movement in 2020.

The institutional perspective study in the *British Journal of Criminology* (2023) found that across Latin American countries, the quality of state institutions — including police effectiveness and corruption levels — explained **more variance in homicide rates than conventional socioeconomic variables**. This finding is powerful: it means that in this region, getting institutions right matters more than income growth for reducing violence.

#### Why Deployment Without Legitimacy Fails — or Backfires

Several mechanisms explain why deployment strategies without legitimacy reforms tend to underperform or even increase violence:

1. **Information failure**: Police who are distrusted receive less voluntary information from communities. Crime-solving rates remain low even with more officers. Organised crime groups can operate because witnesses will not testify.

2. **Abusive enforcement cycles**: Where police are corrupt or violent, increased presence can mean increased extraction, harassment, and rights violations — particularly targeting already-marginalised communities. This generates grievances that can fuel both informal justice and further violence.

3. **Legitimation of vigilantism**: In areas where police are absent, corrupt, or predatory, communities sometimes turn to vigilante groups or accept the protective governance of criminal organisations. This dynamic is extensively documented in parts of Mexico, Central America, and West Africa. Deploying more police without addressing these dynamics can trigger violent confrontations between state and informal security actors.

4. **Rally-around-crime effects**: Indiscriminate enforcement that produces false arrests, abuses, or kills community members can generate sympathy for criminal actors and undermine cooperative relationships critical to intelligence.

### 3.7 Case Studies: Contrasting Approaches

#### El Salvador: Extreme Mano Dura Under Bukele (2022–present)

El Salvador's experience is the most dramatic recent test of pure enforcement-focused policing. Between 2015 and 2019, the country's homicide rate fell from 103 to around 36 per 100,000 — partly due to negotiated gang arrangements. After Bukele's declaration of a state of emergency in March 2022, security forces conducted mass arrests of over 87,000 individuals, catapulting El Salvador to the world's highest incarceration rate. By 2024, the homicide rate had fallen to approximately **2.4 per 100,000** — a historically unprecedented decline.

What does this case prove? Several things simultaneously:
- **Incapacitation works at scale**: Removing a very large proportion of gang members from circulation breaks territorial gang control and rapidly reduces homicide.
- **The human rights costs are severe**: Multiple human rights organisations documented widespread arbitrary detention, deaths in custody (over 200 confirmed), torture, and systematic denial of due process. The International Crisis Group described the "model" as a "democratic emergency."
- **Replicability is limited**: Other countries (Honduras, Ecuador) attempting similar approaches have not achieved comparable results. El Salvador's relatively small geography, the concentrated nature of MS-13/Barrio 18 gang structures, and the political context were all enabling conditions unlikely to generalise.
- **Sustainability is unknown**: If the underlying social conditions — poverty, inequality, lack of opportunity — that generate gang recruitment are not addressed, violence may resurge when (or if) the emergency measures end.

#### Bogotá: Data-Driven Deployment Gains (2024)

A sharply contrasting example is Bogotá's evidence-based optimised deployment study. Rather than increasing police numbers or coercive powers, Colombian researchers and police implemented an algorithm-driven patrol allocation system, redistributing existing resources to high-crime times and places. The 7% violent crime reduction and 8% property crime reduction achieved at no additional cost demonstrates that intelligence-led deployment is a high-efficiency, rights-compatible intervention — though the study also acknowledged that underlying social conditions were not changed.

#### Boston Operation Ceasefire / Group Violence Intervention (1990s–present)

Boston's focused deterrence model has been rigorously evaluated across multiple cities over three decades. Its defining feature is legitimacy-preserving: it targets only those demonstrably at high risk of violence, communicates consequences respectfully and directly, and simultaneously offers genuine social support. Community leaders and organisations are central partners, not passive recipients. The model has achieved sustained homicide reductions and has been adapted internationally, including in Trinidad and Tobago, Glasgow (Scotland), and several Colombian cities — though with varying fidelity and results.

#### Nigeria: #EndSARS and the Cost of Illegitimacy

Nigeria's SARS (Special Anti-Robbery Squad) was a police unit that became notorious for extortion, torture, and extrajudicial killings of young Nigerians — particularly in urban areas and particularly targeting young men perceived as criminals based on appearance (dreadlocks, laptops, smartphones). The 2020 #EndSARS protests, which resulted in security forces killing demonstrators at the Lekki Toll Gate, dramatically illustrated the price of institutional illegitimacy: the police unit nominally tasked with reducing robbery had become a principal source of predatory violence, generating mass public resistance that further weakened police authority.

### 3.8 A Sequencing Question: Legitimacy First or Deployment First?

The framing of "legitimacy first" versus "deployment first" may be a false dichotomy, but it raises a genuine strategic question for governments and donors with limited budgets and time horizons.

The evidence suggests the following nuanced position:

**In contexts of extreme, acute violence spikes** — where criminal actors have effectively captured territory and are killing at rates that destabilise social order — some degree of rapid enforcement response may be necessary to restore a baseline of security. However, *how* this enforcement is conducted matters enormously. Evidence from Operation Ceasefire and similar programs suggests that **targeted, transparent enforcement with procedural safeguards preserves legitimacy** while reducing violence. Indiscriminate mass operations that terrorise whole communities set back legitimacy and ultimately undermine the crime reduction goal.

**In medium-term reform contexts** — where the goal is sustainably reducing homicide rates rather than managing an acute crisis — the institutional quality literature is emphatic: **without improvements in police accountability, corruption, and procedural conduct, even technically sophisticated deployment strategies will be self-limiting**. Officers who are paid to avoid investigating cases will not investigate them; communities who distrust police will not provide information; courts that are corrupted will not convict.

**The most effective long-term frameworks appear to combine:**
1. Legitimacy-building measures (accountability mechanisms, anti-corruption reforms, training in procedural justice, community liaison structures) *concurrent with*
2. Evidence-based deployment (hot spots, focused deterrence, optimised patrol) *concurrent with*
3. Social investment in the upstream conditions that generate criminal recruitment (education, employment, urbanisation quality, drug treatment)

This is demanding institutionally and fiscally — but the evidence base supports it over single-lever approaches.

## 5. Open Questions and Next Steps

### Unresolved Empirical Questions

1. **Does focused deterrence work in contexts of very low police legitimacy?** Most of the evidence base comes from U.S. and Western European cities where police, though imperfect, have baseline levels of public acceptance. Whether the same approach produces equivalent results where police are widely viewed as predatory or criminal is underexplored. Adaptations being trialled in Trinidad and Tobago, El Salvador (pre-Bukele), and Colombia offer preliminary data, but rigorous evaluation is scarce.

2. **What is the minimum viable level of institutional quality needed for deployment strategies to work?** The literature consistently says legitimacy matters, but does not specify thresholds. Is community policing productive when 60% of citizens believe police are corrupt? 80%? What degree of reform is necessary before deployment investment pays off?

3. **How do organised crime equilibria reshape after enforcement disruptions?** The "kingpin strategy" literature has repeatedly found that targeting cartel or gang leadership fragments criminal organisations and raises violence. But what determines whether fragmentation leads to a new stable equilibrium at lower violence levels, or to a prolonged war among successor groups? Better causal modelling here would guide enforcement targeting.

4. **What are the long-term outcomes of the Bukele model?** El Salvador's trajectory will be critical evidence for the mano dura debate. Key questions: Do homicide reductions persist when/if the emergency measures are relaxed? What is the recidivism rate among released detainees? Does incapacitation-led peace substitute for or enable social investment?

### Policy and Operational Questions

5. **How should international development organisations sequence and condition support?** Current practice — often funding police equipment and training without conditioning on accountability reforms — may be reinforcing illegitimacy rather than reducing it. Donor coordination and conditionality frameworks need sharper design.

6. **Can procedural justice training be scaled in low-capacity police forces?** The evidence base for procedural justice training is mostly from high-income country contexts. Implementation in police forces that are underpaid, politically directed, and institutionally fragile is a different challenge. What adaptations are necessary?

7. **How should violence prevention distinguish between crime types?** Conflict-related, gang-related, intimate partner, and inter-communal violence have different causal structures and likely require different interventions. Generic "violence reduction" programmes risk misallocating resources by treating heterogeneous phenomena as uniform.

8. **What role should community-based, non-police violence interruption play?** Evidence from "cure violence" and "Peacemakers" models — where credible messengers from affected communities mediate conflicts and interrupt cycles of retaliatory violence — is promising but underscaled, particularly in Latin American and African contexts.

### Priority Next Steps for Researchers and Practitioners

- Commission rigorous evaluations of focused deterrence and hot spots policing in low-legitimacy contexts in Latin America and Africa, with pre-registered designs.
- Develop cross-national datasets linking institutional quality indicators (corruption, professionalism, accountability) to sub-national homicide variation within countries, enabling more granular analysis than current national-level studies.
