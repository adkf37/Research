## 1. Question and Framing

Malaria is one of the oldest and most lethal infectious diseases in human history. In 2023, the World Health Organization (WHO) estimated **263 million malaria cases and 597,000 deaths globally** — roughly equivalent to a loaded commercial aircraft crashing every hour of every day. Yet the disease's impact is radically unequal: approximately 95% of deaths occur in sub-Saharan Africa, and five countries alone — Nigeria, the Democratic Republic of the Congo, Uganda, Ethiopia, and Mozambique — account for over half of all global cases.

Against this backdrop, a striking divergence has emerged. Forty-four countries and one territory have now been certified malaria-free by the WHO. Since 2000, an estimated **2.2 billion cases and 12.7 million deaths have been averted** through a combination of scaled-up interventions. China eliminated a disease that once afflicted 30 million of its citizens annually. Sri Lanka, El Salvador, and Belize have reached zero indigenous cases. Meanwhile, Nigeria — with similar global access to proven tools — accounts for roughly **30%** of the world's malaria burden.

This brief asks: **What explains this divergence?** Is it geography, wealth, political will, strategy, or some combination of all four? And what can the evidence tell us about which approaches actually work?

The answer matters enormously. With funding for malaria programs falling at roughly half the required level, and with artemisinin drug resistance now confirmed in at least eight African countries, the choices made by national governments and their international partners in the next decade will determine whether malaria control advances or collapses.

## 2. Key Findings

1. **Yes, some countries are demonstrably better at malaria mitigation.** The variation is not random — it correlates with specific policy choices, institutional capacities, and levels of sustained commitment rather than geography or wealth alone.

2. **Elimination is achievable at many income levels.** Countries as economically diverse as China, Sri Lanka, El Salvador, Belize, Cabo Verde, and Egypt have achieved WHO malaria-free certification. High income is not a prerequisite.

3. **The "1-3-7" surveillance model** (China's paradigm of case reporting in 1 day, investigation in 3, response in 7) has been widely recognised as a transferable gold standard for the elimination phase and is being studied for adaptation in sub-Saharan Africa.

4. **Integrated, layered interventions consistently outperform single-tool approaches.** Countries combining insecticide-treated nets (ITNs), indoor residual spraying (IRS), rapid diagnostic testing (RDTs), artemisinin-based combination therapies (ACTs), and seasonal malaria chemoprevention (SMC) achieve better outcomes than those deploying any single measure alone.

5. **Community health workers are a force multiplier.** In Sri Lanka, El Salvador, and several African pilot programs, training local health workers in active case detection and treatment doubled the reach of formal health systems.

6. **Funding and political will are the binding constraints for high-burden nations.** Nigeria's failure to control malaria is not primarily a knowledge problem — proven tools exist — but a problem of implementation, funding, governance, and health system weakness.

7. **Two simultaneous threats risk reversing 25 years of progress:** artemisinin resistance is now confirmed in eight African countries, and global malaria funding in 2024 (~$3.9 billion) is less than half the WHO target of $9.3 billion per year.

8. **New vaccines (RTS,S and R21/Matrix-M) offer a meaningful boost** but are not silver bullets: real-world efficacy is 30–50% reduction in cases, most powerful when layered onto existing vector control.

## 3. Detailed Analysis

### 3.1 The Global Burden: A Deeply Unequal Problem

The 2024 WHO World Malaria Report reveals the stark geography of the disease. Sub-Saharan Africa carries 94% of the global case load. Within Africa, the distribution is further skewed: the top five countries by burden (Nigeria, DRC, Uganda, Ethiopia, Mozambique) alone account for more than 50% of global cases.

This concentration is partly biological — *Anopheles gambiae*, the primary African vector, is uniquely efficient at transmitting *Plasmodium falciparum*, the most lethal parasite species — and partly structural. Countries with the highest burden also tend to have the weakest health systems, lowest domestic health expenditure, highest rates of insecticide and drug resistance, and the heaviest dependence on external donor funding.

At the other extreme, 25 countries worldwide now report **fewer than 10 malaria cases per year**, compared with just 4 countries in 2000. This dramatic shift demonstrates that elimination is not a fantasy — but the path from high transmission to zero is steep and requires sustained commitment over decades.

### 3.2 The Elimination Success Stories

#### China: The 1-3-7 Model

China's path from 30 million annual malaria cases in the 1940s to WHO-certified malaria-free status in 2021 is the most dramatic elimination story of the modern era. The final phase of China's campaign was defined by its **"1-3-7" strategy**:

- **1 day:** Any confirmed malaria case must be reported to the national surveillance system.
- **3 days:** A full case investigation must be completed, identifying potential sources of infection.
- **7 days:** A focused response — including targeted vector control and treatment of identified contacts — must be mounted to interrupt transmission.

This protocol was institutionalised across China's health system and backed by strong central government financing. By 2017, China had recorded **zero indigenous malaria cases** for four consecutive years, satisfying the WHO requirement for certification. The country also invested heavily in capacity building, digital reporting infrastructure, and free universal access to diagnosis and treatment.

The model has since been studied for adaptation in sub-Saharan Africa and has influenced WHO's elimination guidelines globally.

#### Sri Lanka: Decentralised Surveillance and Community Ownership

Sri Lanka received WHO malaria-free certification in 2016 after decades of sustained effort. Sri Lanka is notable for having experienced a serious malaria resurgence in the 1990s during its civil conflict — demonstrating both how fragile gains can be and how they can be recovered.

The key to Sri Lanka's final elimination was a **decentralised surveillance architecture** in which public health inspectors and trained community volunteers served as the first line of detection. Every fever case in a malaria-risk zone was investigated, and community members were engaged as partners rather than passive recipients of interventions. Cross-sectoral coordination — between health, education, military, and local government — was critical.

#### El Salvador: Promotores de Salud

El Salvador achieved WHO certification in 2021, becoming the first Central American country certified in the 21st century. Its approach centred on *promotores de salud* (community health promoters): laypersons from local communities who were trained, equipped, and paid to conduct house-to-house active case detection, distribute bed nets, provide basic treatment, and report data to the national surveillance system.

This model reduced the dependence on formal health facilities (often unavailable in remote rural areas) and dramatically increased coverage. Continuous training, close supervision, and political commitment from the national malaria program sustained the effort over more than two decades.

#### Cabo Verde and Egypt: African Elimination

In 2024, **Cabo Verde** became the third African country certified malaria-free (after Mauritius and Algeria), and **Egypt** became the fifth. Both achieved certification through strong vector control programs, robust passive and active surveillance, and — critically — the political decision to treat every case as a discrete event requiring investigation and response rather than a routine outcome to be managed statistically.

Cabo Verde's island geography gave it an epidemiological advantage (restricted vector movement), but Egypt's elimination in a large, diverse country with significant water infrastructure challenges demonstrates that context alone does not determine outcome.

### 3.3 High-Burden Countries: Why Control Remains Elusive

#### Nigeria: The World's Largest Malaria Burden

Nigeria accounts for approximately **27–30% of all global malaria cases and deaths**, a figure that has remained stubbornly high despite decades of international attention and investment. Understanding Nigeria's failure to control malaria requires examining the full system, not just the availability of tools.

**Health system capacity:** Many health facilities lack reliable staff, diagnostic equipment, and drug supply chains. Stock-outs of artemisinin-based combination therapies are common, particularly in rural areas. Community health workers exist but are often unpaid, undersupported, and operating without adequate supervision.

**Funding:** Nigeria's domestic spending on malaria control is far below the required level, and the country is heavily dependent on external donors (primarily the U.S. President's Malaria Initiative and the Global Fund). This creates programme fragility — a funding interruption, such as occurred during the COVID-19 pandemic, can rapidly reverse gains.

**Vector control coverage:** ITN distribution campaigns have improved but remain inadequate. IRS coverage is limited. Insecticide resistance in *Anopheles gambiae* populations is increasing, reducing the effectiveness of pyrethroids — the most widely used class of insecticide.

**Drug resistance:** Artemisinin partial resistance mutations have been detected in Nigeria, raising concerns about the durability of ACTs as first-line treatment.

**Surveillance:** Nigeria's malaria surveillance system substantially underestimates the true burden (WHO estimates suggest only a fraction of cases are formally reported), making it difficult to target interventions efficiently.

**Governance:** Programme management is fragmented across federal, state, and local levels, with inconsistent implementation quality.

None of these problems is unique to Nigeria — they appear in similar combinations in the DRC, Uganda, Mozambique, and elsewhere. But their co-occurrence in a country of 220 million people with year-round high-transmission conditions makes the challenge exceptionally difficult.

### 3.4 Comparative Strategies by Region

#### Sub-Saharan Africa: Scale vs. Precision

The dominant challenge in sub-Saharan Africa is **scale**: the number of people at risk, the geographic size of the endemic zone, and the year-round nature of transmission in most areas. The primary tools deployed are:

- **Insecticide-treated nets (ITNs/LLINs):** Mass distribution campaigns have delivered hundreds of millions of nets. However, net usage rates lag behind distribution, net durability is limited, and insecticide resistance is reducing effectiveness.
- **Indoor residual spraying (IRS):** Highly effective when implemented with high fidelity and coverage. Rwanda's IRS campaign in Ngoma District reduced annual malaria cases by approximately 14,380 in the year following the first campaign — a statistically significant (p < 0.001) impact that illustrates what is achievable with targeted, well-executed IRS.
- **Seasonal malaria chemoprevention (SMC):** Administering prophylactic antimalarials to children under five during high-transmission seasons. Has demonstrated high efficacy in the Sahel and has been scaled across West and Central Africa.
- **Rapid diagnostic tests and ACTs:** Expanding access to confirmed diagnosis and effective treatment has reduced mortality substantially, though drug resistance now threatens ACT efficacy.

The African region has made genuine progress: between 2000 and 2022, malaria incidence declined by approximately 40% and mortality by approximately 60% across the WHO African Region. But absolute case numbers remain enormous, and progress has plateaued in recent years.

#### Southeast Asia: Targeting Elimination

Southeast Asia has taken a more targeted approach, oriented explicitly toward **elimination** in most countries. The region's malaria profile differs importantly from Africa's:

- *Plasmodium vivax* malaria (which can establish liver dormancy and relapse) is more prevalent, requiring more complex treatment regimens.
- Transmission is often concentrated in forest environments and among specific mobile populations (forest workers, migrants, military personnel) rather than generalised across communities.
- *Anopheles* vector species differ from Africa, making some African vector control methods less applicable.

Countries in the Greater Mekong Subregion (Myanmar, Thailand, Laos, Cambodia, Vietnam) have used **mass drug administration (MDA)** in hotspot areas, aggressive **border surveillance and migrant screening**, and community-level active case detection to drive down cases. Cross-border coordination — difficult given political sensitivities — has been essential, since malaria does not respect national borders and re-importation from adjacent countries can rapidly undo gains.

Cambodia and Vietnam have achieved dramatic reductions. Sri Lanka's elimination (certified 2016) was the regional flagship. The greatest ongoing threat in the region is **artemisinin resistance**, which emerged in Cambodia around 2008–2009 and has spread across the Mekong region, threatening global malaria treatment.

| Region | Primary Strategy | Major Successes | Ongoing Challenges |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sub-Saharan Africa | Mass ITN distribution, IRS, SMC, ACTs, vaccines | 40–60% reduction in cases/deaths since 2000; vaccine rollout | Drug and insecticide resistance; funding gaps; system capacity |
| Southeast Asia | Targeted elimination; MDA; surveillance; border control | Sri Lanka certified 2016; Mekong reductions | Vivax malaria; mobile populations; artemisinin resistance |
| Latin America | Community health workers; surveillance; vector control | El Salvador (2021), Belize (2023) certified | Forest ecology; cross-border transmission; funding sustainability |
| China / Western Pacific | 1-3-7 surveillance; mass government investment; free care | China certified 2021 | Preventing importation from endemic neighbours |

### 3.5 Vaccines: The New Frontier

For decades, the absence of an effective malaria vaccine was a significant constraint on control efforts. That changed with two recent developments:

**RTS,S/AS01 (Mosquirix):** The first WHO-recommended malaria vaccine, endorsed in 2021. In clinical trials and real-world pilots conducted in Ghana, Kenya, and Malawi between 2019 and 2023:
- ~30% reduction in severe malaria with a four-dose schedule in children aged 5–17 months.
- 32% reduction in severe malaria hospitalizations and 9% reduction in all-cause child mortality in pilot areas.
- By end of 2023, over 6 million doses delivered and more than 2 million children protected.

**R21/Matrix-M:** The second WHO-recommended malaria vaccine, approved in 2023, developed by the University of Oxford and manufactured by the Serum Institute of India. Trials show greater than 50% reduction in clinical malaria in the 12 months following three initial doses — making it the first malaria vaccine to exceed the WHO's own 75% efficacy threshold in some settings.

As of 2024, **24 African countries** are incorporating malaria vaccines into their routine immunization schedules. Ghana is expanding both RTS,S and R21 rollouts and plans to cover all high-transmission regions by 2029.

**Critical context:** These vaccines are significant but not transformative on their own. Real-world efficacy of 30–50% is meaningful for a disease causing 600,000 deaths annually, but vaccines at this efficacy level prevent rather than eliminate malaria. Their greatest impact comes when layered onto existing vector control and treatment programs — another argument for integrated, multi-tool strategies.

### 3.6 The Threat Landscape: Resistance and Funding

Two converging threats risk reversing the progress of the past quarter-century.

**Artemisinin resistance:** Artemisinin-based combination therapies (ACTs) have been the backbone of malaria treatment globally since chloroquine resistance rendered the previous standard treatment useless in the 1980s–90s. Chloroquine resistance contributed to a catastrophic resurgence in African malaria mortality in the late 1990s. ACT resistance would be equally devastating.

In 2024, resistance mutations (specifically kelch13 mutations conferring partial artemisinin resistance) have been confirmed or strongly suspected in **at least eight African countries**, including Rwanda, Uganda, Ethiopia, Eritrea, and Tanzania. In some areas, resistant parasite strains account for 10–20% of cases. Oxford University's Nuffield Department of Medicine has called for "immediate policy changes" including adoption of multiple first-line therapies (MFT) — rotating between different ACTs — to slow resistance spread.

**Funding gap:** Global malaria financing in 2024 was approximately **$3.9 billion** — less than half the **$9.3 billion annual target** set by the WHO Global Technical Strategy for Malaria 2016–2030. Overseas Development Aid from wealthy countries fell by roughly 21% during 2023–2024. The Global Fund — which finances approximately 62% of all international malaria programs — faced uncertainty over U.S. contributions, creating programme instability across dozens of high-burden countries.

In Kenya and Tanzania, governments reported tens of millions of dollars in funding shortfalls that directly threatened net distribution campaigns, SMC programs, and diagnostic supply chains. The consequences of funding gaps are not abstract: interrupted ITN campaigns can lead to measurable rises in child mortality within one to two years.

### 3.7 What Separates the Leaders from the Laggards

Comparing the elimination success stories against persistently high-burden nations reveals a consistent pattern of enabling and disabling factors:

| Factor | Elimination Countries | High-Burden Countries |
|---|---|---|
| **Political will** | Malaria framed as a national priority with executive commitment | Malaria competes with many other health and development priorities |
| **Domestic financing** | Significant domestic health budget allocated to malaria | Heavy dependence on external donors; domestic spending insufficient |
| **Surveillance system** | Timely, high-coverage case detection and reporting | Underreporting; weak data systems; poor targeting of interventions |
| **Community engagement** | CHWs as active, resourced partners in service delivery | CHWs underpaid, undersupplied, or absent in key areas |
| **Vector control fidelity** | High-coverage IRS and/or ITN with regular updates | Distribution gaps; poor uptake; insecticide resistance |
| **Health system capacity** | Reliable diagnostic testing and drug supply chains | Stockouts; undertrained staff; facility gaps |
| **Integrated approach** | Multiple tools deployed together and adapted to local context | Single-tool dependence; limited adaptation to resistance |
| **Sustained effort** | Decades of consistent program management | Programme disruptions due to funding, conflict, or political change |

Geography matters but is not deterministic. Rwanda, a landlocked, high-altitude sub-Saharan African country with limited income, has achieved some of Africa's fastest malaria case reductions through strong government commitment, rigorous IRS targeting, and community health worker networks — demonstrating that middle-income African countries can make rapid gains with the right approach. Conversely, countries with geographic advantages (island geography, lower vector density) that lack political commitment have failed to achieve elimination.

**The core insight** is that malaria control is tractable — the tools exist — but it requires a functioning health system, sustained financing, surveillance capacity, and the political will to treat every case as a problem to be solved rather than an expected background condition.

## 5. Open Questions and Next Steps

### Unresolved Scientific Questions

1. **Can artemisinin resistance be contained?** The current spread of kelch13 resistance mutations through East and Southern Africa raises the spectre of a return to the pre-ACT era. Research into new antimalarial compounds is ongoing (including multiple pipeline candidates at the Medicines for Malaria Venture), but no replacement for ACTs is imminent. The key question is whether multiple first-line therapy (MFT) strategies can slow resistance spread before an alternative is available.

2. **What will the next-generation malaria vaccine look like?** Current vaccines (RTS,S, R21) target the *Plasmodium falciparum* circumsporozoite protein. More ambitious candidates targeting blood-stage parasites or transmission-blocking vaccines could achieve higher efficacy but are further from deployment.

3. **How transferable is the 1-3-7 model to sub-Saharan Africa?** China's model was implemented in a country with a strong centralised state capacity and a population that had already reduced malaria to very low levels. The evidence base for applying 1-3-7 style surveillance in high-transmission African settings is still being built.

4. **What will AI-enhanced vector surveillance yield?** Several research programs are testing AI-powered analysis of mosquito acoustics, satellite imagery of breeding sites, and genomic surveillance of parasite populations to enable real-time, targeted intervention. Results to date are promising but not yet operationally deployed at scale.

### Policy and Operational Questions

5. **Can domestic financing be meaningfully increased in high-burden countries?** Several African governments have pledged to increase domestic health spending, but implementation has lagged. Debt burdens and economic headwinds make this difficult. Innovative financing mechanisms (malaria bonds, regional risk pools) have been proposed but not yet proven at scale.

6. **How should the malaria community respond to the U.S. aid reduction?** The Global Fund's dependency on U.S. contributions (approximately 33% of Global Fund financing comes from the U.S.) creates a systemic vulnerability. Diversifying the donor base and building domestic financing capacity are critical but take years to achieve.

7. **What does equity-centered malaria control look like in practice?** The WHO's 2024 report explicitly frames malaria as an equity issue: vulnerable groups (children under five, pregnant women, remote rural populations, conflict-affected communities) are both most at risk and least served by current systems. Operationalising equity-centered approaches — not just targeting efficiency — requires deliberate design choices in program implementation.

### Priority Next Steps for Researchers and Practitioners

- **Monitor artemisinin resistance spread** with expanded genomic surveillance programs in East and Central Africa, sharing data rapidly across national programs.
- **Scale community health worker programs** in high-burden countries with attention to adequate compensation, supervision, and supply chain support.
- **Accelerate vaccine rollout** in the 24 countries already approved, with particular attention to ensuring cold chain capacity and community demand generation.
- **Commission independent evaluations** of which specific implementation factors (coverage thresholds, community engagement depth, surveillance frequency) drive the largest gains in context-similar countries.
- **Develop transition financing plans** for countries currently dependent on U.S. bilateral aid, anticipating further reductions.
