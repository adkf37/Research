## 1. Question and Framing

South Africa is one of the most unequal countries on earth. Its Gini coefficient — a standard measure of income inequality where 0 is perfect equality and 1 is perfect inequality — has persistently hovered between **0.63 and 0.67**, placing it at or near the top of every global ranking. This extreme inequality is not incidental: it is the compounded product of over three centuries of colonial dispossession and four decades of codified apartheid, a system that deliberately concentrated land, capital, skilled employment, and public services in the hands of a racial minority. The post-1994 democratic government inherited an economy deeply segmented by race, space, and skill — and despite three decades of democratically elected governance, the fundamental structure of the economy has changed less than either advocates or critics expected.

South Africa is also a **middle-income country** by World Bank classification, with a GDP per capita (PPP-adjusted) of approximately **$14,000–15,000** as of 2024. This places it in a group that includes Brazil, Colombia, Malaysia, and Mexico — countries rich enough to have real state capacity and infrastructure, but not yet prosperous enough to absorb the political costs of the structural reforms required to break through to high-income status. The World Bank and others have identified this as the **"middle-income trap"**: a pattern in which middle-income countries grow rapidly in early phases by absorbing existing technologies and deploying underutilised labour, then stagnate as they exhaust easy gains and lack the institutional, human capital, and innovation foundations to shift to productivity-led growth.

The combination — extreme inequality *and* middle-income trap — is particularly pernicious. High inequality depresses aggregate demand, misallocates talent (millions of potentially productive people are excluded from formal economic participation), generates political instability, and raises the cost of maintaining social order. Meanwhile, the middle-income trap dynamic means that South Africa cannot simply grow its way out of inequality by riding a wave of rapid overall expansion: productivity growth itself requires resolving the skill deficits and spatial exclusions that inequality perpetuates.

This brief addresses three interrelated policy questions:

1. **Education investment**: How large should investments be, how should they be structured pedagogically and institutionally, and — critically — what is the labor force available to deliver them?
2. **Rail infrastructure**: Can high-speed or improved rail networks meaningfully reduce transportation costs and increase mobility for the rural and peri-urban poor?
3. **Other essential interventions**: What complementary policies — land reform, industrial strategy, social protection, urban densification — are necessary for a coherent development strategy?

The brief draws primarily on South Africa's context but uses comparative evidence from Brazil, South Korea, Malaysia, China, and other middle-income countries to ground assessments of what has worked and under what conditions.

## 2. Key Findings

1. **South Africa is caught in a dual trap**: a middle-income trap compounded by a structural inequality trap. The two reinforce each other — inequality limits human capital accumulation, which limits productivity growth, which limits the fiscal space needed to address inequality. Breaking out requires simultaneous action on multiple fronts, not sequential reform.

2. **Education spending is not the binding constraint — quality is.** South Africa already spends approximately **6–7% of GDP on education**, among the highest ratios globally for a middle-income country. Yet the PIRLS 2021 international literacy benchmark found that **81% of South African Grade 4 learners cannot read for meaning** in any language. The problem is not resources but how they are deployed: specifically, foundational literacy and numeracy instruction quality, teacher preparation, and school management.

3. **The teacher labor force is deeply bifurcated.** South Africa has approximately **420,000 public school teachers**. Many, particularly in historically disadvantaged schools, have themselves received poor-quality basic education, creating a structural feedback loop. Solving the teacher quality problem requires both remediation of the existing workforce (through structured coaching and in-service training, not one-off workshops) and transformation of teacher pre-service education — a multi-decade undertaking.

4. **High-speed rail is not the right priority for South Africa's transport challenges** in the near to medium term. South Africa's core commuter transport failure is not speed but **frequency, reliability, affordability, and spatial coverage** — problems Prasa (the passenger rail agency) has catastrophically failed to address over the past fifteen years. Rebuilding functional commuter rail and investing in bus rapid transit networks in major cities would yield far larger welfare gains, especially for the poor, than a prestige high-speed intercity network.

5. **Spatial apartheid persists economically even after its legal abolition.** The apartheid-era system of placing Black townships far from economic centres, with poor transport links, has an ongoing economic cost: workers in South African cities spend **2–4 hours per day commuting**, representing a massive loss of productive time and household income. Spatial policy — urban densification, social housing near economic centres, mixed-use zoning — is an underrated development lever.

6. **Brazil's Bolsa Família and South Korea's developmental state offer complementary templates.** Conditional cash transfers can simultaneously reduce present poverty and build human capital by conditioning transfers on school attendance and health check-ups. South Korea's state-directed investment in technical education and selective industrial promotion generated rapid structural transformation — though the political conditions differed markedly from present-day South Africa.

7. **Industrial policy targeted at labor-intensive manufacturing and services can generate the formal employment necessary to absorb the large pool of low-to-middle-skill workers.** South Africa's unemployment rate of **33–42% (broad definition)** is structurally anomalous even for a middle-income country. No amount of education improvement alone will resolve it without a parallel expansion of formal employment opportunities.

8. **Social protection at South Africa's current scale (approximately 18 million grant recipients in a country of 62 million) is a foundation, not a temporary scaffold.** South Africa's Child Support Grant and other social grants have meaningfully reduced extreme poverty. Expanding them to cover working-age adults without income — through either a Basic Income Grant or a large public employment programme — is increasingly supported by fiscal modelling, though financing pathways remain contested.

## 3. Detailed Analysis

### 3.1 The Inequality-Stagnation Nexus: Why Middle-Income Traps Are Worst When Unequal

The theoretical connection between inequality and middle-income traps is now well-established. Several mechanisms link them:

**Human capital exclusion**: When poor families cannot invest in children's nutrition, health, and education, a large fraction of the population enters adult life with skills far below their genetic and cognitive potential. This directly depresses aggregate human capital and, with it, productivity. South Africa's situation is acute: the quality gap between the 20% of schools serving affluent households (historically "Model C" and private schools) and the 80% serving the majority is extreme even by regional standards. The result is a skills distribution with a small peak of highly educated workers, a hollowed-out middle, and a very large base of workers with poor foundational skills.

**Demand compression**: High inequality compresses the domestic consumer market. When income is concentrated at the top, aggregate consumption grows more slowly and is tilted toward imports of luxury goods rather than domestically produced goods and services. This limits the market size that firms can access, reducing returns to investment and innovation.

**Rent-seeking and capture**: Extreme inequality creates powerful incentives for elites to use political connections to protect their positions rather than compete on productivity. South Africa's experience with "state capture" during the Zuma era (2009–2018) — a systematic looting of state-owned enterprises and procurement processes by a politically connected network — illustrates how inequality and elite capture interact to damage institutions and growth simultaneously.

**The World Bank's 2024 World Development Report** on the middle-income trap provides the most rigorous recent treatment of these dynamics. It argues that middle-income countries need a three-stage transition: from imitation (absorbing existing technologies) to **infusion** (adapting and diffusing them at scale) to **innovation** (frontier R&D). South Africa is struggling to complete the infusion stage because the inequality-education-skills nexus prevents broad diffusion of productivity-enhancing practices across the economy.

### 3.2 Education Investment: Scale, Quality, and the Labor Force Problem

#### Current Spending and Its Paradox

South Africa's education expenditure is frequently cited as evidence of commitment to transformation. Public spending on education has ranged from **6% to 7.1% of GDP** consistently since the early 2000s (National Treasury data; World Bank EdStats). In absolute per-learner terms, South Africa spends more than most sub-Saharan African peers and is comparable to many Eastern European middle-income countries.

The outcomes, however, are catastrophic by international standards:
- **PIRLS 2021**: 81% of Grade 4 learners (approximately age 10) cannot read for meaning in any language — the worst performance of any country participating in the international assessment.
- **SACMEQ IV** (Southern and Eastern Africa Consortium for Monitoring Educational Quality): South African Grade 6 mathematics and reading scores have improved marginally but remain below what would be expected given per-learner spending levels.
- **National Senior Certificate results**: Pass rates have improved on paper, but the cognitive demand of the examination has been repeatedly questioned, and the proportion of matriculants qualifying for university-level study remains low.

This paradox — high spending, low outcomes — is documented in detail by Nic Spaull (Stellenbosch University) and colleagues: the problem is not funding but **what the money is being used for**, and particularly how instruction is structured and delivered.

#### What the Evidence Says About Improving Instruction

A substantial body of research has now converged on several principles for improving learning outcomes at scale in low-and-middle-income country contexts:

**Foundational literacy and numeracy must be prioritised in the early grades.** Children who cannot read fluently by Grade 3 are on a trajectory that is very difficult to alter later. South Africa's national reading plan (the "Reading Panel" report commissioned by the DBE) correctly identifies this but implementation has been inconsistent. The structured literacy approach — explicit, systematic phonics instruction — has a robust evidence base from studies in South Africa itself (Foorman et al., 2016; Ardington et al.'s work at the University of Cape Town's SALDRU).

**Curriculum materials matter enormously.** Across many African countries, providing high-quality, well-sequenced textbooks aligned to the curriculum has been shown to meaningfully improve learning outcomes (Evans and Mendez Acosta, 2021, *Annual Review of Economics*). South Africa's textbook situation has improved since the crisis of 2012 (when Limpopo learners went the entire year without books) but curriculum-pacing guides and structured teacher materials remain inconsistently implemented.

**Coaching and in-service support, not workshops, change teacher practice.** The evidence on professional development is clear: one-off workshops have minimal lasting impact on teacher practice. What works is **sustained, school-based instructional coaching** in which a trained coach visits classrooms regularly, demonstrates techniques, observes lessons, and gives feedback. This is resource-intensive but evidence-backed. South Africa's National Education Collaboration Trust and some provincial-level programmes have piloted this model with promising results.

#### The Teacher Labor Force Problem

South Africa has approximately **420,000 teachers** in the public system. The challenge is structural:

1. **Historical skills deficits**: Many teachers who serve in historically disadvantaged schools were themselves educated in those schools during the apartheid or early post-apartheid period, when outcomes were even worse than today. A 2016 study by Spaull and Kotze found that a significant proportion of primary school mathematics teachers in the bottom two income quintiles of schools could not reliably answer Grade 6-level mathematics questions — meaning they are being asked to teach content they themselves have not mastered.

2. **Training pipeline quality**: The 26 university faculties of education in South Africa have highly variable quality, and there is limited consensus on what constitutes adequate pre-service teacher preparation. Studies show that beginning teachers often feel unprepared for the realities of classroom management in poorly resourced schools.

3. **Distribution of the best teachers**: Better-qualified teachers sort into better-resourced schools with higher pay-scale supplements — a pattern observed in South Africa and internationally. The schools that most need skilled instruction are least likely to attract it.

4. **What can be done**: Several intervention types have evidence behind them:
   - **Remediation of the existing workforce through structured programmes**: This is the highest-leverage intervention because it operates at scale. The Funda Wande programme (an NGO working with the DBE) has shown that structured literacy coaching can meaningfully improve Foundation Phase teachers' instructional quality in a 12–18 month programme.
   - **Bursary programmes to attract high performers into teaching**: South Africa's NSFAS bursary system has expanded, and additional incentives for high-achieving matriculants to enter teaching (especially in STEM subjects and in rural areas) are theoretically straightforward but politically and fiscally challenging.
   - **School leadership development**: Principals matter enormously. Research by Gustafsson (2016) and others shows that school leadership quality is one of the strongest predictors of school-level learning outcomes in South Africa, controlling for socioeconomic context. The National School Leadership Institute requires significant strengthening.

#### Timeline and Realistic Expectations

Education reform operates on generational timescales. A child born in 2026 who enters Grade 1 in 2033 and completes schooling in 2046 will only benefit from reforms initiated today if those reforms have been sustained for 20 years. This is not a reason not to act, but it means that education investment must be understood as a long-horizon strategy, not a medium-term lever for job creation. The near-term growth challenge requires parallel investments in employment creation that are not contingent on the education pipeline having been fixed.

### 3.3 High-Speed and Rapid Rail: Connectivity as a Development Lever

#### The Case for Rail Investment

The theoretical case for rail investment in a country like South Africa rests on several foundations:

1. **Reducing commuting costs for the poor**: High transportation costs are a major tax on low-income workers. Research from South Africa's own National Income Dynamics Study (NIDS-CRAM) found that **transport costs absorb 10–20% of low-income workers' monthly earnings** — a severe penalty that effectively lowers their real wages and reduces their willingness to accept work far from home.

2. **Connecting labour markets**: The apartheid spatial legacy means that the largest pools of unemployed labour are physically distant from the largest concentrations of formal jobs. Better rail connectivity would, in theory, expand the effective labour market radius for both workers and employers.

3. **Reducing freight costs**: South Africa's logistics costs for agriculture and manufacturing are high by international standards, partly due to the deterioration of rail freight infrastructure (Transnet Freight Rail). This raises costs for tradeable sectors and reduces competitiveness.

#### High-Speed Rail Specifically

The question of whether South Africa should invest in **high-speed rail** (typically defined as rail capable of 250km/h or more) deserves a nuanced answer.

**The argument in favour** points to:
- China's experience demonstrating that HSR investment can spatially restructure an economy, linking secondary cities to major economic nodes and enabling regional specialisation (Donaldson, 2018, *American Economic Review* on Indian railways offers a parallel). 
- Potential to reduce domestic aviation emissions and travel times between Johannesburg, Durban, and Cape Town.
- Employment generation during construction.

**The arguments against HSR for South Africa's near-term priorities** are more compelling:

- **Affordability and utilisation**: HSR is expensive to build (typically $10–40 million per kilometre for fully separated high-speed track) and requires high ridership to be financially viable. South Africa's urban poor — who stand to gain most from improved mobility — are priced out of HSR fares in virtually every country's experience. HSR primarily benefits middle-class intercity travellers.
- **Opportunity cost**: The same capital invested in **fixing commuter rail** would yield far larger welfare gains. Prasa, South Africa's passenger rail operator, has suffered catastrophic infrastructure deterioration: extensive vandalism, train carriage shortages (hundreds of coaches remain out of service), and severe schedule unreliability. **On some lines, service frequency has dropped from 30 trains per day to fewer than 5**. Restoring Prasa to baseline functionality would require an estimated R150–200 billion over a decade — far less than a HSR network would cost, and with much larger benefits to low-income commuters.
- **Population density**: HSR is most viable where high population densities along corridors generate demand. South Africa's geography — large distances between major cities with relatively small intermediate cities — is not ideal HSR territory.

**The correct rail priority** for South Africa is therefore not HSR but:
1. **Restoring the commuter rail network** (Metrorail) to reliable, frequent, affordable service in Johannesburg, Cape Town, Durban, and Port Elizabeth.
2. **Investing in bus rapid transit (BRT)** networks in medium-sized cities where rail is not viable.
3. **Rehabilitating Transnet Freight Rail** to reduce logistics costs for mining, agriculture, and manufacturing.
4. **Extending suburban rail reach** into peri-urban areas where commuting costs are highest.

If fiscal resources permit after these priorities, a **medium-speed intercity rail corridor** linking Johannesburg-Durban-Cape Town at 160–200km/h — achievable on upgraded but not fully dedicated new track — would offer meaningful travel time reductions at a fraction of HSR cost.

#### International Evidence on Rail and Development

The most rigorous economic evidence on rail and development comes from natural experiments:

- **David Donaldson (2018, AER)**: Using variation in the timing of railway construction in colonial India, he found that railroads reduced the incidence of famine, increased trade, and raised real incomes. The effects were large precisely because pre-rail transport costs were so high.
- **China's HSR experience**: A body of studies (including Zheng and Kahn, 2013; Ke et al., 2017) finds that HSR in China increased GDP growth in connected cities and shifted economic activity toward more tradeable, skill-intensive sectors. However, China's HSR was built at scale after a foundation of dense commuter rail already existed, and in a country with very high population density and large intercity travel demand.
- **South African commuter rail**: Venter (2013) and others at the University of Pretoria's built environment research unit have documented the welfare costs of commuter rail deterioration for low-income workers. The evidence strongly supports the view that rail restoration in the commuter context is highly welfare-positive.

### 3.4 Spatial Transformation: Undoing Apartheid Geography

The spatial legacy of apartheid is perhaps South Africa's most persistent and underappreciated development constraint. The Group Areas Act and related legislation systematically placed Black, Coloured, and Indian residential areas **far from economic centres** (city CBDs, industrial zones, commercial parks), connected by infrequent and unaffordable public transport.

Three decades after apartheid's legal end, this geography is largely intact:
- **Soweto** (1.3 million residents) is 25 km from Johannesburg's CBD.
- **Khayelitsha** (700,000+ residents) is 35 km from Cape Town's CBD.
- **Umlazi** (600,000+ residents) is 16 km from Durban's CBD.

The economic cost of this spatial mismatch is enormous. Using data from South Africa's National Income Dynamics Study, researchers at UCT and Stellenbosch have estimated that **high commuting costs lower effective wages for township residents by 15–25%** — a structural tax on labour participation that also reduces firms' willingness to hire low-wage workers (since the minimum wage must cover transport costs).

**What spatial policy can do**:
1. **Inclusionary housing near economic nodes**: Requiring or incentivising developers of commercial precincts to include social housing components, reducing average commuting distances.
2. **Upgrading informal settlements in situ** rather than relocation: Relocating residents to peripheral RDP housing (as has been South Africa's dominant model) deepens spatial exclusion. Instead, investing in in-situ upgrading of informal settlements with water, sanitation, electricity, and roads preserves proximity to economic opportunity.
3. **Mixed-use zoning reform**: South Africa's cities are heavily single-use zoned, which mandates separation of residential, commercial, and industrial land uses. Reforming zoning codes to allow mixed use (retail, small workshop, residential in the same building) enables informal economic activity near homes and reduces commuting demand.
4. **Land value capture**: The increase in land values near new transport infrastructure can be partly captured by government through development levies and used to cross-subsidise affordable housing near stations.

### 3.5 Industrial Policy and Structural Transformation

South Africa's unemployment rate — approximately **32–33% on the official definition** and **42–43% on the expanded definition** (which includes discouraged workers who have stopped seeking employment) — is structurally anomalous. Most comparable middle-income countries have unemployment rates of 5–15%. The source of this anomaly lies in a prolonged process of **de-industrialisation** since the mid-1990s: manufacturing's share of GDP fell from around 21% in 1994 to approximately 13% by 2024. Meanwhile, the labour force grew, creating a massive structural gap between labour supply and formal employment demand.

No education intervention alone can resolve this. Even if South Africa's basic education system were dramatically improved tomorrow, there would still be millions of adults with limited formal skills who need employment pathways *now*. This points to the importance of **demand-side labour market interventions** — creating jobs — alongside supply-side human capital investment.

#### Labour-Intensive Manufacturing

South Africa's labour cost structure has historically been uncompetitive for labour-intensive manufacturing relative to countries like Bangladesh, Vietnam, or Ethiopia. However, recent shifts — rising wages in China and East Asia, reshoring pressures, and South Africa's preferential market access under the African Growth and Opportunity Act (AGOA) and African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA) — have improved relative conditions for certain sectors:

- **Agro-processing**: South Africa's comparative advantage in agriculture (deciduous fruit, wine, horticulture, meat) creates natural agro-processing opportunities that are labour-intensive and geographically dispersed.
- **Automotive and components**: South Africa's existing automotive industrial policy (the Automotive Production and Development Programme, APDP) has successfully maintained a significant vehicle assembly and components industry. Expanding this — particularly for electric vehicle components — could be a strategic priority.
- **Green manufacturing**: South Africa's planned energy transition away from coal creates opportunities in renewable energy manufacturing (solar panels, wind turbine components) and the green hydrogen value chain. The Just Energy Transition Partnership (JETP) with G7 countries offers financing for this.
- **Light manufacturing for African markets**: Under AfCFTA, South Africa has preferential access to a market of 1.3 billion people. This creates opportunities for goods that South African manufacturers can produce at scale for the continent.

#### Services and the Digital Economy

Services sectors that can create formal employment for lower-skill workers include **business process outsourcing (BPO)** and contact centre industries. South Africa's BPO industry employs approximately 250,000 workers and has been one of the faster-growing formal employment sectors over the past decade, though it remains vulnerable to automation risk.

**Tourism** employs roughly 1.5 million people directly and indirectly and remains a major opportunity, though it requires safety and infrastructure improvements to realise its potential.

#### The Role of Special Economic Zones

South Africa has used Special Economic Zones (SEZs) — Dube TradePort near Durban, Coega near Port Elizabeth, and others — with mixed results. SEZs can work when they reduce regulatory friction, provide reliable utilities, and offer proximity to logistics infrastructure. South Africa's zones have suffered from unreliable electricity (load-shedding) and port congestion, severely undermining their attractiveness. Resolving these infrastructure failures is a prerequisite for SEZ-driven investment attraction.

### 3.6 Social Protection as Both Safety Net and Growth Engine

South Africa's social grants system is one of the largest in the developing world relative to population. Approximately **18.6 million people** received a social grant as of 2024, including:
- **Child Support Grant** (CSG): R500/month per child, for 13 million+ children
- **Old Age Pension**: R2,190/month
- **Disability Grant**: R2,190/month
- **Social Relief of Distress** (SRD, the post-COVID basic income support): R370/month for approximately 9 million adults

The CSG in particular has well-documented positive effects. Research by Leibbrandt, Woolard, and colleagues at UCT, and by Agüero, Carter, and May, has found that receipt of the CSG is associated with better nutrition outcomes, higher school attendance rates, and higher adult earnings for children who received it during early childhood. This is exactly the human capital investment mechanism that theory predicts.

**The case for a Basic Income Grant (BIG)** for working-age adults has grown significantly since the COVID-19 pandemic revealed the extreme vulnerability of the large pool of adults not covered by the SRD or other grants. The Presidency's Economic Advisory Council and independent economists including Ingrid Woolard and Gilad Isaacs at the Institute for Economic Justice have modelled different BIG options. A grant of R500–R700 per month for adults aged 18–59 not receiving other social grants would cost an estimated **R50–100 billion per year** depending on design — roughly 1.5–3% of GDP. The financing question is unresolved, but options include:
- Reallocation from inefficient spending (bloated government wage bill, under-performing state enterprises)
- Moderate tax increases, particularly on personal income tax at high brackets
- A small VAT increase, ideally with a larger zero-rated basket to protect the poor
- Revenue from a progressive wealth tax

Importantly, the economic multiplier from a BIG in the South African context is likely positive: most grant income is spent locally on food, transport, and household goods, supporting local economic activity. The Olivier de Schutter (UN Special Rapporteur on Extreme Poverty) report on South Africa (2024) explicitly endorsed the BIG as consistent with South Africa's human rights obligations and economic logic.

### 3.7 Comparative Cases: What Can South Africa Learn from Brazil, South Korea, and China?

#### Brazil: Bolsa Família and Its Lessons

Brazil's Bolsa Família (now Bolsa Família 2.0, rebranded Auxílio Brasil under Bolsonaro and returned to its original name and design under Lula) is the world's most studied conditional cash transfer (CCT) programme. At its 2015 peak it covered **50 million people** (about 25% of Brazil's population), at a cost of approximately **0.5% of GDP**.

Extensive evaluation literature — including Soares et al. (2010), Paes de Barros et al. (2006), and Damon and Glewwe (2019) — documents that Bolsa Família:
- Reduced Brazil's Gini coefficient by an estimated 1.5–2 points during its most active phase
- Increased school enrolment and attendance among recipient children
- Improved child nutrition indicators
- Had no significant labour supply disincentive effects on adults (a common concern about cash transfers that has generally not been borne out)

South Africa's CSG and SRD can be understood as partial analogues, but Brazil's programme was more aggressively scaled and conditionalities were more consistently enforced. South Africa's challenge is the gap for working-age adults without income who don't have children qualifying them for the CSG — a population of several million who currently have no income support at all.

#### South Korea: The Developmental State Model

South Korea's transformation from a low-income, war-devastated country in 1960 to a high-income OECD member by the 1990s remains the most dramatic documented case of middle-income trap escape. The key features of the Korean model:

1. **State-directed credit allocation**: The government directed bank lending toward targeted industries (steel, petrochemicals, electronics, shipbuilding) through the Economic Planning Board and Korean Development Bank. This concentrated investment in sectors with scale economies and learning potential.
2. **Aggressive human capital investment**: Korea's schooling system expanded extremely rapidly in the 1960s and 1970s. By the 1980s, Korea had the highest tertiary education enrolment rate among developing countries. Crucially, Korea also built strong technical and vocational education streams, not just university pathways.
3. **Disciplined export orientation**: Korean firms receiving state support were required to demonstrate export performance, imposing a market discipline that prevented prolonged uncompetitive subsidy dependence.
4. **Land reform (1950s)**: Korea's post-war land reform — radical redistribution from landlords to tenants — created a more equal rural starting point, reducing the political power of a rent-seeking agrarian elite and freeing resources for industrial investment.

**What transfers to South Africa and what does not**: The South Korean model depended on a strong, competent Weberian state bureaucracy insulated from clientelist politics — a precondition South Africa currently lacks. The Zuma-era state capture demonstrated precisely what happens when the developmental state apparatus is colonised by rent-seeking networks. Korea's lesson for South Africa is less about the specific policy tools than about the **institutional prerequisites**: a professional public service, meritocratic appointment to key economic agencies, and effective accountability mechanisms.

#### China: Infrastructure and Human Capital at Scale

China's development trajectory offers several specific lessons for South Africa:

1. **Infrastructure investment as a growth driver**: China's massive investment in infrastructure — including 40,000 km of high-speed rail — preceded and accompanied its sustained growth. But China's rail investment was built on a foundation of existing dense conventional rail, and it served an economy of 1.4 billion people with enormous intercity travel demand. The lesson for South Africa is not to replicate HSR but to understand that **infrastructure investment must match the demand structure of the economy**.

2. **Technical and vocational education**: China massively expanded its technical college and vocational training system in the 2000s, training millions of technicians and skilled workers for manufacturing industries. South Africa's TVET (Technical and Vocational Education and Training) college sector, which enrols approximately 700,000 students, is widely regarded as producing graduates whose skills are mismatched with employer needs. Overhauling the TVET system to produce work-ready graduates — possibly through expanded partnerships with industry — is a priority.

3. **Special Economic Zones**: China's SEZ model, pioneered in Shenzhen and expanded nationally, created enclaves of preferential market conditions that attracted foreign direct investment and enabled technology and management transfer. South Africa's SEZ experience has been less successful, primarily due to unreliable electricity and poor port logistics — solvable infrastructure problems, not fundamental structural failures.

#### Malaysia: Managing Multi-Ethnic Inequality

Malaysia offers perhaps the most directly relevant comparative case for South Africa: a formerly British colony with substantial ethnic inequality, which implemented in 1971 the **New Economic Policy (NEP)** — an aggressive affirmative action programme to increase Bumiputera (indigenous Malay) ownership, employment, and education. Under the NEP:
- Corporate ownership targets were set (30% Bumiputera ownership of listed companies)
- University admissions favoured Bumiputera students
- Government contracts included racial participation requirements

The results were genuinely mixed. Malaysia's overall growth (averaging 6–8% per annum through the 1980s and 1990s) reduced poverty dramatically and grew the Bumiputera middle class. However, the NEP also:
- Created rent-seeking incentives and "Ali Baba" proxy structures (where Bumiputera fronts enabled non-Bumiputera businesses to access contracts)
- Failed to significantly close the skills gap because quotas got people into universities but didn't ensure they graduated with competitive skills
- Generated Chinese and Indian Malaysian emigration of highly skilled workers

South Africa's Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment (B-BBEE) policy has some structural parallels to Malaysia's NEP, including similar critiques: it has enabled the growth of a Black corporate and professional class while being largely inaccessible to the rural and informal-sector poor. Like Malaysia, South Africa's affirmative action framework has focused more on ownership transfer (share transactions benefiting the well-connected) than on fundamentally restructuring access to education, land, and economic opportunity.

### 3.8 Sequencing and Political Economy: The Order of Interventions

A consistent challenge in development economics is the question of **sequencing**: which reforms must come first? South Africa faces several sequencing dilemmas:

**Education before employment, or employment before education?** Education improvements take decades to feed through to the labour market. Meanwhile, unemployed adults need income now. The answer cannot be "fix education first, then create jobs." Both must happen simultaneously, with different tools: foundational education reform for the long term; industrial policy, public employment, and social grants for the near term.

**Infrastructure before investment, or investment before infrastructure?** South Africa's infrastructure deficit — load-shedding, port congestion, rail deterioration — has been the single largest deterrent to private investment over the past decade. But infrastructure investment requires fiscal space, which requires growth, which requires investment. Breaking this circularity requires the state to take on fiscal risk to resolve infrastructure failures — the logic behind the Presidential Infrastructure Coordinating Commission's programmes.

**Institutional reform before development policy, or alongside it?** South Korea's lesson appears to require institutional quality first. But waiting for institutional reform before doing anything else is not viable either. The practical answer is that South Africa must pursue **pockets of effectiveness** — well-governed agencies and programmes that can deliver results even in an imperfect overall governance environment — while simultaneously pursuing broader institutional reform. The South African Revenue Service under Ivan Pillay and Tom Moyane's contrasting tenures illustrates both possibilities: a high-performing institution can be built and can also be rapidly destroyed.

**The political economy constraint** is perhaps the most fundamental. South Africa's largest formal sector trade union federation (COSATU) and the South African Communist Party (SACP) are in a tripartite alliance with the ANC and have historically blocked labour market reforms that might expand employment in the formal sector (such as sectoral minimum wages differentiated by geographic zone, or changes to unfair dismissal provisions that raise the cost of hiring workers). Simultaneously, large business interests have historically resisted land reform, wealth taxes, and empowerment requirements. Any realistic development strategy must navigate these political forces — which is partly why South Africa has remained in a reform stasis despite broad consensus on what needs to happen.

## 5. Open Questions and Next Steps

### Unresolved Policy Questions

1. **How large should the Basic Income Grant be, and how should it be financed without crowding out productive investment?** The fiscal modelling is contested. A BIG large enough to be meaningful (above the food poverty line of ~R760/month in 2024 terms) would cost several percentage points of GDP. The revenue question — wealth tax, VAT, personal income tax, reallocation — has not been politically resolved.

2. **Can affirmative action policies be redesigned to be more economically inclusive without losing their core equity objectives?** South Africa's B-BBEE has primarily benefited the connected and credentialed Black elite rather than the rural and informal-sector poor. A class-based redesign (targeting the bottom income quintiles regardless of race, which in South Africa are overwhelmingly Black) has been proposed but faces significant political resistance within the ANC alliance.

3. **What is the optimal institutional vehicle for an industrial policy? Does South Africa have the state capacity to implement it effectively?** Industrial policy failures in South Africa (SAA, Eskom, Denel, PRASA) are all partly explained by poor governance of state institutions. Any new industrial policy must grapple with the patronage and capture dynamics that have disabled previous development institutions.

4. **Can Prasa be restored to functional service, and by whom?** The technical and institutional pathways to restoring commuter rail are reasonably well understood. What is less clear is the political economy: Prasa's workforce and procurement contracts represent patronage networks that resist reform. International concessioning models (as used in some Latin American commuter rail systems) offer a partial solution but require political willingness to cede control.

5. **What is the role of land reform in the development equation?** South Africa's constitutional negotiation in 1994 effectively protected existing property rights, and land reform since then has been slow and often poorly implemented. More fundamental redistribution — whether via an accelerated willing-seller-willing-buyer market with government financing, expropriation with fair compensation, or more radical land reform — remains one of the most contested policy questions. The economic evidence from land redistribution in other contexts (Zimbabwe's chaotic expropriation versus Kenya's more gradual land titling) suggests that process and implementation quality matter enormously.

6. **How can the TVET sector be overhauled to produce work-ready graduates?** The structural mismatch between what TVET colleges teach and what employers need is well-documented. Solutions — employer levy funds for training, dual-track apprenticeship programmes modelled on Germany's Berufsschule system, industry-college partnerships — are known. The implementation challenge is the quality of college management and faculty.

### Priority Next Steps for Researchers and Practitioners

1. **Commission a rigorous fiscal modelling study** of BIG financing options under different growth scenarios, explicitly accounting for multiplier effects of grant spending.

2. **Evaluate the Funda Wande and similar structured literacy programmes at scale** in at least two provinces (beyond initial Western Cape and Eastern Cape pilots) with randomised design, to generate evidence for national policy.

3. **Develop a costed Prasa recovery plan** with clear institutional accountability, benchmarks, and independent oversight mechanisms — analogous to the Eskom debt relief and operational turnaround plan of 2023.

4. **Conduct geospatial analysis of the labour market distance penalty**: mapping unemployed workers' location against formal employment nodes, and estimating the potential employment impact of different transport connectivity investments (commuter rail restoration vs. BRT extension vs. HSR).

5. **Pilot an SEZ or special regulatory zone** that genuinely resolves the infrastructure constraint (dedicated power supply, bonded logistics, streamlined permitting) to test whether manufacturing investment can be attracted when these barriers are addressed.

6. **Engage organised labour on alternative employment expansion models**: COSATU's resistance to labour market flexibility is partly a rational defence of members' interests. A social compact that offers union members meaningful participation in industrial policy design in exchange for flexibility provisions for new entrants could be a path forward, following examples from Nordic active labour market policy.
